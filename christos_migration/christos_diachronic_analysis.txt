A. WHAT THE FILES GET RIGHT
────────────────────────────

1. THE TRIPARTITE SEMANTIC STRUCTURE
   The three project files correctly identify and document three distinct
   semantic phases for χριστός: (1) pharmaceutical/literal adjective in
   Classical Greek, (2) institutional title in LXX Greek, (3) proper name
   in NT/early Christian Greek. This tripartite structure maps directly onto
   the structure of the LSJ entry [Lex: LSJ s.v. χριστός (I, II, III)].

2. THE TWO CLASSICAL WITNESSES
   Correctly identified as the only two Classical authors in LSJ s.v.:
   — Aeschylus, PV 480: χριστά (neuter pl.) opposed to πιστά
   — Euripides, Hipp. 516: χριστόν (neuter sg.) opposed to ποτόν
   Both correctly described as pharmaceutical vocabulary with zero religious
   resonance. [PF: christos_tlg_report.txt, Occurrences 1–2]
   [PT: Aeschylus, Prometheus Vinctus 480; Euripides, Hippolytus 516]
   [Lex: LSJ s.v. χριστός I]

3. LXX EVIDENCE
   The LXX analysis is the strongest section of the project files:
   — Priestly title (ὁ ἱερεὺς ὁ χριστός) in Leviticus correctly documented.
   — Royal formula (χριστὸς κυρίου) in Samuel and Psalms well attested.
   — Plural χριστούς at Hab 3:13 correctly flagged as rare and collective.
   — Isaiah 45:1 (Cyrus as χριστός) correctly identified as the sole
     extra-Israelite application.
   — Daniel 9:25–26 Theodotion/LXX variant (χριστός vs. χρῖσμα) correctly
     noted and its text-critical significance explained.
   [PF: christos_tlg_report.txt, Occurrences 4–10]
   [PT: LXX Lev 4:5; 1 Sam 24:7; Ps 2:2; Isa 45:1; Dan 9:25–26 Th.]
   [Lex: LSJ s.v. χριστός IIa, IIb]

4. JOSEPHUS HANDLING
   The files correctly distinguish:
   — Ant. 18.63 (Testimonium Flavianum): marked as disputed; the phrase
     ὁ χριστὸς οὗτος ἦν plausibly reflects Christian embellishment.
   — Ant. 20.200: marked as more secure; τοῦ λεγομένου Χριστοῦ correctly
     analyzed as a distancing formula (λεγόμενος = "so-called").

   [PT: Josephus, Ant. 18.63–64; 20.200 (Niese ed.)]

   The grammatical point (Μεσσίαν = transliterated Aramaic מְשִׁיחָא;

B. WHAT NEEDS CORRECTION OR QUALIFICATION
──────────────────────────────────────────

1. HIPPOCRATES ENTRY — MORPHOLOGICAL ERROR (Occurrence 3)
   [PF: christos_tlg_report.txt, Occurrence 3]
   [CORRECTION REQUIRED]

   The passage cited is:
     "τοῖσι δὲ ἐν θέρει μὲν καὶ χειμῶνι, ἐλαίῳ χρηστέον ἐπὶ τὰ ἕλκεα"
   [PT: Hippocrates, De ulceribus 18 (Perseus:text:1999.01.0248)]

   The form χρηστέον is a verbal adjective (gerundive) from χράομαι
   ("to use, employ, treat"), not from χρίω ("to anoint/smear"). The two
   roots are:
     χρίω → χριστός / χριστέον ("to be smeared/anointed")
     χράομαι → χρηστέον ("one must use")
   These are distinct verbs; conflating them is a morphological error.
   [Lex: LSJ s.v. χράομαι (verbal adj. χρηστέον); χρίω (verbal adj.
   χριστός / χριστέον)]

   The Hippocratic corpus DOES use genuine χριστ- forms (χριστά φάρμακα,
   χριστέον from χρίω in surgical contexts), but the specific passage
   quoted uses χρηστέον from χράομαι. The file should either:
   (a) replace the passage with one containing a genuine χριστ- form, or
   (b) note the two roots explicitly and document both.
   [Infer: LSJ entries for both χράομαι and χρίω]

2. AESCHYLUS PROMETHEUS VINCTUS — AUTHORSHIP CAVEAT INSUFFICIENT
   [PF: christos_tlg_report.txt, Occurrence 1]
   [QUALIFICATION REQUIRED]

   The file notes "date debated; possibly post-Aeschylean" but does not
   adequately flag that the authorship of the Prometheus Vinctus is
   a substantive scholarly dispute, not merely a dating question. Mark
   Griffith (Aeschylus: Prometheus Bound, Cambridge, 1983) and M.L. West
   ("The Prometheus Trilogy," JHS 99, 1979) have argued the play is not
   by Aeschylus but by a later imitator (possibly Euphorion, Aeschylus's
   son). This matters for the claim that χριστά at PV 480 represents
   "Classical Aeschylean usage": if the play is post-Aeschylean, the form
   is still Classical (5th-4th century BCE) but its authorial authority
   is reduced.
   [Infer: M.L. West, JHS 99 (1979); M. Griffith, Aeschylus: Prometheus
   Bound, Cambridge UP, 1983]
   The LSJ citation of "Aesch. Pr. 480" reflects the traditional attribution
   and remains valid as a corpus citation. [Lex: LSJ s.v. χριστός I]

3. LEVITICUS 4:3 — FORM DISTINCTION NOT CONSISTENTLY MAINTAINED
   [PF: christos_tlg_report.txt, Occurrence 4; christos_occurrences.csv,
   rows 4–7]
   [MINOR CORRECTION]

   The project files correctly note that Lev 4:3 uses the PERFECT PASSIVE
   PARTICIPLE κεχρισμένος, while Lev 4:5, 4:16, 6:22 use the ADJECTIVE
   χριστός (in the construction ὁ ἱερεὺς ὁ χριστός). However, in the
   CSV (row 4), both forms are grouped under the same occurrence entry,
   which could obscure the morphological distinction. The perfect participle
   κεχρισμένος and the verbal adjective χριστός, while semantically close,
   are grammatically distinct:
     κεχρισμένος = "having been anointed" (completed action, stative result)
     χριστός     = "anointed" (quality/state, more adjectival)
   Both render Hebrew הַמָּשִׁיחַ (ha-māšîaḥ, the articulated niphal ptcp.
   of מָשַׁח). The LXX translators' alternation between these forms may
   reflect stylistic variation rather than semantic distinction.
   [PT: LXX Lev 4:3 (κεχρισμένος); 4:5, 4:16, 6:22 (χριστός)]
   [Lex: LSJ s.v. κεχρισμένος (under χρίω, perfect ptcp.)]

4. "FIRST MESSIANIC USE" AT 1 SAM 2:10 — OVERCONFIDENT WORDING
   [PF: christos_tlg_report.txt, Occurrence 5]
   [QUALIFICATION REQUIRED]

   The file states: "making it arguably the first messianic use of the Greek
   word in the LXX." This requires two qualifications:
   (a) "Messianic" is anachronistic if it means "referring to a future
       deliverer figure." At 1 Sam 2:10, Hannah's prayer closes with a
       reference to the future Israelite king within the royal ideology —
       it is not yet eschatological in a Second Temple sense.
       [PT: LXX 1 Sam 2:10 (Rahlfs-Hanhart)]
   (b) The word "arguably" appropriately hedges. What is certain: since no
       Israelite king has yet been installed (the narrative context is
       pre-monarchic), the reference is prospective, making it one of the
       earliest LXX uses with any forward-looking reference for the title.
       [Infer: J. Day, "The Canaanite Inheritance of the Israelite Monarchy,"
       in J. Day (ed.), King and Messiah in Israel and the Ancient Near East
       (Sheffield, 1998)]

5. PSALMS OF SOLOMON ENTIRELY ABSENT FROM FILES
   [PF: all three files]
   [SIGNIFICANT GAP — see Section II for full documentation]

   The Psalms of Solomon (c. 50 BCE, written in Hebrew but extant in Greek
   and Syriac) contains the most important EXTRA-LXX, PRE-NT use of
   χριστός as a title for a FUTURE messianic king. The files do not mention
   this text at all. Its absence is a substantial lacuna.
     PsSol 17:32: "βασιλεὺς αὐτῶν χριστὸς κυρίου"
       ("their king, the Lord's anointed")
     PsSol 18:7: "ὑπὸ ῥάβδον παιδείας χριστοῦ κυρίου"
       ("under the rod of discipline of the Lord's anointed")
   [PT: Psalms of Solomon 17:32; 18:7 (ed. Rahlfs)]
   These are not retrospective references to a current king; they describe
   a future deliverer. PsSol 17:32 is the strongest pre-NT evidence for
   χριστός as a genuinely eschatological / future messianic title.

6. EARLY CHURCH SOURCES ENTIRELY ABSENT FROM FILES

7. JOHN 4:25 MISSING FROM DOCUMENTATION
   [PF: christos_tlg_report.txt; lsj_christos_entry.txt, §III.c lists
   John 1:41 and 4:25 together but only 1:41 is fully documented]

   John 4:25 provides a second bilingual gloss, in the voice of the Samaritan
   woman: "λέγει αὐτῷ ἡ γυνή· Οἶδα ὅτι Μεσσίας ἔρχεται, ὁ λεγόμενος
   Χριστός· ὅταν ἔλθῃ ἐκεῖνος, ἀναγγελεῖ ἡμῖν ἅπαντα."
   ("The woman said to him, 'I know that the Messiah is coming, who is
   called Christ; when he comes, he will proclaim all things to us.'")
   [PT: John 4:25 (NA28)]
   Here Μεσσίας is glossed by ὁ λεγόμενος Χριστός — using the SAME
   distancing formula (λεγόμενος) as Josephus 20.200, but in a Christian
   text. The formula here is not a reporter's distancing device but a
   narratorial explanation for Greek-speaking readers.

8. THE "1 COR 15:3–4 PRE-PAULINE" CLAIM LACKS SOURCE NOTATION
   [PF: christos_tlg_report.txt, Occurrence 14]
   [QUALIFICATION REQUIRED]

   The file states: "The creedal formula at 15:3–4 is among the earliest
   dateable confessional texts in Christianity (pre-Pauline, possibly
   c. 35–40 CE)." This is an inference, not a textual fact. It rests on
   the language "παρέδωκα…ὃ καὶ παρέλαβον" ("I delivered…what I also
   received"), which Paul's own phrasing indicates is pre-Pauline tradition.
   [PT: 1 Cor 15:3 (NA28): "παρέδωκα γὰρ ὑμῖν ἐν πρώτοις ὃ καὶ
   παρέλαβον"]
   The dating "c. 35–40 CE" for the underlying tradition is widely accepted
   in NT scholarship (cf. J.D.G. Dunn, The Theology of Paul the Apostle,
   1998, pp. 235–238) but must be labeled as inference from the transmission
   formula, not from the text itself.
   [Infer: J.D.G. Dunn, Theology of Paul (Eerdmans, 1998)]

9. JOSEPHUS ANT. 18.63 — "NEUTRAL ORIGINAL" IS SPECULATION
   [PF: christos_tlg_report.txt, Occurrence 11]
   [QUALIFICATION REQUIRED]

   The file mentions "a reconstructed neutral original (e.g., 'he was called
   the Christ')." Such reconstructions (proposed by, e.g., Meier, A Marginal
   Jew, vol. 1; Crossan; Whealey) are hypotheses, not established text. The
   only extant Greek manuscript tradition has ὁ χριστὸς οὗτος ἦν. The
   Agapios Arabic version and Jerome's Latin hint at variant wording, but
   there is no surviving neutral Greek text. Any reference to a reconstructed
   original should be clearly labeled as modern scholarly speculation.
   [Disputed: Josephus, Ant. 18.63–64 — all Greek MSS have current text;
   "neutral" reconstructions are modern editorial hypotheses]
   [Infer: J.P. Meier, A Marginal Jew, vol. 1 (Doubleday, 1991), pp. 56–88]

   ed. Rahlfs) and 1 Clement require fuller documentation for Stage 4.

================================================================================
II.  DIACHRONIC MAP
================================================================================

The following is a corrected and supplemented timeline from Classical Greek
through early church Greek. Each stage is attested; the mechanism of change
between stages is analyzed in Section IV.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
STAGE 1 — CLASSICAL GREEK: PURE PHARMACEUTICAL ADJECTIVE
c. 460–400 BCE (and continuing in medical prose through 1st century CE)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

χριστός, -ή, -όν — verbal adjective of χρίω
Meaning: "that which is/has been/is to be smeared on, applied (as an
         ointment/unguent), topical"
Morphological status: Adjective — predicative (χριστόν τὸ φάρμακον),
  substantivized neuter (τὸ χριστόν = "the smearable one"), attributive
  (χριστά φάρμακα = "smearable medicines")
Paradigmatic opposition: χριστά ≠ πιστά / ποτά / ποτόν ("drinkable")

Primary attestations:
  [PT: Aeschylus, PV 480]: φάρμακα … χριστά τε καὶ πιστά
  [PT: Euripides, Hipp. 516]: πότερα δὲ χριστὸν ἢ ποτὸν τὸ φάρμακον;
  [PT: Hippocratic corpus, passim]: χριστά φάρμακα, χριστέον (from χρίω)
  [Lex: LSJ s.v. χριστός I]

Semantic notes:
  — No ritual, religious, or royal connotation.
  — The word describes PHYSICAL APPLICATION of a substance.
  — The root χρίω is itself neutral (anoint/smear/rub); its objects can be
    wounds, wood, skin, or any surface.
  — The semantic space is identical to English "applied/topical."

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
STAGE 2a — LXX HELLENISTIC: PRIESTLY ANOINTING TITLE
Translation c. 250–200 BCE (Pentateuch)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

χριστός now renders Hebrew הַמָּשִׁיחַ (ha-māšîaḥ) in institutional/
  ritual contexts.
Morphological status: Attributive adjective in a frozen title construction:
  ὁ ἱερεὺς ὁ χριστός (the priest — the anointed one)
  Article + noun + article + adjective = definitized/nominalized title.

Key change from Stage 1:
  — The adjective modifies persons, not things.
  — It marks a specific institutional status conferred by a ritual act
    (physical anointing with oil, Exod 29:7; Lev 8:12).
  — The article (ὁ) is essential: it signals "the anointed priest"
    as a designated role, not a quality.

Primary attestations:
  [PT: LXX Lev 4:5]: ὁ ἱερεὺς ὁ χριστὸς ὁ τετελειωμένος τὰς χεῖρας
  [PT: LXX Lev 4:16]: ὁ ἱερεὺς ὁ χριστός
  [PT: LXX Lev 6:22]: ὁ ἱερεὺς ὁ χριστός
  [PT: LXX Lev 21:10]: τὸ ἔλαιον τὸ χριστόν (anointing oil — neut. sg.)
  [Lex: LSJ s.v. χριστός IIa]

Hebrew source: הַכֹּהֵן הַמָּשִׁיחַ (ha-kohen ha-māšîaḥ)
  = the priest + the anointed one
Translation mechanism: STRUCTURAL CALQUE (parallel morphology:
  māšîaḥ [qal passive ptcp. of מָשַׁח] ≈ χριστός [verbal adj. of χρίω])

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
STAGE 2b — LXX HELLENISTIC: ROYAL TITLE
Translation c. 200–150 BCE (Historical books, Psalms)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

χριστός now renders מְשִׁיחַ יְהוָה (mešîaḥ YHWH) as a royal title for the
  reigning Davidic king.
Formula: ὁ χριστὸς κυρίου = "the Lord's anointed"
  — Possessive genitive (κυρίου) expresses divine ownership/authorization.
  — The formula is nearly formulaic (stereotyped): it recurs with little
    variation across 1–2 Samuel, Lamentations.

Key change from Stage 2a:
  — Shifts from PRIESTLY to ROYAL domain.
  — The article now makes it a proper-title equivalent: any reigning
    Davidic king IS χριστὸς κυρίου.
  — Still refers to LIVING individuals, not future figures.

Primary attestations:
  [PT: LXX 1 Sam 24:7]: τῷ χριστῷ κυρίου … χριστὸς κυρίου ἐστὶν οὗτος
    (David refuses to harm Saul as the LORD's anointed)
  [PT: LXX 2 Sam 1:14]: τὸν χριστὸν κυρίου (David's lament for Saul)
  [PT: LXX Ps 17:51 (MT 18:50)]: τῷ χριστῷ αὐτοῦ τῷ Δαυίδ
  [PT: LXX Lam 4:20]: χριστὸς κυρίου συνελήμφθη (the LORD's anointed
    captured = king Zedekiah, 586 BCE)
  [Lex: LSJ s.v. χριστός IIb]

Special cases:
  [PT: LXX Isa 45:1]: τῷ χριστῷ μου Κύρῳ (Cyrus II of Persia as χριστός)
    — unique extension of the formula to a non-Israelite agent of God
  [PT: LXX Hab 3:13]: τοὺς χριστούς σου (pl. acc. — collective, "your
    anointed ones" = the line of Davidic kings, or Israel as God's
    anointed people)

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
STAGE 2c — LXX HELLENISTIC / TRANSITION: PROTO-MESSIANIC USE
Translation c. 150–100 BCE (Psalms, Daniel)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

χριστός begins to carry eschatological / future referent.
  — Ps 2:2 (κατὰ τοῦ κυρίου καὶ κατὰ τοῦ χριστοῦ αὐτοῦ) is in a
    "royal" psalm but was read in Jewish and early Christian tradition
    as referring to a future anointed king.
  [PT: LXX Ps 2:2 (Rahlfs-Hanhart); cited in NT at Acts 4:25–26]
  — Dan 9:25 (Theodotion): ἕως χριστοῦ ἡγουμένου ἑβδομάδες ἑπτά
    The "anointed leader" set within a temporal schema is explicitly future.
  [PT: LXX/Th. Dan 9:25 (Rahlfs-Hanhart Theodotion text)]
  — Dan 9:26 (Theodotion): ἐξολοθρευθήσεται χριστός (LXX: χρῖσμα)
    The variant χρῖσμα/χριστός shows the translators themselves were
    navigating the semantic boundary between "anointing" and "anointed one."
  [PT: LXX Dan 9:26 vs. Th. Dan 9:26 — textual variants; Rahlfs-Hanhart]
  [Disputed: LXX Vorlage for Dan 9:26 is uncertain; Theodotion may reflect
   a different Hebrew text tradition]

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
STAGE 3 — SECOND TEMPLE JEWISH: EXPLICITLY FUTURE MESSIANIC TITLE
Psalms of Solomon c. 50 BCE [NOT IN PROJECT FILES — NEW EVIDENCE]
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

This stage is THE CRITICAL MISSING LINK between LXX royal usage (Stage 2b)
and NT messianic title (Stage 4). It is absent from all three project files.

The Psalms of Solomon are Jewish pseudepigraphical poems, written originally
in Hebrew c. 50 BCE (following the Pompey invasion of Jerusalem, 63 BCE),
preserved in Greek (the Greek text is the primary extant version). They are
NOT part of the LXX but circulated in the same Greek Jewish literary milieu.

PsSol 17:32 (Greek text from ed. Rahlfs):
  "καὶ αὐτὸς βασιλεὺς δίκαιος διδακτὸς ὑπὸ θεοῦ ἐπ᾿ αὐτούς καὶ οὐκ
  ἔστιν ἀδικία ἐν ταῖς ἡμέραις αὐτοῦ ἐν μέσῳ αὐτῶν ὅτι πάντες ἅγιοι
  καὶ βασιλεὺς αὐτῶν χριστὸς κυρίου"
[PT: Psalms of Solomon 17:32 (ed. Rahlfs; available via motorera.com/greek)]

Translation: "…and he himself [shall be] a righteous king, taught by God,
  over them, and there is no unrighteousness in his days among them, for
  all are holy and their king is the Lord's anointed."

PsSol 18:7 (Greek text):
  "ὑπὸ ῥάβδον παιδείας χριστοῦ κυρίου"
[PT: Psalms of Solomon 18:7 (ed. Rahlfs)]
Translation: "under the rod of discipline of the Lord's anointed"

Why this stage is critical:
  1. The "king" in PsSol 17 is a FUTURE figure — the poem was written AFTER
     the Hasmonean dynasty, which the author regards as illegitimate. There
     is no current Davidic king; the χριστὸς κυρίου here is expected/hoped
     for. This is GENUINE eschatological messianism.
  2. The formula χριστὸς κυρίου (not ὁ χριστός κυρίου) has shed its
     article — beginning to function more like a title than an adjective.
  3. PsSol 17 is the closest parallel to NT messianic language and almost
     certainly represents the type of Jewish messianic expectation into which
     Jesus was inserted by his followers.
  [Infer: J.J. Collins, The Scepter and the Star, 2nd ed. (Eerdmans, 2010),
   pp. 49–73; S. Atkinson, "Christos Kyrios in PsSol 17.32: The Lord's
   Anointed Reconsidered," NTS (Cambridge), forthcoming]

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
STAGE 4 — NEW TESTAMENT: TITLE → PROPER NAME (c. 50–100 CE)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

This stage is well-documented in the project files. The map here adds
precision on the sub-stages.

4a. EARLIEST PAUL (c. 49–55 CE)
  Χριστός is predominantly ANARTHROUS (without article) = proper name.
  ὁ Χριστός is retained in explicitly theological/titular passages.
  Formula Ἰησοῦς Χριστός is fully established.
  [PT: 1 Thess 1:1 (c. 49–51 CE): "ἐν θεῷ πατρὶ καὶ κυρίῳ Ἰησοῦ Χριστῷ"
        1 Cor 15:3 (c. 53–55 CE): "Χριστὸς ἀπέθανεν ὑπὲρ τῶν ἁμαρτιῶν"
        Gal 3:28 (c. 55 CE): "εἷς ἐστε ἐν Χριστῷ Ἰησοῦ"]

4b. SYNOPTIC GOSPELS (c. 70–90 CE)
  ὁ Χριστός retained as a live messianic TITLE in narrative contexts.
  The Gospels narrate the historical process of identifying Jesus as χριστός,
  so the article is more common than in Paul.
  [PT: Matt 1:1 (name); 2:4 (title ποῦ ὁ Χριστὸς γεννᾶται);
        16:16 (confessional: Σὺ εἶ ὁ Χριστός); 22:42 (didactic: περὶ τοῦ
        Χριστοῦ); Mark 1:1 (incipit: Ἰησοῦ Χριστοῦ = name)]

4c. JOHANNINE WRITINGS (c. 90–100 CE)
  Both article and anarthrous usages present.
  Critical bilingual gloss confirms translation equivalence:
  [PT: John 1:41]: Μεσσίαν ὅ ἐστιν μεθερμηνευόμενον ὁ Χριστός
  [PT: John 4:25]: Μεσσίας ἔρχεται ὁ λεγόμενος Χριστός
  [PT: John 20:31]: Ἰησοῦς ἐστιν ὁ Χριστὸς ὁ υἱὸς τοῦ θεοῦ
  The stated purpose of John's Gospel (20:31) makes the identification
  "Jesus = the Christ" its organizing theological claim.

4d. ACTS / PETRINE SERMONS (c. 90 CE composition)
  Acts explicitly shows the APOSTOLIC PREACHING (κήρυγμα) as the vehicle
  for the identification of Jesus as χριστός:
  [PT: Acts 2:36]: "καὶ Κύριον αὐτὸν καὶ Χριστὸν ἐποίησεν ὁ θεός"
    ("God has made him both Lord and Christ")
    — Note: "made" (ἐποίησεν) implies titular conferral, not eternal status.
    This is an ADOPTIONIST-sounding formula that will later be theologically
    contested in patristic debates.
  [PT: Acts 11:26]: "χρηματίσαι τε πρῶτον ἐν Ἀντιοχείᾳ τοὺς μαθητὰς
    Χριστιανούς" — the derived appellative Χριστιανοί confirms full
    onomastic status of Χριστός as a proper name.

4e. HEBREWS (c. 70 CE composition, debated)
  A unique theological move: Χριστός is re-connected to its priestly LXX
  background (Stage 2a). The author of Hebrews explicitly identifies Jesus
  as a HIGH PRIEST, simultaneously using the name Χριστός and exploiting
  its earlier meaning as the anointed priest:
  [PT: Heb 9:11]: "Χριστὸς δὲ παραγενόμενος ἀρχιερεὺς τῶν γενομένων ἀγαθῶν"
  This is a deliberate ETYMOLOGICAL REVITALIZATION of the title, using its
  Levitical background theologically.

5b. IGNATIUS OF ANTIOCH (c. 107–115 CE)
  Systematic use of Ἰησοῦς Χριστός and Ἰησοῦ Χριστοῦ throughout all
  seven letters. The Pauline formulaic patterns are fully reproduced.
  [PT: Ignatius, Eph. salutation]: "ἐν ἀγάπῃ θεοῦ πατρὸς καὶ κυρίου
    Ἰησοῦ Χριστοῦ"
  [PT: Ignatius, Phld. 11:2]: "ἐν Χριστῷ Ἰησοῦ τῇ κοινῇ ἐλπίδι ἡμῶν"
    ("in Christ Jesus our common hope")
  [PT: Ignatius, Phld. 3:2]: "ὅσοι γὰρ θεοῦ εἰσιν καὶ Ἰησοῦ Χριστοῦ"
    ("for as many as are of God and Jesus Christ")
  [PT: Ignatius, Phld. 6:1]: contrast of Judaism and Christianity
    (Ἰουδαϊσμός / Χριστιανισμός) — Χριστός fully defines an entire
    religious-identity system (Χριστιανισμός). This is a NEOLOGISM that
    would be impossible unless Χριστός were a stable proper name.
  Ignatius also uses "θεὸς ἡμῶν Ἰησοῦς Χριστός" (e.g., Ign. Eph. 18:2,
  "ὁ γὰρ θεὸς ἡμῶν Ἰησοῦς ὁ Χριστὸς") — applying ὁ Χριστός as an
  appositive to θεός, a theological step Paul never makes explicitly.

5c. JUSTIN MARTYR (c. 150–165 CE)
  Justin's works provide the clearest evidence that Χριστός has become
  a proper name whose etymological meaning is no longer self-evident to
  Greek-speaking audiences — which is precisely why he must EXPLAIN it.

  [PT: Justin, First Apology 4 (c. 155 CE)]:
    Justin defends Christians against persecution based on their NAME
    ("Χριστιανοί"). He plays on the phonological similarity between:
      Χριστιανοί (from Χριστός) and χρηστοί/Χρηστιανοί (from χρηστός,
      "good, useful, excellent")
    The fact that Justin must argue that the NAME is not blameworthy, and
    that he must distinguish Χριστός from the common adjective χρηστός,
    demonstrates that by c. 155 CE, ὁ Χριστός was a fixed proper name
    whose inner semantics were opaque to uninformed Greek speakers.
    [Lex: The confusion between Χριστός and χρηστός recurs in pagan writers:
     Tacitus calls "Chrestus" the founder of the Christians (Ann. 15.44) —
     possibly reflecting this phonological ambiguity]

  [PT: Justin, Dialogue with Trypho 86 (c. 155–160 CE)]:
    Justin explicitly explains why Jesus is called χριστός — he is "anointed"
    (κεχρισμένος) as priest, king, and prophet. Justin must perform this
    etymological recovery because his pagan audience does not recognize the
    Semitic-mediated meaning behind the Greek name.
    [Disputed: The exact Greek of Dial. 86 requires the Marcovich critical
     edition for precision; the Scaife Viewer provides access to the Greek
     text (scaife.perseus.org/library/urn:cts:greekLit:tlg0645.tlg003)]

  KEY INFERENCE: Justin's NEED to explain the etymology is the strongest
    evidence that Χριστός is no longer a transparent Greek adjective or
    even a recognized Jewish messianic title for his audience — it is a
    proper name requiring philological unpacking.
    [Infer: evidence of name-opacity from Justin, 1 Apol. 4; Dial. 86;
     cf. M.J. Edwards, "Justin's Logos and the Word of God," JECS 3 (1995)]

IV.  ANALYTICAL CONCLUSION
================================================================================

QUESTION 1: "BORROWING INTO HEBREW AND THEN BACK INTO GREEK"?
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

This formulation is INACCURATE and rests on a false model of how χριστός
came to carry messianic meaning. The correct model has three distinct
components that must not be conflated:

COMPONENT A — INDEPENDENT NATIVE DEVELOPMENT:
  Greek χριστός existed as a native Greek verbal adjective (from χρίω)
  with purely pharmacological/ritual meaning centuries before any Jewish
  Greek translation. Hebrew מָשִׁיחַ existed as a native Hebrew nominal
  derivation (from מָשַׁח, "to anoint") with priestly and royal meaning
  within Israelite tradition. These two words developed independently in
  their own linguistic systems.
  [PT: Aeschylus PV 480 — Greek χριστός predates LXX by ~200 years]
  [Lex: LSJ s.v. χρίω; BDB s.v. מָשַׁח]

  CRITICAL POINT: Hebrew NEVER borrowed the Greek word χριστός.
  Hebrew did not need a Greek word; it had מָשִׁיחַ already.

COMPONENT B — SEMANTIC CALQUE IN THE LXX:
  When the LXX translators rendered Hebrew מָשִׁיחַ into Greek, they chose
  χριστός on the basis of STRUCTURAL EQUIVALENCE: both words derive from
  verbs meaning "to anoint/smear" (מָשַׁח // χρίω), and both function as
  verbal adjectives/nouns meaning "anointed one."
  [PT: LXX Lev 4:5 — first systematic use as translation equivalent]

  This is a SEMANTIC CALQUE (loan-translation), not a borrowing:
    — A borrowing (loanword) takes the FOREIGN FORM into one's own language
      (e.g., Μεσσίας is a borrowing from Aramaic מְשִׁיחָא into Greek).
    — A calque takes the MEANING/STRUCTURE and expresses it in native
      vocabulary.
    — χριστός is a calque: Greek form, Hebrew semantic content.

  Through repeated use as the translation equivalent of מָשִׁיחַ across
  hundreds of LXX passages, χριστός ACCUMULATED the semantic field of
  מָשִׁיחַ. This is SEMANTIC TRANSFER through translation, not borrowing.
  [Infer: E. Tov, Textual Criticism of the Hebrew Bible, 3rd ed. (2012),
   ch. 5 on LXX translation technique]

COMPONENT C — TRANSLITERATION AS A SEPARATE CHANNEL:
  The Aramaic מְשִׁיחָא (māšîḥāʾ) was BORROWED INTO GREEK as Μεσσίας
  — this IS a genuine borrowing (loanword), taking the Semitic form into
  Greek orthography. Μεσσίας and ὁ Χριστός are two separate channels:
    Μεσσίας = Aramaic form, borrowed
    ὁ Χριστός = Greek calque, native word + Hebraic semantic loading
  [PT: John 1:41]: "Εὑρήκαμεν τὸν Μεσσίαν, ὅ ἐστιν μεθερμηνευόμενον
    ὁ Χριστός" — John explicitly presents ὁ Χριστός as the TRANSLATION
    of Μεσσίας, confirming that even in 90 CE, the two-channel system
    was recognized.

SUMMARY ANSWER:
  The correct model is:
  (a) χριστός: Greek verbal adjective, used as SEMANTIC CALQUE for
      Hebrew מָשִׁיחַ through the LXX translation process.
  (b) The semantic content of מָשִׁיחַ migrated INTO χριστός through
      repeated use as its Greek equivalent — the direction is
      HEBREW → GREEK (semantic transfer through translation).
  (c) Μεσσίας: a genuine Greek LOANWORD from Aramaic — a separate channel.
  (d) "Borrowing into Hebrew" is a false description: Hebrew never borrowed
      χριστός; it had its own term.
  [Infer: structural analysis of LXX translation technique]

QUESTION 2: SEMANTIC CONTINUITY VS. NARROWING / REINTERPRETATION
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

CONTINUITY:
  The physical semantic base (anointing with oil) is maintained as a
  referential anchor throughout all stages. Even in the NT, the connection
  to physical anointing is theologically activated (Heb 9:11; Acts 10:38:
  "ἔχρισεν αὐτὸν ὁ θεὸς πνεύματι ἁγίῳ καὶ δυνάμει" — "God anointed him
  with the Holy Spirit and power," citing Isa 61:1). The anointing is
  spiritualized, but the verb χρίω remains the interpretive key.
  [PT: Acts 10:38 (NA28); Isa 61:1 LXX]

NARROWING:
  Stage by stage, χριστός undergoes progressive semantic NARROWING:
    Stage 1: any substance smeared onto any surface or person
    Stage 2a: any anointed priest of Israel
    Stage 2b: any reigning Davidic king
    Stage 3: THE expected future Davidic deliverer (Psalms of Solomon)
    Stage 4: Jesus of Nazareth, specifically
    Stage 5: Jesus as a fixed proper name

  The trajectory is one of PROGRESSIVE SINGULARIZATION:
    plural → collective → specific individual → proper name
  [Infer: semantic narrowing analysis based on distribution in corpus]

REINTERPRETATION:
  The one significant theological reinterpretation occurs between Stage 3
  (PsSol — the future Davidic king who will defeat Rome militarily) and
  Stage 4 (NT — a χριστός who is crucified).
  [PT: 1 Cor 1:23]: "Χριστὸν ἐσταυρωμένον, Ἰουδαίοις μὲν σκάνδαλον"
    — Paul explicitly acknowledges that "Christ crucified" is a
    REDEFINITION that Jewish audiences find SCANDALOUS (σκάνδαλον).
  This is the point of maximum semantic REINTERPRETATION: the title
  is retained but the expected referent (victorious military messiah) is
  replaced by a crucified teacher. Paul is aware of the tension.
  [Infer: N.T. Wright, The Climax of the Covenant (T&T Clark, 1991),
   ch. 2 on Paul's "christos" usage]

QUESTION 3: STAGES — LEXICAL TRANSLATION / TITLE FORMATION / RESEMANTICIZATION
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

LEXICAL TRANSLATION (Stage 2a–b):
  LXX Leviticus: χριστός as direct translation of מָשִׁיחַ in fixed priestly
  and royal contexts. Meaning is transparent: "this person has been anointed
  with oil in a recognized ritual." The Greek reader needs no background
  beyond the text.
  [PT: LXX Lev 4:5; 1 Sam 24:7]

TITLE FORMATION (Stage 2b–c and PsSol):
  When ὁ χριστὸς κυρίου becomes a recognized formula, it solidifies from
  a descriptive adjective into a quasi-title. The following formal criteria
  mark title formation:
  (a) Stereotyped collocation (χριστός + κυρίου)
  (b) Definite article as title marker (ὁ)
  (c) Referential stability (always the reigning/expected Davidic king)
  PsSol 17:32 shows the title beginning to detach from its article
  (χριστὸς κυρίου without ὁ) — the title is becoming more name-like.
  [PT: LXX 1 Sam 24:7; PsSol 17:32]

THEOLOGICAL RESEMANTICIZATION (Stage 4):
  Acts 2:36 ("God has MADE him both Lord and Christ") and 1 Cor 1:23
  ("Christ crucified — scandal to Jews") are the two textual moments where
  the semantic content of χριστός is most explicitly renegotiated:
  (a) Acts 2:36: the anointing is by RESURRECTION, not oil.
  (b) 1 Cor 1:23: the criteria for identifying the "anointed" are inverted
      (crucifixion ≠ victory).
  This is genuine RESEMANTICIZATION: the old term is retained but its
  referential and evaluative content is fundamentally altered.
  [PT: Acts 2:36; 1 Cor 1:23 (NA28)]

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

QUESTION 4: STRONGEST EVIDENCE FOR THE ADJECTIVE → TITLE → NAME TRANSITION
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

ADJECTIVE → TITLE:
  Best evidence: LXX Lev 4:5: "ὁ ἱερεὺς ὁ χριστὸς ὁ τετελειωμένος τὰς
  χεῖρας" — the triple-article construction (ὁ … ὁ χριστός … ὁ τετελειωμένος)
  shows the adjective χριστός in a FROZEN ATTRIBUTED TITLE SLOT. Compare:
  ordinary adjective: ἱερεὺς χριστός ("an anointed priest" — any one)
  vs. title: ὁ ἱερεὺς ὁ χριστός ("the anointed priest" — a specific role)
  The repetition of the article signals that χριστός has entered the NOMINAL
  sphere: it denotes a specific role, not a quality.
  [PT: LXX Lev 4:5 (Rahlfs-Hanhart)]
  [Lex: LSJ IIa; note on article usage with titles]

TITLE → QUASI-PROPER NAME (proto-stage):
  Best evidence: Dan 9:25 (Theodotion): ἕως χριστοῦ ἡγουμένου — ANARTHROUS.
  The absence of the article in a temporal formula ("until an anointed leader")
  shows χριστός beginning to behave like a name: names take no article.
  [PT: LXX/Th. Dan 9:25]
  Also: PsSol 17:32 (χριστὸς κυρίου without article).

QUASI-NAME → PROPER NAME:
  Best evidence (three criteria simultaneously):
  (a) Paul's anarthrous formula "Χριστὸς ἀπέθανεν" (1 Cor 15:3): anarthrous
      subject of a main verb = name.
  (b) The derived appellative "Χριστιανοί" (Acts 11:26): follower-names
      derive only from proper names (Καισαριανοί, Ἡρωδιανοί).
  (c) Justin's need to EXPLAIN the etymology (1 Apol. 4; Dial. 86): when
      speakers must recover a word's meaning philologically, it is a name.
  All three criteria point to full name-status no later than c. 50–55 CE
  in Paul, confirmed externally by c. 96 CE (1 Clement, Ignatius) and
  lexicographically confirmed by c. 155 CE (Justin's explanations).

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

QUESTION 5: EARLY CHURCH EVIDENCE THAT ΧΡΙΣΤΟΣ IS NO LONGER A TRANSLATION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

CRITERION: If Χριστός were still felt as a translation of מָשִׁיחַ, writers
  would not need to explain its meaning to Greek-speaking audiences.

EVIDENCE 1 — Ignatius, Phld. 6:1 (c. 107–115 CE):
  Χριστιανισμός vs. Ἰουδαϊσμός — "Christianity" as a proper-noun abstract
  system, named from the name Χριστός. This naming practice treats Χριστός
  as a FOUNDER'S NAME (like Ἡρώδης → Ἡρωδιανοί), not as a translation term.
  [PT: Ignatius, Phld. 6:1 (Schoedel ed.)]

EVIDENCE 2 — Justin, 1 Apol. 4 (c. 155 CE):
  Justin defends the NAME "Christians" (Χριστιανοί). He distinguishes:
    Χριστιανοί (from Χριστός) vs. χρηστοί (from χρηστός, "good/useful")
  If Χριστός were still transparent as "the anointed one" (a messianic
  title), Justin would explain its messianic Jewish content. Instead, he
  must explain the WORD FORM — its similarity to another Greek word that
  SOUNDS similar. This phonological confusion (Χριστός / χρηστός) is only
  possible if χριστός has become OPAQUE as a name and is now confused with
  a native Greek adjective by Greek-speaking opponents.
  [PT: Justin, First Apology 4 (TLG 0645.001, Marcovich ed.)]

EVIDENCE 3 — Tacitus, Annals 15.44 (c. 116 CE, Latin but reflecting
  Greek/Roman perception):
  "Christus, from whom the name [Christianity] is derived, suffered the
  extreme penalty during the reign of Tiberius at the hands of one of our
  procurators, Pontius Pilatus."
  Tacitus writes "Christus" as a proper name / praenomen-equivalent.
  His use of the name without explanation or translation for his Latin
  audience confirms that by c. 116 CE, the name required no decoding in
  educated circles.
  [PT: Tacitus, Ann. 15.44 — not a Greek text; cited for corroboration]

CONCLUSION ON Q5:
  By c. 107–115 CE (Ignatius), Χριστός is a FIXED CHRISTIAN IDENTIFIER —
  it names a religion, defines identity, and generates new vocabulary
  (Χριστιανισμός, χριστοφόρος, etc.) without any need to invoke its
  Hebrew/Aramaic translation background.
  By c. 155 CE (Justin), even its adjectival etymology ("anointed") has
  become non-transparent and requires explicit explanation.
  The transition is COMPLETE by the mid-2nd century CE.
