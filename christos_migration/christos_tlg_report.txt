LSJ ENTRY: χριστός
================================================================================

Headword: χριστός, -ή, -όν  (verbal adj. from χρίω)
Source: Liddell–Scott–Jones, A Greek–English Lexicon, 9th ed. (1940), s.v.

I. Of things: "to be rubbed on, used as ointment or salve" (opp. πιστός,
   "to be drunk").
   — Aeschylus, Prometheus Vinctus 480
   — Euripides, Hippolytus 516

II. Of persons: "anointed"
   IIa. Anointed priests: LXX Leviticus 4:3, 4:5, 4:16, 6:22
       τὸ ἔλαιον τὸ χ. = "anointing oil": LXX Leviticus 21:10
   IIb. Kings of Israel — ὁ χ. κυρίου ("the Lord's anointed"):
       LXX 1 Samuel 24:7; LXX Psalm 17:51 (= MT Psalm 18:50)
   IIc. Plural: χριστοί σου ("your anointed ones"):
       LXX Habakkuk 3:13; 1 Maccabees 2:57(?)

III. In NT: ὁ χ. = the Messiah, translating Heb. מָשִׁיחַ.
   — Matthew 2:4; Luke 2:26 (as title, with article)
   — Matthew 1:1: Ἰησοῦ Χριστοῦ (as proper name, without article)
   — Matthew 16:16: Ἰησοῦς ὁ λεγόμενος Χριστός (appositive)

Cross-reference: Θayer, Greek Lexicon of the NT (extensive NT discussion);
   Hebrew מָשִׁיחַ = māšîaḥ; Latin Christus / Messias.

Etymology: χρίω ("to anoint, smear") + verbal adjective suffix -τός →
   χριστός, "that which is/has been/is to be anointed/smeared."

OCCURRENCE 1
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

AUTHOR:       Aeschylus (Αἰσχύλος)
WORK:         Prometheus Vinctus (Προμηθεὺς Δεσμώτης)
DATE:         c. 460–456 BCE (date debated; possibly post-Aeschylean)
PERIOD:       Classical

GREEK TEXT:
  [lines 478–483, following Dindorf/West numbering]
  εἶτ' οὐ μέγιστον τοῦτ' ἐπεδειξάμην βροτοῖς;
  φάρμακα δ' ἐξηῦρον πολλὰ τῶν νοσημάτων
  480 [χριστά τε καὶ πιστά], ὡς ἀπαλλάξω νόσους.

  Note: The exact line-numbering varies by edition. The LSJ cites Pr. 480
  for χριστά in the contrast χριστά (smearable) vs. πιστά (drinkable).
  The context is Prometheus's catalogue of gifts to humanity, including
  knowledge of medicinal arts.

TRANSLATION / GLOSS:
  "…and I discovered many remedies for diseases — smearable [ones] and
  drinkable [ones] — so that I might free [them] from illnesses."
  [χριστά = salves / topical applications; πιστά = potions / oral medicines]

WORD FORM:    χριστά — neuter plural nominative (predicative adjective)
              from χριστός, -ή, -όν

MEANING IN CONTEXT:
  Literal/pharmacological adjective: "to be smeared on, applicable as
  ointment." χριστά are topical remedies as opposed to πιστά (drinkable
  medicines). No religious or ritual dimension whatever.

REFERENCE:    Aeschylus, Prometheus Vinctus 480
              TLG Canon: 0085.003
              Perseus: Perseus:text:1999.01.0010

LEXICON NOTE:
  LSJ I (of things): "to be rubbed on, used as ointment or salve, opp. πιστός,
  Aesch. Pr. 480." This is the earliest attested use in the extant corpus.
  The form belongs entirely to the medical/pharmaceutical vocabulary and
  carries no semantic connection to ritual anointing or messianic usage.

OCCURRENCE 2
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

AUTHOR:       Euripides (Εὐριπίδης)
WORK:         Hippolytus (Ἱππόλυτος)
DATE:         428 BCE (first performance)
PERIOD:       Classical

GREEK TEXT:
  [lines 513–516]
  ΤΡΟΦΟΣ: ἔχω τι τούτων φάρμακον παυστήριον.
  ΦΑΙΔΡΑ: χριστὸν ἢ ποτόν;
  ΤΡΟΦΟΣ: οὐκ οἶδ'· εὐτυχεῖν βούλου, τέκνον.

  Full line 516:
  ΦΑΙΔΡΑ: πότερα δὲ χριστὸν ἢ ποτὸν τὸ φάρμακον;

TRANSLATION / GLOSS:
  NURSE: "I have some remedy that will stop this."
  PHAEDRA: "Is the drug a salve [to be smeared] or a potion [to be drunk]?"
  NURSE: "I don't know — just wish to be happy, child."

WORD FORM:    χριστόν — neuter singular accusative (predicative adjective /
              substantivized: "a smearable thing," i.e., a topical application)

MEANING IN CONTEXT:
  Literal/pharmacological: Phaedra asks whether the Nurse's love-charm is
  applied externally (χριστόν — a salve) or taken internally (ποτόν — a
  potion). The word contrasts application methods. No religious resonance.
  The dramatic context concerns Phaedra's passion for Hippolytus.

REFERENCE:    Euripides, Hippolytus 516
              TLG Canon: 0006.005
              Perseus: Perseus:text:1999.01.0105

LEXICON NOTE:
  LSJ I: cites Eur. Hipp. 516 alongside Aesch. Pr. 480 as the two Classical
  witnesses to χριστός as a pharmaceutical adjective. The word here is
  purely technical medical vocabulary. Contrast with πιστός (from πίνω,
  "drinkable") makes the opposition explicit.

OCCURRENCE 3
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

AUTHOR:       Hippocrates / Corpus Hippocraticum (Ἱπποκράτης)
WORK:         De ulceribus (Περὶ Ἑλκῶν) and other treatises
DATE:         c. 420–380 BCE (compilation of Hippocratic corpus)
PERIOD:       Classical

GREEK TEXT:
  [De ulceribus — representative passage, chapter 18]
  τοῖσι δὲ ἐν θέρει μὲν καὶ χειμῶνι, ἐλαίῳ χρηστέον ἐπὶ τὰ ἕλκεα
  ὅσα δεῖται τοιούτων φαρμάκων.

  [De affectionibus and other surgical/pharmacological treatises also
  employ χριστά / χριστέον / χριστά φάρμακα for topical medicaments]

TRANSLATION / GLOSS:
  "In both summer and winter one must apply oil to those ulcers that require
  such medicines."
  [χρηστέον / χριστά = verbal adjectives indicating 'to be smeared/applied']

WORD FORM:    χριστά / χρηστέον (verbal adj.) — neuter forms

MEANING IN CONTEXT:
  Purely pharmacological. The Hippocratic corpus uses χριστός and related
  forms (χρηστέον, χριστά φάρμακα) throughout its surgical and
  pharmacological treatises to indicate topically-applied medications, as
  distinguished from internal medicines (πιστά / ποτά).

REFERENCE:    Hippocrates, De ulceribus 18
              TLG Canon: 0627.007
              Perseus: Perseus:text:1999.01.0248

LEXICON NOTE:
  The LSJ entry does not separately list Hippocrates for χριστός itself,
  but the medical contrast of χριστά (topical) vs. πιστά (drinkable) is
  documented in LSJ under related entries. The Hippocratic corpus is the
  primary locus of this pharmacological vocabulary in Classical prose.

OCCURRENCE 4
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

AUTHOR:       Septuagint — Leviticus (LXX Λευιτικόν)
WORK:         Septuagint (LXX)
DATE:         Translation c. 250–150 BCE (Pentateuch among earliest)
PERIOD:       Hellenistic

GREEK TEXT:
  Leviticus 4:3:
  ἐὰν ὁ ἀρχιερεὺς ὁ κεχρισμένος ἁμάρτῃ τοῦ τὸν λαὸν ἁμαρτεῖν, καὶ
  προσάξει περὶ τῆς ἁμαρτίας αὐτοῦ, ἧς ἥμαρτεν, μόσχον ἐκ βοῶν
  ἄμωμον τῷ κυρίῳ περὶ τῆς ἁμαρτίας.

  Leviticus 4:5:
  καὶ λήψεται ὁ ἱερεὺς ὁ χριστὸς ὁ τετελειωμένος τὰς χεῖρας

  Leviticus 6:22 (MT 6:15):
  ὁ ἱερεὺς ὁ χριστὸς ἀντ' αὐτοῦ ἐκ τῶν υἱῶν αὐτοῦ ποιήσει αὐτήν

TRANSLATION / GLOSS:
  Lev 4:3: "…if the anointed high priest sins so as to bring guilt on
  the people…"
  Lev 4:5: "…and the anointed priest who has been ordained shall take…"
  Lev 6:22: "…the anointed priest who succeeds him from among his sons
  shall perform it…"

WORD FORM:    χριστός — masc. nom. sing. (attributive adjective + article:
              ὁ χριστός = "the anointed one")
              Also kec-hrisménos (perfect participle) at 4:3

MEANING IN CONTEXT:
  Jewish ritual/institutional title: the anointed (high) priest, the one who
  has been ritually anointed with oil according to Mosaic law. This is the
  LXX translators' choice to render Hebrew הַכֹּהֵן הַמָּשִׁיחַ
  (ha-kohen ha-māšîaḥ, "the anointed priest"). The title designates
  consecrated status, not a future eschatological figure.

REFERENCE:    LXX Leviticus 4:3, 4:5, 4:16, 6:22
              Rahlfs-Hanhart LXX: Leuitikon 4:3 etc.
              TLG full corpus (LXX): TLG 0527

LEXICON NOTE:
  LSJ IIa: "anointed [as priestly title]… LXX Lev 4.5, 4.16, 6.22." The
  entry marks this as a distinct sense from the classical pharmaceutical use,
  representing the beginning of χριστός as an institutional/religious title.

OCCURRENCE 5
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

AUTHOR:       Septuagint — 1 Samuel / 1 Reigns (LXX Α΄ Σαμουήλ)
WORK:         Septuagint (LXX)
DATE:         Translation c. 200–150 BCE
PERIOD:       Hellenistic

GREEK TEXT:
  1 Samuel 2:10 (LXX):
  κύριος ἀσθενήσει ἀντίδικον αὐτοῦ· κύριος ἅγιος. μὴ καυχάσθω ὁ
  φρόνιμος ἐν τῇ φρονήσει αὐτοῦ, καὶ μὴ καυχάσθω ὁ δυνατὸς ἐν τῇ
  δυνάμει αὐτοῦ, καὶ μὴ καυχάσθω ὁ πλούσιος ἐν τῷ πλούτῳ αὐτοῦ·
  ἀλλ' ἢ ἐν τούτῳ καυχάσθω ὁ καυχώμενος, συνίειν καὶ γινώσκειν τὸν
  κύριον· κύριος ἀναβιβάζει καὶ καταβιβάζει, κύριος νεκροῖ καὶ ζῳοποιεῖ·
  κύριος πτωχίζει καὶ πλουτίζει, ταπεινοῖ καὶ ἀνυψοῖ· ἀνίστησιν ἐκ γῆς
  πένητα καὶ ἀπὸ κοπρίας ἐγείρει πτωχόν... κύριος δώσει ἰσχὺν τοῖς
  βασιλεῦσιν ἡμῶν καὶ ὑψώσει κέρας χριστοῦ αὐτοῦ.

  1 Samuel 24:7 (LXX = 24:6 MT):
  καὶ εἶπεν Δαυὶδ πρὸς τοὺς ἄνδρας αὐτοῦ Μηδαμῶς μοι παρὰ κυρίου,
  εἰ ποιήσω τὸ ῥῆμα τοῦτο τῷ κυρίῳ μου τῷ χριστῷ κυρίου ἐπεκτεῖναι
  χεῖρά μου ἐπ' αὐτόν, ὅτι χριστὸς κυρίου ἐστὶν οὗτος.

TRANSLATION / GLOSS:
  1 Sam 2:10: "…the LORD will give strength to our kings and will exalt
  the horn of his anointed [one]." (Hannah's Song)
  1 Sam 24:7: "…David said to his men, 'Far be it from me by the LORD's
  sake that I should do this thing to my master, the LORD's anointed,
  to stretch out my hand against him, for he is the LORD's anointed.'"

WORD FORM:    1 Sam 2:10 — χριστοῦ αὐτοῦ (gen. sing. — "of his anointed")
              1 Sam 24:7 — τῷ χριστῷ κυρίου (dat. sing.) &
                          χριστὸς κυρίου ἐστὶν (nom. sing., predicate)

MEANING IN CONTEXT:
  Jewish royal/messianic title: the king of Israel as Yahweh's anointed
  representative. At 1 Sam 2:10 (Hannah's prayer), the reference is
  prospective (no Israelite king yet exists), making it arguably the first
  messianic use of the Greek word in the LXX. At 24:7, Saul is the
  χριστὸς κυρίου; David refuses to harm him because of this status.

REFERENCE:    LXX 1 Samuel (= 1 Reigns) 2:10; 24:7
              Rahlfs-Hanhart LXX
              TLG 0527 (full corpus)

LEXICON NOTE:
  LSJ IIb: "Kings of Israel: 1 K[ings, = 1 Sam] 24.7, Ps. 17.51."
  The anointing of Israelite kings with oil (cf. 1 Sam 16) provides the
  literal basis for the title; χριστός here denotes legitimate royal status
  conferred by divine authorization through ritual.

OCCURRENCE 6
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

AUTHOR:       Septuagint — Psalms (LXX Ψαλμοί)
WORK:         Septuagint (LXX)
DATE:         Translation c. 200–100 BCE
PERIOD:       Hellenistic

GREEK TEXT:
  Psalm 2:2 (LXX):
  παρέστησαν οἱ βασιλεῖς τῆς γῆς καὶ οἱ ἄρχοντες συνήχθησαν ἐπὶ τὸ
  αὐτὸ κατὰ τοῦ κυρίου καὶ κατὰ τοῦ χριστοῦ αὐτοῦ.

  Psalm 17:51 (LXX) [= MT Psalm 18:50]:
  μεγαλύνων τὰς σωτηρίας τοῦ βασιλέως αὐτοῦ καὶ ποιῶν ἔλεος τῷ
  χριστῷ αὐτοῦ, τῷ Δαυὶδ καὶ τῷ σπέρματι αὐτοῦ ἕως αἰῶνος.

  Psalm 88:38 (LXX) [= MT Psalm 89:38]:
  σὺ δὲ ἀπώσω καὶ ἐξουδένωσας, ἀνεβάλου τὸν χριστόν σου.

  Psalm 88:51 (LXX) [= MT Psalm 89:51]:
  οὗ ὠνείδισαν οἱ ἐχθροί σου, κύριε, οὗ ὠνείδισαν τὴν ἀνταλλαγὴν
  τοῦ χριστοῦ σου.

  Psalm 131:10 (LXX) [= MT Psalm 132:10]:
  ἕνεκεν Δαυὶδ τοῦ δούλου σου μὴ ἀποστρέψῃς τὸ πρόσωπον τοῦ χριστοῦ σου.

  Psalm 131:17 (LXX) [= MT Psalm 132:17]:
  ἐκεῖ ἐξανατελῶ κέρας τῷ Δαυὶδ ἡτοίμασα λύχνον τῷ χριστῷ μου.

TRANSLATION / GLOSS:
  Ps 2:2: "The kings of the earth stood up and the rulers gathered together
  against the LORD and against his anointed."
  Ps 17:51: "…who shows loving-kindness to his anointed — to David and to
  his seed forever."
  Ps 88:38: "But you have rejected and spurned [him], you have been angry
  with your anointed."
  Ps 131:10: "…do not turn away the face of your anointed one."
  Ps 131:17: "…I have prepared a lamp for my anointed."

WORD FORM:    Various cases:
              χριστοῦ αὐτοῦ (gen.) — Ps 2:2
              χριστῷ αὐτοῦ (dat.) — Ps 17:51
              τὸν χριστόν σου (acc.) — Ps 88:38
              τοῦ χριστοῦ σου (gen.) — Ps 88:51, 131:10
              τῷ χριστῷ μου (dat.) — Ps 131:17

MEANING IN CONTEXT:
  Dual register: royal (Davidic king) and proto-messianic. Ps 2:2 is the
  most theologically charged — later applied messianically in NT (Acts 4:26)
  and in early Jewish apocalypticism (Psalms of Solomon 17). Ps 131:17
  ("I have prepared a lamp for my anointed") points to a dynastic continuity
  that becomes eschatologized in later tradition.

REFERENCE:    LXX Psalms 2:2; 17:51; 88:38; 88:51; 131:10; 131:17
              (= MT Psalms 2:2; 18:50; 89:37; 89:51; 132:10; 132:17)
              Rahlfs-Hanhart LXX

LEXICON NOTE:
  LSJ IIb cites Ps. 17.51 (= MT 18.50). The Psalter contains more
  occurrences of χριστός than any other single LXX book; these passages
  form the primary scriptural basis for Jewish and early Christian messianic
  interpretation.

OCCURRENCE 7
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

AUTHOR:       Septuagint — Isaiah (LXX Ἠσαΐας)
WORK:         Septuagint (LXX)
DATE:         Translation c. 200–150 BCE
PERIOD:       Hellenistic

GREEK TEXT:
  Isaiah 45:1 (LXX):
  Οὕτως λέγει κύριος ὁ θεὸς τῷ χριστῷ μου Κύρῳ, οὗ ἐκράτησα τῆς
  δεξιᾶς ἐπακοῦσαι ἔμπροσθεν αὐτοῦ ἔθνη, καὶ ἰσχὺν βασιλέων διαρρήξω·
  ἀνοίξω ἔμπροσθεν αὐτοῦ θύρας, καὶ πόλεις οὐ συγκλεισθήσονται.

TRANSLATION / GLOSS:
  "Thus says the Lord God to my anointed, Cyrus — whose right hand I have
  grasped — to subdue nations before him, to ungird the loins of kings,
  to open doors before him so that gates will not be shut."

WORD FORM:    τῷ χριστῷ μου — dat. sing. masc. (indirect object: "to my
              anointed [one]")

MEANING IN CONTEXT:
  Non-Jewish royal anointing: uniquely, the LXX applies χριστός to Cyrus II
  of Persia (c. 600–530 BCE) as God's instrument of deliverance.  This is
  the only instance in the Hebrew Bible / LXX where a non-Israelite is
  called māšîaḥ / χριστός. Theologically significant because it
  decouples the title from Davidic descent.

REFERENCE:    LXX Isaiah 45:1
              Rahlfs-Hanhart LXX

LEXICON NOTE:
  This occurrence stretches the royal/priestly category of LSJ IIb: Cyrus
  is χριστός κυρίου without being an Israelite king or priest. The semantic
  category here is "divinely authorized agent, anointed for a mission."

OCCURRENCE 8
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

AUTHOR:       Septuagint — Daniel (LXX & Theodotion, Δανιήλ)
WORK:         Septuagint (LXX)
DATE:         Translation c. 100 BCE (Theodotion revision: c. 2nd century CE)
PERIOD:       Hellenistic / Roman

GREEK TEXT:
  Daniel 9:25 (Theodotion):
  καὶ γνώσῃ καὶ συνήσεις· ἀπὸ ἐξόδου λόγου τοῦ ἀποκριθῆναι καὶ τοῦ
  οἰκοδομῆσαι Ἰερουσαλὴμ ἕως χριστοῦ ἡγουμένου ἑβδομάδες ἑπτὰ

  Daniel 9:26 (LXX):
  καὶ μετὰ τὰς ἑβδομάδας τὰς ἑξήκοντα δύο ἐξολοθρευθήσεται χρῖσμα
  καὶ κρίμα οὐκ ἔστιν ἐν αὐτῷ·

  [Note: Theodotion 9:26 reads ἐξολοθρευθήσεται χριστός — "an anointed one
  will be cut off" — the LXX reads χρῖσμα ("anointing") in place of χριστός]

TRANSLATION / GLOSS:
  Dan 9:25 (Th): "…from the going out of a word to restore and build
  Jerusalem until an anointed leader, [there shall be] seven weeks."
  Dan 9:26 (Th): "…and after the sixty-two weeks an anointed one will be
  cut off and will have nothing."

WORD FORM:    Dan 9:25 — χριστοῦ ἡγουμένου (gen. sing. — partitive gen.,
              "an anointed leader")
              Dan 9:26 (Th) — χριστός (nom. sing., subject)

MEANING IN CONTEXT:
  Eschatological / proto-messianic: Daniel 9's "seventy weeks" prophecy
  uses χριστός for a future anointed leader whose coming is set within a
  numerical schema. This text becomes foundational for messianic chronology
  in Second Temple Judaism (Dead Sea Scrolls) and early Christianity.

REFERENCE:    LXX Daniel 9:25–26 (Theodotion text)
              Rahlfs-Hanhart LXX

LEXICON NOTE:
  The LXX variants (χρῖσμα vs. χριστός) show the translators were aware
  of the ambiguity. The Theodotion reading with χριστός is the version cited
  in NT (cf. possible allusion in John 1:41) and patristic exegesis.

OCCURRENCE 9
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

AUTHOR:       Septuagint — Habakkuk (LXX Ἀμβακοὺμ)
WORK:         Septuagint (LXX)
DATE:         Translation c. 200–150 BCE
PERIOD:       Hellenistic

GREEK TEXT:
  Habakkuk 3:13 (LXX):
  ἐξήλθες εἰς σωτηρίαν λαοῦ σου τοῦ σῷσαι τοὺς χριστούς σου·
  ἔβαλες εἰς κεφαλὰς ἀνόμων θάνατον, ἐξήγειρας δεσμοὺς ἕως τραχήλου

TRANSLATION / GLOSS:
  "You went out for the salvation of your people, to save your anointed ones.
  You crushed the head of the house of the wicked, laying it bare from
  foundation to neck."

WORD FORM:    τοὺς χριστούς σου — masc. acc. plural ("your anointed ones")
              [PLURAL — the only LXX occurrence of χριστός in the plural,
              aside from possible Maccabaean texts]

MEANING IN CONTEXT:
  Plural royal/ritual title: "your anointed ones" refers collectively either
  to the people of Israel as God's anointed nation, or to the Davidic kings
  as a line. The plural χριστοί is extremely rare and represents a
  corporate application of the title.

REFERENCE:    LXX Habakkuk 3:13
              Rahlfs-Hanhart LXX

LEXICON NOTE:
  LSJ s.v. cites this as evidence of the plural. The collective use is
  attested nowhere in the NT; by the time of Christian usage χριστός has
  been restricted to a singular title for Jesus.

OCCURRENCE 10
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

AUTHOR:       Septuagint — Lamentations (LXX Θρῆνοι Ἱερεμίου)
WORK:         Septuagint (LXX)
DATE:         Translation c. 200–150 BCE
PERIOD:       Hellenistic

GREEK TEXT:
  Lamentations 4:20 (LXX):
  πνεῦμα προσώπου ἡμῶν χριστὸς κυρίου συνελήμφθη ἐν ταῖς διαφθοραῖς
  αὐτῶν, οὗ εἴπαμεν ἐν τῇ σκιᾷ αὐτοῦ ζησόμεθα ἐν τοῖς ἔθνεσιν.

TRANSLATION / GLOSS:
  "The breath of our nostrils, the LORD's anointed, was caught in their
  traps — he of whom we had said, 'Under his shadow we shall live among
  the nations.'"

WORD FORM:    χριστὸς κυρίου — nom. sing. masc. (subject in apposition
              to "πνεῦμα προσώπου ἡμῶν")

MEANING IN CONTEXT:
  Royal elegiac title: the Babylonian capture of king Zedekiah. χριστὸς
  κυρίου here is a royal title mourned in the context of national
  catastrophe. The verse poignantly portrays the king as the "life-breath"
  of the nation. Early Christian readers applied this to Christ's passion.

REFERENCE:    LXX Lamentations 4:20
              Rahlfs-Hanhart LXX

LEXICON NOTE:
  Standard LSJ IIb type. The formula χριστὸς κυρίου (nearly always with
  genitive κυρίου) is the characteristic LXX construction across Samuel,
  Psalms, and Lamentations.

OCCURRENCE 11
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

AUTHOR:       Flavius Josephus (Ἰώσηπος / Φλάουιος Ἰώσηπος)
WORK:         Antiquitates Judaicae (Ἰουδαϊκὴ Ἀρχαιολογία) Book 18
DATE:         c. 93–94 CE
PERIOD:       Roman

GREEK TEXT:
  Antiquities 18.63–64 (Testimonium Flavianum):
  Γίνεται δὲ κατὰ τοῦτον τὸν χρόνον Ἰησοῦς σοφὸς ἀνήρ, εἴγε ἄνδρα
  αὐτὸν λέγειν χρή· ἦν γὰρ παραδόξων ἔργων ποιητής, διδάσκαλος
  ἀνθρώπων τῶν ἡδονῇ τἀληθῆ δεχομένων, καὶ πολλοὺς μὲν Ἰουδαίους,
  πολλοὺς δὲ καὶ τοῦ Ἑλληνικοῦ ἐπηγάγετο· ὁ χριστὸς οὗτος ἦν. καὶ
  αὐτὸν ἐνδείξει τῶν πρώτων ἀνδρῶν παρ' ἡμῖν σταυρῷ ἐπιτετιμηκότος
  Πιλάτου οὐκ ἐπαύσαντο οἱ τὸ πρῶτον ἀγαπήσαντες· ἐφάνη γὰρ αὐτοῖς
  τρίτην ἔχων ἡμέραν πάλιν ζῶν τῶν θείων προφητῶν ταῦτά τε καὶ ἄλλα
  μυρία περὶ αὐτοῦ θαυμάσια εἰρηκότων. εἰς ἔτι τε νῦν τῶν Χριστιανῶν
  ἀπὸ τοῦδε ὠνομασμένον οὐκ ἐπέλιπε τὸ φῦλον.

TRANSLATION / GLOSS:
  "About this time there lived Jesus, a wise man, if indeed one ought to
  call him a man. For he was one who did surprising deeds, and a teacher
  of such people as accept the truth gladly. He led many Jews and many
  of the Greeks astray. He was the Christ. When Pilate, upon hearing him
  accused by men of the highest standing among us, had condemned him to be
  crucified, those who had come to love him did not cease to follow him…"

WORD FORM:    ὁ χριστὸς οὗτος ἦν — nom. sing. ("he was the Christ")
              Χριστιανῶν — gen. pl. (derived proper noun, "Christians")

MEANING IN CONTEXT:
  Christian theological title (though controversial): The Testimonium
  Flavianum is debated; most scholars today accept a Josephan core with
  Christian interpolation/embellishment. The phrase ὁ χριστὸς οὗτος ἦν
  ("he was the Christ") in its current form reflects Christian usage. Even
  in a reconstructed neutral original (e.g., "he was called the Christ"),
  Josephus acknowledges the title. The derived noun Χριστιανοί ("Christians")
  confirms wider Roman-era currency of the title as a proper name.

REFERENCE:    Josephus, Antiquitates Judaicae 18.63–64
              Niese edition; TLG 0526.001

LEXICON NOTE:
  Josephus uses ὁ χριστός as a recognized proper name / title known to his
  audience. Whether authentic or interpolated, the passage reflects that by
  c. 94 CE the Greek title had become the standard Christian designator.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
OCCURRENCE 12
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

AUTHOR:       Flavius Josephus (Φλάουιος Ἰώσηπος)
WORK:         Antiquitates Judaicae Book 20
DATE:         c. 93–94 CE
PERIOD:       Roman

GREEK TEXT:
  Antiquities 20.200 (James passage):
  Ἄναιος δὲ τοιοῦτος ὢν τὸν χαρακτῆρα… ἀδελφὸν δὲ αὐτοῦ τοῦ
  λεγομένου Χριστοῦ, Ἰάκωβος ὄνομα αὐτῷ, καί τινας ἑτέρους, ὡς
  παρανομησάντων κατηγορίαν ποιησάμενος, παρέδωκεν λιθασθησομένους.

TRANSLATION / GLOSS:
  "…and brought before [the Sanhedrin] the brother of Jesus who was called
  Christ, whose name was James, and some others; and when he had formed an
  accusation against them as breakers of the law, he delivered them to be
  stoned."

WORD FORM:    τοῦ λεγομένου Χριστοῦ — gen. sing. masc. (attributive
              participial clause: "the one who was called Christ")

MEANING IN CONTEXT:
  Christian title used with journalistic detachment: τοῦ λεγομένου
  Χριστοῦ ("the one called/so-called Christ") is the standard Josephan
  formula of attribution without endorsement. The participial λεγόμενος
  signals that Josephus reports others' usage rather than affirming
  the title. This passage is widely regarded by scholars as authentic
  Josephus (no Christian interpolation suspected), making it among the most
  reliable non-Christian attestations of Χριστός as a title.

REFERENCE:    Josephus, Antiquitates Judaicae 20.200
              Niese edition; TLG 0526.001

LEXICON NOTE:
  The formula ὁ λεγόμενος Χριστός recurs at Matthew 27:17, 22 in the
  same distancing construction. Josephus uses it to identify Jesus for
  readers already familiar with the title, treating Χριστός as a proper
  name / distinctive epithet without engaging its theological content.

OCCURRENCE 13 — NEW TESTAMENT (Representative: Matthew)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

AUTHOR:       Matthew (Ματθαῖος) — attributed
WORK:         Gospel of Matthew (Κατὰ Ματθαῖον)
DATE:         c. 80–90 CE
PERIOD:       Roman

GREEK TEXT:
  Matthew 1:1:
  Βίβλος γενέσεως Ἰησοῦ Χριστοῦ, υἱοῦ Δαυὶδ, υἱοῦ Ἀβραάμ.

  Matthew 2:4:
  καὶ συναγαγὼν πάντας τοὺς ἀρχιερεῖς καὶ γραμματεῖς τοῦ λαοῦ
  ἐπυνθάνετο παρ' αὐτῶν ποῦ ὁ Χριστὸς γεννᾶται.

  Matthew 16:16:
  ἀποκριθεὶς δὲ Σίμων Πέτρος εἶπεν· Σὺ εἶ ὁ Χριστὸς ὁ υἱὸς τοῦ θεοῦ
  τοῦ ζῶντος.

  Matthew 22:42:
  λέγων· Τί ὑμῖν δοκεῖ περὶ τοῦ Χριστοῦ; τίνος υἱός ἐστιν;

TRANSLATION / GLOSS:
  1:1: "The book of the genealogy of Jesus Christ, son of David, son of
  Abraham."
  2:4: "…he inquired of them where the Christ was to be born."
  16:16: "Simon Peter answered and said, 'You are the Christ, the Son of the
  living God.'"
  22:42: "…saying, 'What do you think about the Christ? Whose son is he?'"

WORD FORM:    Ἰησοῦ Χριστοῦ (gen., proper name compound); ὁ Χριστός
              (nom. with article, title); τοῦ Χριστοῦ (gen. with article)

MEANING IN CONTEXT:
  Christian theological title / proper name: In Matthew, Χριστός functions
  both as article-free proper name (Ἰησοῦς Χριστός) and as a definite
  title requiring the article (ὁ Χριστός). The title encodes the claim
  that Jesus fulfills the LXX messianic references (esp. Ps 2:2, Isa 9,
  Mic 5:2). Matthew uses it approximately 16 times.

REFERENCE:    Matthew 1:1; 2:4; 16:16; 22:42 (NA28)
              TLG Canon: 0031.001

LEXICON NOTE:
  LSJ III: "ὁ χ. the Messiah (Hebr. מָשִׁיχַ), LXX, NT, etc."
  With article = definite title "the Christ/Messiah"; without = proper name.
  Paul's letters make this shift explicit: Ἰησοῦς Χριστός (name)
  vs. ὁ Χριστός (title).

OCCURRENCE 14 — NEW TESTAMENT (Representative: Paul — 1 Corinthians)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

AUTHOR:       Paul of Tarsus (Παῦλος)
WORK:         First Letter to the Corinthians (Α΄ Κορινθίους)
DATE:         c. 53–55 CE (earliest surviving datable Christian document)
PERIOD:       Roman

GREEK TEXT:
  1 Corinthians 1:23–24:
  ἡμεῖς δὲ κηρύσσομεν Χριστὸν ἐσταυρωμένον, Ἰουδαίοις μὲν σκάνδαλον,
  ἔθνεσιν δὲ μωρίαν, αὐτοῖς δὲ τοῖς κλητοῖς, Ἰουδαίοις τε καὶ
  Ἕλλησιν, Χριστὸν θεοῦ δύναμιν καὶ θεοῦ σοφίαν.

  1 Corinthians 15:3–4:
  παρέδωκα γὰρ ὑμῖν ἐν πρώτοις, ὃ καὶ παρέλαβον, ὅτι Χριστὸς ἀπέθανεν
  ὑπὲρ τῶν ἁμαρτιῶν ἡμῶν κατὰ τὰς γραφάς, καὶ ὅτι ἐτάφη, καὶ ὅτι
  ἐγήγερται τῇ ἡμέρᾳ τῇ τρίτῃ κατὰ τὰς γραφάς.

TRANSLATION / GLOSS:
  1 Cor 1:23–24: "…but we preach Christ crucified — a stumbling block to
  Jews and foolishness to Gentiles, but to those who are called, both Jews
  and Greeks, Christ the power of God and the wisdom of God."
  1 Cor 15:3–4: "For I delivered to you as of first importance what I also
  received: that Christ died for our sins according to the scriptures, and
  that he was buried, and that he was raised on the third day according to
  the scriptures."

WORD FORM:    Χριστὸν ἐσταυρωμένον (acc., modified by ptcp.);
              Χριστὸς ἀπέθανεν (nom., subject — proper name)

MEANING IN CONTEXT:
  Christian theological title fully absorbed as proper name. In Paul, Χριστός
  has largely shed its article and functions as a proper name, though
  retaining its titular force in contexts like ὁ Χριστός. The creedal
  formula at 15:3–4 is among the earliest dateable confessional texts in
  Christianity (pre-Pauline, possibly c. 35–40 CE). Paul uses Χριστός
  approximately 65 times in 1 Cor alone.

REFERENCE:    1 Corinthians 1:23–24; 15:3–4 (NA28)
              TLG Canon: 0031.006

LEXICON NOTE:
  The shift from title (ὁ Χριστός) to proper name (Χριστός without article)
  is first fully systematic in Paul. LSJ notes the name usage develops from
  the titular; Thayer's NT lexicon provides extensive documentation.

OCCURRENCE 15 — NEW TESTAMENT (Representative: John)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

AUTHOR:       John (Ἰωάννης) — attributed
WORK:         Gospel of John (Κατὰ Ἰωάννην)
DATE:         c. 90–100 CE
PERIOD:       Roman

GREEK TEXT:
  John 1:41:
  εὑρίσκει οὗτος πρῶτον τὸν ἀδελφὸν τὸν ἴδιον Σίμωνα καὶ λέγει αὐτῷ·
  Εὑρήκαμεν τὸν Μεσσίαν, ὅ ἐστιν μεθερμηνευόμενον ὁ Χριστός.

  John 20:31:
  ταῦτα δὲ γέγραπται ἵνα πιστεύ[σ]ητε ὅτι Ἰησοῦς ἐστιν ὁ Χριστὸς
  ὁ υἱὸς τοῦ θεοῦ, καὶ ἵνα πιστεύοντες ζωὴν ἔχητε ἐν τῷ ὀνόματι αὐτοῦ.

TRANSLATION / GLOSS:
  John 1:41: "He [Andrew] first found his own brother Simon and said to him,
  'We have found the Messiah' (which is translated, the Christ)."
  John 20:31: "…these things are written so that you may believe that Jesus
  is the Christ, the Son of God."

WORD FORM:    ὁ Χριστός (nom. with article, title);
              John 1:41 explicitly equates Μεσσίας (transliterated Hebrew/
              Aramaic) = ὁ Χριστός (Greek translation)

MEANING IN CONTEXT:
  Christian theological title. John 1:41 is the NT's only explicit
  Greek–Semitic bilingual gloss on the word: "Messias = ho Christos."
  This is the semantically richest NT moment for establishing the
  equivalence that underlies the entire Christian use of the term.
  John uses Χριστός approximately 19 times; John 20:31 is the stated
  purpose-statement of the Gospel.

REFERENCE:    John 1:41; 20:31 (NA28)
              TLG Canon: 0031.004

LEXICON NOTE:
  John 1:41 provides what no other NT passage does explicitly: a translation
  note equating Μεσσίας with Χριστός, demonstrating that by c. 100 CE the
  Greek title had become fully conventional and required explanation only for
  non-Aramaic audiences.
