# Pagan Roots of Monotheism

**A philological and cultural-historical research archive** investigating the Greek and Mediterranean substrates underlying early Christian practice — from the pharmaceutical origins of *χριστός* to the pharmacological traditions of Greek mystery religion to the mythological grammar shared between the Argonautica and the Gospels.

Published via GitHub Pages: [`docs/`](docs/index.html)

---

## About This Repository

This archive houses ongoing academic philological research into the pagan origins and Hellenistic cultural substrates of early Christianity. The research proceeds through three interconnected branches:

1. **χριστός Migration** — A diachronic philological history of the Greek word *χριστός* from its earliest pharmaceutical attestation in Classical tragedy (Aeschylus, c.460 BCE) through its use as a Jewish ritual title in the LXX, its eschatological intensification in Second Temple literature, and its crystallization into both a messianic title and a proper name in the New Testament. Fifteen documented TLG occurrences; five-stage semantic map; full LSJ lexicon reconstruction; 38-entry occurrences database.

2. **Pharmakon Research** — A philological-pharmacological investigation of the ritual drug traditions embedded in Greek mystery religion (Eleusinian, Bacchic, Orphic) as a neglected cultural substrate for understanding the ministry and passion of Jesus. Argues that the physical act of anointing (*χρίω*) with pharmacologically active substances connects the pharmakological lineage from Medea and Hecate through Galen's theriac to the anointing oils, death-drugs, and sacred meals of early Christianity. Includes a 63-entry master sources database and a cumulative probabilistic case across ten independent evidence streams.

3. **Jason / Jesus Sidequest** — A mythological typology study documenting the etymological and structural relationship between *Ἰάσων* (Jason) and *Ἰησοῦς* (Jesus), historically documented in 2 Maccabees 4:7. Develops the hypothesis that the Argonautica (Apollonius Rhodius, c.250 BCE) functions as a literary proto-Gospel, with Medea as a literary ancestor of Mary Magdalene, through six structural parallels and five axes of female typological comparison.

### Methodological Principles

- All Greek cited from standard critical editions (TLG, Perseus Digital Library, Rahlfs-Hanhart LXX, NA28, Niese Josephus)
- Lexicography via LSJ 9th edition, Thayer NT Lexicon, Brown-Driver-Briggs
- Claims distinguished as: philologically established, historically documented, scholarly inference, working hypothesis, and disputed
- Epistemological standard: convergent probability, not proof (appropriate to ancient historical inquiry)
- No institutional affiliation; independent research for open academic access
