LSJ Entry: χριστός

Liddell–Scott–Jones, 9th ed. (1940), s.v. χριστός

Headword: χριστός, -ή, -όν  (verbal adj. from χρίω)

I. Of things: "to be rubbed on, used as ointment or salve" (opp. πιστός, "to be drunk") — Aeschylus, Prometheus Vinctus 480  ·  Euripides, Hippolytus 516

II. Of persons: "anointed"  ·  IIa. Anointed priests: LXX Leviticus 4:3, 4:5, 4:16, 6:22  ·  IIb. Kings of Israel — ὁ χ. κυρίου ("the Lord's anointed"): LXX 1 Samuel 24:7; LXX Psalm 17:51  ·  IIc. Plural: χριστοί σου: LXX Habakkuk 3:13

III. In NT: ὁ χ. = the Messiah, translating Heb. מָשִׁיחַ  ·  Matthew 2:4 (title, with article); Matthew 1:1 (proper name, without article)

Etymology

χρίω ("to anoint, smear") + verbal adjective suffix -τός → χριστός, "that which is/has been/is to be anointed/smeared." The Hebrew structural parallel is מָשַׁח (mašaḥ, "to anoint") → מָשִׁיחַ (māšîaḥ, "anointed one"). Both yield verbal adjectives with identical semantic range. The LXX chose χριστός as a structural calque, not a loanword.

Occurrence Catalog

Occurrence 1  Classical

Aeschylus — Prometheus Vinctus

Author / Work / Date
Aeschylus (Αἰσχύλος) · Prometheus Vinctus (Προμηθεὺς Δεσμώτης) · c. 460–456 BCE · Authorship disputed (West 1979; Griffith 1983)
TLG Canon / Perseus
0085.003  ·  Perseus:text:1999.01.0010
Greek Text (lines 478–483)

φάρμακα δ' ἐξηῦρον πολλὰ τῶν νοσημάτων

χριστά τε καὶ πιστά, ὡς ἀπαλλάξω νόσους.

Translation
"…and I discovered many remedies for diseases — smearable [ones] and drinkable [ones] — so that I might free [them] from illnesses."
Word Form
χριστά — neuter plural nominative (predicative adjective) · χριστά = topical/salve applications vs. πιστά = oral/drinkable medicines
Meaning in Context
Literal/pharmacological adjective: "to be smeared on, applicable as ointment." No religious or ritual dimension whatever. The word describes physical application of a substance in Prometheus's catalogue of gifts to humanity.
Lexicon Note
LSJ I (of things): "to be rubbed on, used as ointment or salve, opp. πιστός, Aesch. Pr. 480." This is the earliest attested use in the extant corpus. The form belongs entirely to medical/pharmaceutical vocabulary.
Occurrence 2  Classical

Euripides — Hippolytus

Author / Work / Date
Euripides (Εὐριπίδης) · Hippolytus (Ἱππόλυτος) · 428 BCE (first performance, securely dated)
TLG Canon / Perseus
0006.005  ·  Perseus:text:1999.01.0105
Greek Text (lines 513–516)

ΤΡΟΦΟΣ: ἔχω τι τούτων φάρμακον παυστήριον.

ΦΑΙΔΡΑ: χριστὸν ἢ ποτόν;

ΤΡΟΦΟΣ: οὐκ οἶδ'· εὐτυχεῖν βούλου, τέκνον.

[Full line 516: πότερα δὲ χριστὸν ἢ ποτὸν τὸ φάρμακον;]

Translation
NURSE: "I have some remedy that will stop this." PHAEDRA: "Is the drug a salve [to be smeared] or a potion [to be drunk]?" NURSE: "I don't know — just wish to be happy, child."
Word Form
χριστόν — neuter singular accusative (predicative adjective / substantivized: "a smearable thing")
Meaning in Context
Literal/pharmacological: Phaedra asks whether the Nurse's love-charm is applied externally (a salve) or taken internally (a potion). The word contrasts application methods. No religious resonance.
Lexicon Note
LSJ I cites Eur. Hipp. 516 alongside Aesch. Pr. 480 as the two Classical witnesses to χριστός as a pharmaceutical adjective. The opposition χριστόν / ποτόν makes the topical vs. internal contrast explicit.
Occurrence 3  Classical

Hippocrates — De ulceribus and Corpus

Author / Work / Date
Hippocratic Corpus (Ἱπποκράτης) · De ulceribus (Περὶ Ἑλκῶν) and related surgical treatises · c. 420–380 BCE
TLG Canon / Perseus
0627.007  ·  Perseus:text:1999.01.0248
Greek Text (De ulceribus 18)

τοῖσι δὲ ἐν θέρει μὲν καὶ χειμῶνι, ἐλαίῳ χρηστέον ἐπὶ τὰ ἕλκεα ὅσα δεῖται τοιούτων φαρμάκων.

Translation
"In both summer and winter one must apply oil to those ulcers that require such medicines."
Word Form / Morphological Note
Correction: χρηστέον in De ulceribus 18 is from χράομαι ("to use"), not χρίω ("to anoint"). Genuine χριστά forms (from χρίω) do appear in the Hippocratic corpus in surgical/pharmacological treatises (χριστά φάρμακα = "topical medications") but the specific passage quoted uses a distinct root. Full TLG credentials needed to retrieve the correct passage. The Hippocratic corpus is the primary prose locus of the pharmaceutical χριστά vs. πιστά distinction.
Meaning in Context
Purely pharmacological. The paradigmatic opposition of χριστά (topical) vs. πιστά / ποτά (drinkable) in the Hippocratic tradition is the primary technical medical vocabulary of this period.
Occurrence 4  Hellenistic

LXX — Leviticus (Priestly Title)

Work / Date
Septuagint (LXX), Leuitikon · Translation c. 250–150 BCE (Pentateuch among earliest) · TLG 0527 · Rahlfs-Hanhart LXX
Greek Texts

Lev 4:3: ἐὰν ὁ ἀρχιερεὺς ὁ κεχρισμένος ἁμάρτῃ τοῦ τὸν λαὸν ἁμαρτεῖν…

Lev 4:5: καὶ λήψεται ὁ ἱερεὺς ὁ χριστὸς ὁ τετελειωμένος τὰς χεῖρας

Lev 6:22: ὁ ἱερεὺς ὁ χριστὸς ἀντ' αὐτοῦ ἐκ τῶν υἱῶν αὐτοῦ ποιήσει αὐτήν

Translation
Lev 4:3: "…if the anointed high priest sins so as to bring guilt on the people…" · Lev 4:5: "…and the anointed priest who has been ordained shall take…" · Lev 6:22: "…the anointed priest who succeeds him from among his sons shall perform it…"
Word Form
χριστός — masc. nom. sing. (attributive adjective + article: ὁ χριστός = "the anointed one"). Note: Lev 4:3 uses the perfect participle κεχρισμένος ("having been anointed") while 4:5, 4:16, 6:22 use the adjective χριστός — both render Hebrew הַמָּשִׁיחַ.
Meaning in Context
Jewish ritual/institutional title: the anointed (high) priest consecrated by ritual oil application (Exod 29:7; Lev 8:12). Designates consecrated status, not a future eschatological figure. Translation mechanism: structural calque — Hebrew הַכֹּהֵן הַמָּשִׁיחַ → Greek ὁ ἱερεὺς ὁ χριστός (parallel morphology: māšîaḥ = qal passive ptcp. ≈ χριστός = verbal adjective, both from "to anoint").
Lexicon Note
LSJ IIa: "anointed [as priestly title]… LXX Lev 4.5, 4.16, 6.22." First use of χριστός as an institutional/religious title in the Greek corpus.
Occurrence 5  Hellenistic

LXX — 1 Samuel (Royal Title)

Work / Date
Septuagint (LXX), A' Basileion (1 Samuel) · Translation c. 200–150 BCE · TLG 0527 · Rahlfs-Hanhart LXX
Greek Texts

1 Sam 2:10 (Hannah's Song):

κύριος δώσει ἰσχὺν τοῖς βασιλεῦσιν ἡμῶν καὶ ὑψώσει κέρας χριστοῦ αὐτοῦ.

1 Sam 24:7:

τῷ χριστῷ κυρίου ἐπεκτεῖναι χεῖρά μου ἐπ' αὐτόν, ὅτι χριστὸς κυρίου ἐστὶν οὗτος.

Translation
1 Sam 2:10: "…the LORD will give strength to our kings and will exalt the horn of his anointed [one]." · 1 Sam 24:7: "…to the LORD's anointed, to stretch out my hand against him, for he is the LORD's anointed."
Word Form
1 Sam 2:10 — χριστοῦ αὐτοῦ (gen. sing., "of his anointed") · 1 Sam 24:7 — τῷ χριστῷ κυρίου (dat. sing.) + χριστὸς κυρίου ἐστίν (nom. predicate)
Meaning in Context
Jewish royal title: the king of Israel as Yahweh's anointed representative. At 1 Sam 2:10 (pre-monarchic context), the reference is PROSPECTIVE — arguably the earliest LXX use with any forward-looking force. At 24:7, Saul is the χριστὸς κυρίου; David refuses to harm him because of this status. The formula χριστὸς κυρίου is paradigmatic for the royal stage of the word's history.
Lexicon Note
LSJ IIb: "Kings of Israel: 1 K[ings] 24.7, Ps. 17.51." The anointing of Israelite kings (cf. 1 Sam 16) provides the literal basis; χριστός here denotes legitimate royal status conferred by divine authorization through ritual.
Occurrence 6  Hellenistic

LXX — Psalms (Royal & Proto-Messianic)

Work / Date
Septuagint (LXX), Psalmoi · Translation c. 200–100 BCE · TLG 0527 · Rahlfs-Hanhart LXX
Key Occurrences

Ps 2:2 (LXX) — most-cited messianic psalm:

κατὰ τοῦ κυρίου καὶ κατὰ τοῦ χριστοῦ αὐτοῦ.

Ps 17:51 (LXX = MT 18:50):

ποιῶν ἔλεος τῷ χριστῷ αὐτοῦ, τῷ Δαυὶδ καὶ τῷ σπέρματι αὐτοῦ ἕως αἰῶνος.

Ps 88:38; 131:10; 131:17 (additional attestations)

Translation
Ps 2:2: "…against the LORD and against his anointed." · Ps 17:51: "…showing loving-kindness to his anointed — to David and to his seed forever." · Ps 88:38: "…you have spurned your anointed." · Ps 131:17: "I have prepared a lamp for my anointed."
Meaning in Context
Dual register: royal (Davidic king) and proto-messianic. Ps 2:2 is the most theologically charged — later applied messianically in NT (Acts 4:26) and in early Jewish apocalypticism (Psalms of Solomon 17). The Psalter contains more occurrences of χριστός than any other single LXX book; these passages form the primary scriptural basis for Jewish and early Christian messianic interpretation.
Lexicon Note
LSJ IIb explicitly cites Ps. 17.51 (= MT 18.50). Multiple cases attested: χριστοῦ αὐτοῦ (gen., Ps 2:2) · χριστῷ αὐτοῦ (dat., Ps 17:51) · τὸν χριστόν σου (acc., Ps 88:38) · τῷ χριστῷ μου (dat., Ps 131:17).
Occurrence 7  Hellenistic

LXX — Isaiah 45:1 (Cyrus as χριστός)

Work / Date
Septuagint (LXX), Esaias · Translation c. 200–150 BCE · TLG 0527 · Rahlfs-Hanhart LXX
Greek Text

Οὕτως λέγει κύριος ὁ θεὸς τῷ χριστῷ μου Κύρῳ, οὗ ἐκράτησα τῆς δεξιᾶς ἐπακοῦσαι ἔμπροσθεν αὐτοῦ ἔθνη…

Translation
"Thus says the Lord God to my anointed, Cyrus — whose right hand I have grasped — to subdue nations before him…"
Word Form
τῷ χριστῷ μου — dat. sing. masc. (indirect object + possessive genitive: "to my anointed [one]")
Meaning in Context
Non-Jewish royal anointing: uniquely, the LXX applies χριστός to Cyrus II of Persia (c. 600–530 BCE) as God's instrument of deliverance. This is the ONLY instance in the Hebrew Bible/LXX where a non-Israelite is called māšîaḥ/χριστός. Theologically significant because it decouples the title from Davidic descent — χριστός = "divinely authorized agent" rather than ethnic insider.
Lexicon Note
This occurrence stretches the royal/priestly category of LSJ IIb. It proves the semantic category is "divinely commissioned agent anointed for a mission" rather than simply "Israelite king."
Occurrence 8  Hellenistic

LXX — Daniel 9:25–26 (Proto-Messianic, Theodotion)

Work / Date / Version
Septuagint (LXX) + Theodotion revision · Danihel · LXX translation c. 100 BCE; Theodotion revision c. 2nd century CE · TLG 0527 · Rahlfs-Hanhart LXX
Greek Texts

Dan 9:25 (Theodotion):

ἕως χριστοῦ ἡγουμένου ἑβδομάδες ἑπτά

Dan 9:26 — Textual Variant:

LXX: ἐξολοθρευθήσεται χρῖσμα ("an anointing will be cut off")  |  Theodotion: ἐξολοθρευθήσεται χριστός ("an anointed one will be cut off")

Translation
Dan 9:25 (Th): "…from the going out of a word to restore and build Jerusalem until an anointed leader, [there shall be] seven weeks." · Dan 9:26 (Th): "…and after the sixty-two weeks an anointed one will be cut off and will have nothing."
Word Form
Dan 9:25 — χριστοῦ ἡγουμένου (gen. sing. — ANARTHROUS, no article) · Dan 9:26 (Th) — χριστός (nom. sing., subject)
Meaning in Context
Eschatological/proto-messianic: Daniel 9's "seventy weeks" prophecy uses χριστός for a FUTURE anointed leader set within a numerical schema. This text becomes foundational for Second Temple messianic chronology (Dead Sea Scrolls) and early Christianity. The variant χρῖσμα/χριστός at 9:26 shows LXX translators navigating the semantic boundary between "anointing" (abstract noun) and "anointed one" (person).
Lexicon Note
The Theodotion reading with χριστός (not the LXX χρῖσμα) is the version cited in NT and patristic exegesis. Anarthrous χριστοῦ at 9:25 signals proto-name status — the article is beginning to drop, pointing toward proper-name formation.
Occurrence 9  Hellenistic

LXX — Habakkuk 3:13 (Plural)

Work / Date
Septuagint (LXX), Ambakoum · Translation c. 200–150 BCE · TLG 0527 · Rahlfs-Hanhart LXX
Greek Text

ἐξήλθες εἰς σωτηρίαν λαοῦ σου τοῦ σῷσαι τοὺς χριστούς σου·

Translation / Word Form
"You went out for the salvation of your people, to save your anointed ones." · τοὺς χριστούς σου — masc. acc. PLURAL ("your anointed ones"). This is the only LXX occurrence of χριστός in the plural noted in LSJ.
Meaning in Context
Plural royal/ritual title: "your anointed ones" refers collectively to the line of Davidic kings or to Israel as God's anointed nation. The plural χριστοί is extremely rare. Note: by the time of Christian usage χριστός has been restricted to a singular title for Jesus — the NT NEVER uses plural χριστοί in this collective sense.
Occurrence 10  Hellenistic

LXX — Lamentations 4:20 (Royal Elegiac)

Work / Date
Septuagint (LXX), Threni Ieremiae · Translation c. 200–150 BCE · TLG 0527 · Rahlfs-Hanhart LXX
Greek Text

πνεῦμα προσώπου ἡμῶν χριστὸς κυρίου συνελήμφθη ἐν ταῖς διαφθοραῖς αὐτῶν…

Translation
"The breath of our nostrils, the LORD's anointed, was caught in their traps — he of whom we had said, 'Under his shadow we shall live among the nations.'"
Meaning in Context
Royal elegiac title: the Babylonian capture of King Zedekiah (586 BCE). χριστὸς κυρίου here is a royal title mourned in the context of national catastrophe. The formula χριστὸς κυρίου (nom.) recurs with little variation across Samuel, Psalms, and Lamentations. Early Christian readers applied this to Christ's passion.
Lexicon Note
Standard LSJ IIb type. The formula χριστὸς κυρίου (always with possessive genitive κυρίου) is the characteristic LXX construction for the royal anointed one.
Occurrence 11  Roman

Josephus — Antiquitates Judaicae 18.63–64 (Testimonium Flavianum)

Author / Work / Date
Flavius Josephus (Φλάουιος Ἰώσηπος) · Antiquitates Judaicae Book 18 · c. 93–94 CE · TLG 0526.001 · Niese edition
Greek Text (excerpt)

…ὁ χριστὸς οὗτος ἦν… εἰς ἔτι τε νῦν τῶν Χριστιανῶν ἀπὸ τοῦδε ὠνομασμένον οὐκ ἐπέλιπε τὸ φῦλον.

Translation
"…He was the Christ… and to this day the tribe of Christians, named after him, has not disappeared."
Word Form
ὁ χριστὸς οὗτος ἦν — nom. sing. predicate ("he was the Christ") · Χριστιανῶν — gen. pl. (derived proper noun, "Christians")
Meaning in Context / Authenticity
The Testimonium Flavianum is debated. Most scholars accept a Josephan core with Christian interpolation/embellishment. The phrase ὁ χριστὸς οὗτος ἦν in its current form reflects Christian usage. The derived noun Χριστιανοί ("Christians") confirms wider Roman-era currency of the title as a proper name. Any reference to a "reconstructed neutral original" should be labeled as modern scholarly speculation (Meier, Crossan, Whealey); no surviving neutral Greek MS exists.
Occurrence 12  Roman

Josephus — Antiquitates Judaicae 20.200 (James Passage)

Author / Work / Date
Flavius Josephus · Antiquitates Judaicae Book 20 · c. 93–94 CE · TLG 0526.001 · Niese edition
Greek Text

…τὸν ἀδελφὸν Ἰησοῦ τοῦ λεγομένου Χριστοῦ, Ἰάκωβος ὄνομα αὐτῷ…

Translation
"…the brother of Jesus who was called Christ, whose name was James…"
Word Form / Authenticity
τοῦ λεγομένου Χριστοῦ — gen. sing. masc. (participial attributive clause: "the one so-called Christ"). Widely regarded as authentic Josephus (no Christian interpolation suspected). The distancing formula λεγόμενος ("so-called") signals Josephus reports others' usage without endorsing the title — but presupposes his readers KNOW what "Christ" designates. This is among the most reliable non-Christian attestations of Χριστός as a proper name/title by c. 94 CE.
Lexicon Note
The formula ὁ λεγόμενος Χριστός recurs at Matthew 27:17, 22 in the same distancing construction. Both uses confirm Χριστός had become a recognized distinctive epithet by this period.
Occurrence 13  New Testament

Matthew — Title and Proper Name

Author / Work / Date
Matthew (Ματθαῖος) — attributed · Gospel of Matthew (Κατὰ Ματθαῖον) · c. 80–90 CE · TLG Canon 0031.001 · NA28
Key Passages

1:1 (proper name, no article):

Βίβλος γενέσεως Ἰησοῦ Χριστοῦ, υἱοῦ Δαυίδ, υἱοῦ Ἀβραάμ.

2:4 (title with article):

ἐπυνθάνετο παρ' αὐτῶν ποῦ ὁ Χριστὸς γεννᾶται.

16:16 (Peter's confession):

Σὺ εἶ ὁ Χριστὸς ὁ υἱὸς τοῦ θεοῦ τοῦ ζῶντος.

Translation
1:1: "The book of the genealogy of Jesus Christ, son of David, son of Abraham." · 2:4: "…he inquired of them where the Christ was to be born." · 16:16: "You are the Christ, the Son of the living God."
Meaning in Context
Christian theological title/proper name. In Matthew, Χριστός functions both as article-free proper name (Ἰησοῦς Χριστός) and as a definite title requiring the article (ὁ Χριστός). Matthew also contains ψευδόχριστοι (24:24 — "false Christs"), proving the title is sufficiently technical that a category of false claimants is conceivable. Matthew uses Χριστός approximately 16 times.
Lexicon Note
LSJ III: "ὁ χ. the Messiah (Hebr. מָשִׁיחַ), LXX, NT, etc." With article = definite title "the Christ/Messiah"; without = proper name. Paul makes this shift fully explicit.
Occurrence 14  New Testament

Paul — 1 Corinthians (Earliest Dateable Christian Documents)

Author / Work / Date
Paul of Tarsus (Παῦλος) · First Letter to the Corinthians · c. 53–55 CE (earliest surviving datable Christian document) · TLG Canon 0031.006 · NA28
Key Passages

1:23–24:

ἡμεῖς δὲ κηρύσσομεν Χριστὸν ἐσταυρωμένον, Ἰουδαίοις μὲν σκάνδαλον, ἔθνεσιν δὲ μωρίαν…

15:3–4 (pre-Pauline creedal formula):

Χριστὸς ἀπέθανεν ὑπὲρ τῶν ἁμαρτιῶν ἡμῶν κατὰ τὰς γραφάς, καὶ ὅτι ἐτάφη, καὶ ὅτι ἐγήγερται τῇ ἡμέρᾳ τῇ τρίτῃ κατὰ τὰς γραφάς.

Translation
1:23–24: "…but we preach Christ crucified — a stumbling block to Jews and foolishness to Gentiles…" · 15:3–4: "Christ died for our sins according to the scriptures, and that he was buried, and that he was raised on the third day according to the scriptures."
Meaning in Context
Christian theological title fully absorbed as proper name. In Paul, Χριστός has largely shed its article and functions as a proper name. The pre-Pauline creedal formula at 15:3–4 is among the earliest dateable confessional texts in Christianity (tradition received by Paul c. 35–40 CE, inferred from "παρέδωκα…ὃ παρέλαβον" — Dunn, Theology of Paul, 1998, pp.235–238). Paul uses Χριστός approximately 65 times in 1 Corinthians alone.
Lexicon Note
The shift from title (ὁ Χριστός) to proper name (Χριστός without article) is first fully systematic in Paul. Paul explicitly acknowledges the resemanticization: "Christ crucified" is a σκάνδαλον — a scandal relative to messianic expectation, because crucifixion ≠ expected messianic victory.
Occurrence 15  New Testament

John — Bilingual Gloss and Gospel Purpose

Author / Work / Date
John (Ἰωάννης) — attributed · Gospel of John (Κατὰ Ἰωάννην) · c. 90–100 CE · TLG Canon 0031.004 · NA28
Key Passages

1:41 — ONLY NT bilingual gloss:

Εὑρήκαμεν τὸν Μεσσίαν, ὅ ἐστιν μεθερμηνευόμενον ὁ Χριστός.

20:31 — Stated purpose of the Gospel:

ταῦτα δὲ γέγραπται ἵνα πιστεύ[σ]ητε ὅτι Ἰησοῦς ἐστιν ὁ Χριστὸς ὁ υἱὸς τοῦ θεοῦ.

Translation
1:41: "We have found the Messiah" (which is translated, the Christ)." · 20:31: "…these things are written so that you may believe that Jesus is the Christ, the Son of God."
Meaning in Context
Christian theological title. John 1:41 is the NT's only explicit Greek–Semitic bilingual gloss: "Μεσσίας = ὁ Χριστός." The metalinguistic marker μεθερμηνευόμενον ("which is translated") establishes the word's translation status. John 4:25 provides a second bilingual gloss in the voice of the Samaritan woman: Μεσσίας ἔρχεται ὁ λεγόμενος Χριστός. John 20:31 states the Gospel's organizing theological claim explicitly: "Jesus = the Christ."
Lexicon Note
John 1:41 provides what no other NT passage does: a translation note equating Μεσσίας (transliterated Aramaic מְשִׁיחָא) with ὁ Χριστός (Greek calque). By c. 100 CE the Greek title had become fully conventional and required explanation only for non-Aramaic audiences.
