I. The Name — Ἰάσων and Ἰησοῦς

The core observation is simple and philologically grounded: Jason (Ἰάσων) is another name for Jesus (Ἰησοῦς). Not metaphorically. Etymologically, functionally, and historically documented.

Ἰησοῦς — Jesus

יֵשׁוּעַ (Yeshua) Hebrew/Aramaic root: יָשַׁע (yasha') = "to save, to deliver"
Meaning: "God is salvation" or "the one who saves/delivers"
Greek rendering of the Hebrew name.

Ἰάσων — Jason

ἰάομαι (iaomai) Greek root: ἰάομαι = "to heal"
Meaning: "the healer"
Chosen by Greek-speaking Jews as the functional equivalent of Yeshua.

Two different etymological roots — Hebrew yasha' (save) and Greek iaomai (heal) — but the same semantic field: the one who restores those who are lost, broken, or dying.

The Historical Document of the Equivalence — 2 Maccabees 4:7 (LXX, TLG 0527.001):
"When Seleucus died and Antiochus called Epiphanes succeeded to the kingdom, Jason the brother of Onias (Ἰάσων δὲ ὁ ἀδελφὸς Ὀνίου) obtained the high priesthood by corruption."

Jason of 2 Maccabees was a Jewish high priest (~175 BCE) who Hellenized Jerusalem and adopted a Greek name. His Hebrew name was Joshua (Yeshua) — the same name as Jesus. He took the Greek name Jason as the functional equivalent of Yeshua. The Hellenizing Jewish world chose Ἰάσων as the Greek form of Ἰησοῦς / Yeshua. This is documented history, not speculation.

Paul's associate in Thessalonica (Acts 17:5–9) is also named Jason (Ἰάσων) — a Diaspora Jew with a Greek name that is the Hellenized form of Yeshua/Joshua. Romans 16:21: "Jason, my kinsman." The name floats freely between Hebrew Yeshua and Greek Jason in the 2nd century BCE through 1st century CE Jewish world. They are the same name in different linguistic registers.

II. The Figure — Jason as Proto-Jesus

Given the name identity, structural parallels between the Argonautic hero and the Gospel figure deserve systematic comparison. Primary source: Apollonius Rhodius, Argonautica (~3rd c. BCE; TLG 0001.001) — widely read in the Hellenized Jewish world as part of standard Greek literary education (paideia).

1 The Gathering of Disciples
Jason / Argonautica Jason assembles the Argonauts — exceptional men, specifically called and chosen, who follow him on a divine mission to recover a sacred object. Arg. 1.23–227 lists each Argonaut individually.
Jesus / Gospels Jesus assembles twelve disciples — ordinary men, specifically called, who follow him on a divine mission. Each disciple's calling is individually recorded in the Synoptics.
2 The Divine Mission and Sacred Object
Jason / Argonautica The Golden Fleece (χρυσόμαλλον δέρας): a divine object in a distant land, guarded by supernatural forces, whose recovery vindicates Jason's claim to his father's kingdom.
Jesus / Gospels The Kingdom of God (βασιλεία τοῦ θεοῦ): a divine reality to be inaugurated, held back by religious and political opposition, whose arrival vindicates the children of God's inheritance.
3 Passage Through Dangerous Waters — and the Dove
Jason / Argonautica 2.536 The Argonauts pass through the Symplegades (Clashing Rocks) — the threshold between the known and unknown worlds. A divine dove guides them through. The rocks crash but only clip the tail-feathers.
Jesus / Mark 1:9–11 Jesus passes through the Jordan River at baptism — historical threshold to the Promised Land. The Spirit descends "like a dove" (ὡς περιστεράν) immediately after Jesus rises from the death-water.

In both narratives, a divine bird guides the hero through a death-threshold. In Argonautica 2.536 the dove flies ahead through rocks that crush; in Mark 1:10 the dove descends after Jesus rises from submersion. Both threshold passages involve divine guidance and a transformation of the hero.

4 The Hero's Passivity and the Woman's Agency
Jason / Argonautica Standard critique: Jason succeeds largely because Medea does the actual work. She provides the pharmaka, navigates the ritual, breaks the dragon's sleep, betrays her father. The hero is the vehicle; the woman is the agent.
Jesus / Gospels Jesus is often passive in the anointing narratives — anointed by women (Mary of Bethany; the sinful woman in Luke 7; the spice-bearers at the tomb). The women provide the pharmacological agency; the male hero receives it.
5 The Betrayal and Abandonment
Jason / Euripides Medea Jason abandons Medea for Glauce — trades the woman who gave him everything for a politically advantageous marriage. The male companion betrays; the female witness remains.
Jesus / Mark 14:50; 15:40 "They all left him and fled" (καὶ ἀφέντες αὐτὸν ἔφυγον πάντες). The women remain (Mark 15:40–41). Mary Magdalene is at the cross and at the tomb.
6 The Resurrection Figure
Medea in the Myth Medea performs resurrection: she rejuvenates the aged Aeson (Jason's father) with a magical herb-potion; she knows how to bring back the dead and how to send to death. She is the resurrection-figure of the myth (Ovid Met. 7.159–293; Diodorus 4.52).
Mary Magdalene in the Gospels First to see the risen Jesus (John 20:14–18). Comes to the tomb with spices (ἀρώματα — pharmacological aromatic substances) for anointing (Mark 16:1). She is the resurrection-witness of the Gospels.

Both women carry pharmacological knowledge toward a dead or dying male figure and are the agents of the death/resurrection transition.

III. Medea as Proto-Magdalene

The structural parallel maps across five independent axes:

Axis 1 — Foreign Woman / Marginal Geography

MedeaFrom Colchis (eastern Black Sea) — a barbarian from the margins of the Greek world. "Other" in the Hellenistic imagination. Her name marks her Colchian/Median identity.
Mary MagdaleneFrom Magdala (Migdal), Sea of Galilee — "Galilee of the Gentiles" (Matt 4:15). Her name marks her geographic identity. The Galilean margins = Greek imagination's "other."

Axis 2 — Pharmacological Knowledge

MedeaHer defining characteristic: mastery of pharmaka, taught by Hecate. She anoints Jason (Arg. 3.844–869). She heals and kills with substances. Drug-knowledge is her identity.
Mary MagdaleneComes to the tomb with anointing spices: ἀγοράσωσιν ἀρώματα ἵνα… ἀλείψωσιν αὐτόν (Mark 16:1). In Luke 7:37–50, anoints Jesus with myrrh/spikenard — pharmacologically active, physical, intimate.

Axis 3 — Devotion to the Hero / Rejection by the Tradition

MedeaSacrifices homeland, family, and father for Jason. Jason abandons her. Euripides transforms the devoted helper into a terrifying villain — making her the monster so Jason's failure is obscured.
Mary MagdaleneMost prominent female follower. First resurrection witness (John 20). Then: reduced, conflated with the sinful woman of Luke 7. Pope Gregory I (591 CE) declares her a reformed prostitute — no Gospel basis. The woman who held the key is demoted to penitent sinner.

Axis 4 — The Resurrection Knowledge

MedeaKnows how to resurrect the dead (Aeson's rejuvenation; the Pelias trick). She is the keeper of resurrection pharmacology. What looks like death is reversed in her presence.
Mary MagdaleneFirst witness to the resurrection. In John 20:11–18, she mistakes Jesus for "the gardener" (κηπουρόν) — possibly an echo of the rhizotomos / root-cutter / pharmacological practitioner role. What looks like death is reversed in her presence.

Axis 5 — The Hecate / Magdalene Parallel

MedeaHecate's priestess. Hecate = goddess of crossroads, underworld, pharmaka, and liminal spaces between life and death. Medea operates at life/death boundaries.
Mary MagdaleneDelivered of "seven demons" (Luke 8:2) — lived at the boundary between possessed/freed, death-in-life/life. Liminal figure like Hecate's devotees. Magdala (migdal = tower) = possible liminal watchtower / crossing-place.

IV. The Literary Resurrection Hypothesis

The proposal is not that the Gospels plagiarized the Argonautica. The proposal is more interesting: both texts draw on a common deep mythological grammar of the Mediterranean world, and the Greek literary tradition offered a ready-made template — a hero named "the Healer/Savior" guided by a pharmacological woman through death and back — which was available to the Gospel authors and their audience.

The Literary Resurrection — How the Tradition Would Have Worked

1
The Jason myth was widely known in the educated Hellenized Jewish world. The Argonautica was part of the standard literary curriculum (paideia). Educated Jews in Alexandria, Antioch, and Hellenized Palestine read it.
2
The name Jason = Yeshua / Jesus was a recognized equivalence (2 Macc 4:7). A Hellenized Jew reading the Argonautica and hearing about a hero named Ἰάσων would experience the same name-recognition as a Christian hearing Ἰησοῦς — the healer/savior.
3
The Argonautic structure — gathering of followers, divine mission, dangerous journey, woman with pharmaka, death-threshold, recovery of sacred object — was a pre-existing narrative template in the cultural imagination.
4
The Gospel authors (writing in Greek for Greek-speaking audiences, 40–100 CE) worked within this shared cultural imagination. Whether deliberately or by absorption, the Jason/Medea template shapes the Jesus/Magdalene narrative.
5
The result: a Literary Resurrection — the ancient mythological hero is reborn in the Gospel figure, carrying the structural inheritance of the Argonautic tradition into a new theological context.
The most elegant version of the argument:
Jason is Jesus's literary ancestor.
Medea is Mary Magdalene's literary ancestor.
The Argonautica is the proto-Gospel the Greek world had already written, four centuries before the historical Jesus.
When Jesus lived and died and was proclaimed risen, his story was told in the only narrative grammar available: the grammar of the hero-named-healer, guided by the woman-with-pharmaka, through the waters of death and back.

V. Specific Textual Parallels for Future Research

1The Dove at the Threshold
A divine bird guides the hero through a death-threshold at the start of the mission. Research direction: examine περιστερά (dove) in LXX and Hellenistic religious contexts; what is the dove's role in mystery cult imagery? Arg. 2.536: dove through Symplegades · Mark 1:10: dove at Jordan baptism
2The Golden Fleece and the Kingdom of God
Both are divine objects/realities belonging to the hero's heritage, held by hostile forces, recovered at the end of the journey. The Fleece is golden (χρυσόν) — compare the Transfiguration: "his face shone like the sun" (Matt 17:2). Arg. 1–4 · Matt 17:2; Kingdom sayings throughout Synoptics
3The Woman's Anointing and the Hero's Invulnerability
Medea anoints Jason with the Promethean drug (Arg. 3.844–869): makes him invulnerable to fire and iron for one day. Mary of Bethany anoints Jesus with spikenard (Mark 14:3–9): Jesus interprets this as his burial preparation — a pharmacological preparation for death that paradoxically enables his resurrection. In both: the woman's anointing (χρίω) makes the hero capable of passing through death. Arg. 3.844–869 · Mark 14:3–9
4The Garden and the Dragon
The Golden Fleece is guarded by a never-sleeping dragon in a sacred grove (Arg. 4.127–166); Medea drugs the dragon to sleep. Gethsemane (κῆπος — garden) precedes the death-ordeal; Judas's betrayal = the serpent's move. Note also: the Garden of Eden (serpent + sacred tree), the Fleece-grove (dragon-serpent + sacred object), and Gethsemane (garden + death) form a continuous pattern. Arg. 4.127–166 · Mark 14:32–50 · Gen 3
5The Betrayal Within the Inner Circle
Jason's mission is nearly undone by internal dynamics — Medea's betrayal of her father and brother; the Argonauts' internal disputes. Jesus's mission is undone by Judas — one of the twelve, from inside. Both narratives hinge on betrayal from within as the mechanism of the hero's greatest test. Arg. 4.303–481 · Mark 14:10–11, 43–46
6The Trial Before a King
Jason must perform impossible tasks set by King Aeetes (Arg. 3.400–421) before receiving the Fleece: yoke fire-breathing bulls, sow dragon's teeth, defeat armed men. Jesus undergoes trial before Pilate (governor/king): the crowd demands Barabbas; Pilate's verdict is the mechanism of death. Both: the hero faces a king whose judgment sends him toward death. Arg. 3.400–421 · Mark 15:1–15
7Medea's Exile / Mary Magdalene's Marginalization
After Jason's abandonment, Medea flies away in her dragon-chariot — exiled, terrifying, she disappears from the narrative. After the resurrection appearance in John 20:11–18, Mary Magdalene delivers the message ("I have seen the Lord") and is not heard from again in canonical Gospels. In later tradition she is sent to Provence or Ephesus — exiled to the margins. In both cases: after their central role in the death/resurrection moment, the women are exiled from the main narrative. The tradition cannot hold them. Euripides Medea 1316–1420 · John 20:18
