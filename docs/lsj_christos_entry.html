Headword
χριστός, -ή, -όν
verbal adjective of χρίω ("to anoint, smear, rub with oil or fat")
Etymology: χρίω + -τός (verbal adjective suffix indicating "capable of / subject to" the action of the verb) → χριστός: "that which is / has been / is to be anointed or smeared on"
lsj.gr entry  ·  Perseus LSJ

I. Of Things: "to be rubbed on, used as ointment or salve"

Opposed to πιστός / ποτός ("drinkable"). The primary Classical usage is pharmaceutical, modifying things not persons.

Attestation 1  ·  Classical

Aeschylus, Prometheus Vinctus 480

Greek Text
φάρμακα … χριστά τε καὶ πιστά
Translation
"medicines … smearable and drinkable [which free men from illnesses]"
Word Form
χριστά — neuter plural nominative (adjective)
Context
Prometheus enumerating gifts to humankind: knowledge of topical vs. oral medicaments. χριστά = salves / topical applications; πιστά = potions / oral medicines.
Authorship Note
M.L. West (JHS 99, 1979) and M. Griffith (Cambridge, 1983) dispute Aeschylean authorship; possible post-Aeschylean imitation (c.5th–4th century BCE). LSJ citation remains valid as corpus citation.
Attestation 2  ·  Classical

Euripides, Hippolytus 516

Greek Text
ΝΟΣΟΣ: ἔχω τι τούτων φάρμακον παυστήριον.
ΦΑΙΔΡΑ: χριστὸν ἢ ποτόν;
Full line 516: πότερα δὲ χριστὸν ἢ ποτὸν τὸ φάρμακον;
Translation
NURSE: "I have some remedy that will stop this."
PHAEDRA: "Is the drug a salve [to be smeared] or a potion [to be drunk]?"
Context
Phaedra asks whether the Nurse's love-charm is applied externally (χριστόν — a salve) or taken internally (ποτόν — a potion). Purely technical medical vocabulary; no religious resonance.
Pharmacological Note

The χριστά ≠ πιστά opposition is the standard Classical Greek distinction in medical writing. The Hippocratic corpus uses this contrast throughout surgical and pharmacological treatises to indicate topically-applied medications versus internal medicines.

Note: The Hippocratic form χρηστέον (De ulceribus 18) derives from χράομαι ("to use") not χρίω ("to anoint") — these are distinct roots. Genuine χριστά φάρμακα forms from χρίω appear in the Hippocratic corpus but require full TLG query to retrieve.

II. Of Persons: "anointed"

IIa. Anointed Priests (LXX — Leviticus)

The LXX translators chose χριστός as the morphological equivalent of Hebrew מָשִׁיחַ (māšîaḥ), forming a semantic calque. This is the first use of the word for persons rather than things.

Construction
ὁ ἱερεὺς ὁ χριστός

"the anointed priest" (= Hebrew הַכֹּהֵן הַמָּשִׁיחַ)

Key Texts
Lev 4:5: ὁ ἱερεὺς ὁ χριστὸς ὁ τετελειωμένος τὰς χεῖρας — "the anointed priest who has been ordained"
Lev 4:16, 6:22: ὁ ἱερεὺς ὁ χριστός — "the anointed priest"
Lev 21:10: τὸ ἔλαιον τὸ χριστόν — "the anointing oil" (neuter, bridges Stage I and IIa: still modifying an object but in religious context)
Lev 4:3: ὁ ἀρχιερεὺς ὁ κεχρισμένος — perfect passive participle used alongside χριστός (both render same Hebrew הַמָּשִׁיחַ)
Morphological Status
Attributive adjective in frozen title construction: ὁ ἱερεὺς ὁ χριστός. Still syntactically adjectival.

IIb. Anointed Kings (LXX — Samuel, Psalms, Lamentations)

The title expands from priests to kings: χριστὸς κυρίου ("the LORD's anointed") becomes a stereotyped formula.

Reference Greek Text Translation Notes
1 Sam 2:10 ὑψώσει κέρας χριστοῦ αὐτοῦ "will exalt the horn of his anointed" Hannah's prayer; prospective reference (no king yet exists)
1 Sam 24:7 τῷ χριστῷ κυρίου … χριστὸς κυρίου ἐστὶν "to the LORD's anointed … this is the LORD's anointed" Paradigmatic use: Saul as current χριστὸς κυρίου
Ps 2:2 (LXX) κατὰ τοῦ κυρίου καὶ κατὰ τοῦ χριστοῦ αὐτοῦ "against the LORD and against his anointed" Most-cited LXX messianic verse; applied to Jesus in Acts 4:26
Ps 17:51 (LXX) τῷ χριστῷ αὐτοῦ τῷ Δαυίδ "to his anointed — to David" Explicitly cited in LSJ IIb
Lam 4:20 χριστὸς κυρίου συνελήμφθη "the LORD's anointed was caught" Elegiac: Zedekiah's capture 586 BCE

IIc. Plural Usage

Reference
τοὺς χριστούς σου

LXX Habakkuk 3:13 — "your anointed ones" (collective/corporate)

The only LXX occurrence of χριστός in the masculine accusative plural (as cited in LSJ). The NT singularizes the title exclusively onto Jesus; the plural is never used in Christian texts for Christ.

IId. Non-Israelite Application

LXX Isaiah 45:1 — unique occurrence
Οὕτως λέγει κύριος ὁ θεὸς τῷ χριστῷ μου Κύρῳ

"Thus says the Lord God to my anointed — Cyrus [of Persia]"

The only LXX case where χριστός is applied to a non-Israelite. Decouples the title from Davidic lineage; demonstrates χριστός = "divinely authorized agent" rather than ethnic or dynastic designation.

IIe. Eschatological / Proto-Messianic

LXX/Theodotion Daniel 9:25–26
ἕως χριστοῦ ἡγουμένου ἑβδομάδες ἑπτά

Dan 9:25 (Theodotion): "until an anointed leader, seven weeks"

ἐξολοθρευθήσεται χριστός

Dan 9:26 (Theodotion): "an anointed one will be cut off"

Textual variant: LXX text has χρῖσμα ("an anointing") at 9:26; Theodotion has χριστός ("an anointed one"). The Theodotion reading becomes standard in Christian citation. The variant shows the translators navigating the title/abstract-noun boundary. Foundation of Second Temple Jewish "seventy weeks" messianic chronology.

III. Proper Name / Title in New Testament Greek

LSJ III: "ὁ χ., the Messiah (Hebr. μָשִׁיחַ)… used as a proper name for Jesus: Ἰησοῦς Χρ., Matt. 1.1"

Title Channel (with article ὁ)
ὁ Χριστός

= "the Christ / the Messiah" — definite, titular

Matt 2:4; 16:16; 22:42; Luke 2:26; John 1:20; 7:26–27; Acts 2:36

Name Channel (without article)
Ἰησοῦς Χριστός

= personal name "Jesus Christ" — onomastic

Matt 1:1; Mark 1:1; Rom 1:1; 1 Cor 1:1; Gal 1:1; Phil 1:1

Bilingual Gloss — Key Passage
Εὑρήκαμεν τὸν Μεσσίαν, ὅ ἐστιν μεθερμηνευόμενον ὁ Χριστός

John 1:41: "We have found the Messiah (which is translated: the Christ)"

The only NT passage with an explicit Greek–Aramaic bilingual translation gloss. Μεσσίας (transliterated Aramaic מְשִׁיחָא) = ὁ Χριστός (Greek calque). μεθερμηνευόμενον ("which is translated") is the metalinguistic marker. John 4:25 provides a second bilingual gloss in the voice of the Samaritan woman.

Pauline ἐν Χριστῷ Formula
ἐν Χριστῷ / ἐν Χριστῷ Ἰησοῦ

Locative dative with a proper name (~164 occurrences in Pauline corpus)

The ἐν Χριστῷ formula indicating incorporation into / participation in Christ. Using a locative dative with a person's name is the definitive grammatical marker of onomastic status: you can only be "in" someone if they are a name, not a title.

Semantic Evolution

Classical: χριστός = descriptive adjective (pharmaceutical) — modifies things

LXX: ὁ χριστός = institutional title (priestly, royal) — modifies persons

NT early (Synoptics): ὁ Χριστός = eschatological/messianic title — with article

NT middle (Paul): Χριστός = proper name (anarthrous) — onomastic

NT late (John, Rev): both usages in full

Early Church (Ignatius, c.110 CE): Χριστιανισμός — abstract noun derived from proper name seals onomastic status

Josephus — Non-Christian Attestation

Josephus 1  ·  Roman, c.93–94 CE

Antiquitates Judaicae 20.200  ·  Widely considered authentic

Greek Text
τὸν ἀδελφὸν Ἰησοῦ τοῦ λεγομένου Χριστοῦ, Ἰάκωβος ὄνομα αὐτῷ
Translation
"the brother of Jesus who was called Christ, James by name"
Analysis
τοῦ λεγομένου Χριστοῦ — distancing formula: Josephus reports others' usage rather than affirming the title. λεγόμενος ("so-called / called") signals reporter's neutrality. Josephus assumes his readers already know the designation Χριστός — confirming name-status by c.93 CE.
Josephus 2  ·  Roman, c.93–94 CE  ·  Disputed

Antiquitates Judaicae 18.63–64  ·  Testimonium Flavianum

Greek Text
ὁ χριστὸς οὗτος ἦν
Translation
"He was the Christ" — current form reflects probable Christian embellishment
Authenticity
No surviving Greek manuscript has a "neutral" version. All reconstructions of a hypothetical neutral original (e.g., "he was called the Christ") are modern editorial hypotheses, not attested text (Meier, Marginal Jew vol. 1, pp.56–88).

Etymology and Translation Mechanism

The Calque vs. Loanword Distinction

INACCURATE: Describing χριστός as "borrowed into Hebrew and back into Greek."

CORRECT: The LXX translators employed a semantic calque: they chose Greek χριστός as the morphological equivalent of Hebrew מָשִׁיחַ — both are verbal adjectives of the verb "to anoint" in their respective languages. Hebrew never borrowed the Greek word.

The direction of semantic transfer is: Hebrew → Greek through LXX translation.

Μεσσίας (John 1:41; 4:25) is a separate loanword channel: Aramaic מְשִׁיחָא transliterated directly into Greek (not translated). John 1:41 explicitly marks this distinction: the Aramaic loanword Μεσσίαν is glossed by the Greek calque ὁ Χριστός via μεθερμηνευόμενον ("which is translated").
