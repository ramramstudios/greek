The Messianic Claimants

Jewish Pseudo-Messiahs from the Second Temple Period to the Modern Era

4 BCE – 20th Century  ·  23 Documented Claimants Branch: messiahs

Scope and Method

One major caveat governs this catalogue. For many late Second Temple figures, Josephus does not literally say “this man called himself the Messiah.” More often he describes prophets, would-be kings, or deceivers who promised divine deliverance, miraculous signs, or national liberation. Later Jewish reference works then fold many of those figures into the broader tradition of “pseudo-messiahs.” So for the earliest entries, the evidence is often “messianic pretender” rather than an explicit first-person messiah formula. (Jewish Encyclopedia)

“The recurring ‘tricks’ in the sources are usually not donkey-riding but biblical sign-acts and royal theater: promising to part the Jordan, expecting God to show ‘signs of liberty’ in the wilderness, predicting Jerusalem’s walls would fall, promising a sea crossing, wearing royal garments, or putting on a diadem. That pattern is very clear in Josephus and in later catalogues of Jewish pseudo-messiahs.”

I. Second Temple Period (4 BCE – 135 CE)

1. Judas son of Hezekiah / Ezekias

4 BCE  |  Sepphoris, Galilee  |  Royal pretender

Josephus says he attacked the palace at Sepphoris and acted from “an ambitious desire of the royal dignity.” That is best treated as a royal-messianic pretender, even though Josephus does not give an explicit verbal messiah claim.

Source: Josephus, Antiquities 17.271–272 (Project Gutenberg)

2. Simon of Peraea

4 BCE  |  Peraea  |  Self-crowned king

Josephus says he put a diadem on his head and was declared king by his supporters. This is one of the clearest “acted like the king-messiah” cases in Josephus. The diadem was the definitive symbol of Hellenistic kingship; to assume it was to make a political-theological claim that required no verbal formula.

Source: Josephus, Antiquities 17.273–277 (Project Gutenberg)

3. Athronges

4 BCE  |  Judaea  |  Shepherd-king

Josephus says the shepherd “set up for king,” wore a diadem, assembled a council, and was called king. His background as a shepherd carried deep resonance with the Davidic tradition — David himself was taken from the sheepfold (1 Samuel 16:11; Psalm 78:70–71).

Source: Josephus, Antiquities 17.278–284 (Project Gutenberg)

4. Theudas

c. 44–46 CE  |  Jordan River  |  Prophet / sign-act

Josephus calls him a prophet who persuaded people to follow him to the Jordan, promising that he would divide the river by command. The sign-act explicitly recalls Joshua’s crossing of the Jordan (Joshua 3–4) and Elijah’s parting of the waters (2 Kings 2:8) — both acts that carried eschatological weight in Second Temple expectation. Theudas is also mentioned in Acts 5:36, where Gamaliel cites him as a precedent for movements that rise and fall.

Source: Josephus, Antiquities 20.97–99 (Project Gutenberg)

5. The Egyptian Prophet

50s CE  |  Mount of Olives  |  Prophet / sign-act

Josephus says he pretended to be a prophet, led followers from the wilderness to the Mount of Olives, and intended to seize Jerusalem and dominate the people. The Jewish Encyclopedia summary adds that he promised the city walls would fall at his command — a deliberate recapitulation of Joshua at Jericho (Joshua 6). The choice of the Mount of Olives as staging ground carried its own eschatological charge: Zechariah 14:4 places God’s final advent on that mountain. He is also referenced in Acts 21:38, where Paul is mistaken for him.

Source: Josephus, Jewish War 2.261–263 (Penelope / UChicago)

6. The Unnamed Wilderness Prophet under Festus

60–62 CE  |  Wilderness, Judaea  |  Prophet / deliverer

Josephus says this impostor promised “deliverance and freedom from the miseries” of the people if they followed him into the wilderness. The wilderness setting is itself a sign-act: a second Exodus, a return to the liminal space where God first constituted Israel.

Source: Josephus, Antiquities 20.188 (Lexundria)

7. Menahem ben Judah

66 CE  |  Jerusalem  |  Royal pretender / revolt leader

Jewish tradition and later reference works count him among the pseudo-messiahs. Josephus says he returned to Jerusalem “in the state of a king,” became leader of the revolt, and later went to the Temple in royal garments. His entry to the Temple in royal dress is a deliberate messianic sign-act: to appear in the sacred precinct wearing the garments of kingship was to claim the Davidic throne before God.

Source: Josephus, Jewish War 2.433–448; Jewish Encyclopedia

8. Simon bar Kokhba / bar Kosiba

132–135 CE  |  Judaea  |  Declared “King Messiah”

The clearest major ancient case after Josephus. Rabbi Akiva hailed him as “King Messiah,” applying Numbers 24:17 (“a star shall come out of Jacob”) to him directly — hence the name bar Kokhba, “son of the star.” Not everyone accepted the claim: Rabbi Yohanan ben Torta reportedly replied, “Akiva, grass will grow in your cheeks and the son of David will still not have come” (y. Ta’anit 4:8). Bar Kokhba led a devastating revolt that briefly established an independent Jewish state before Roman reconquest. His coinage bears the legend “Year One of the Redemption of Israel.”

Source: Jerusalem Talmud, Ta’anit 4:8; Cassius Dio 69.12–14; Jewish Encyclopedia

II. Late Antiquity and the Early Medieval Period (5th – 8th Century)

9. Moses of Crete

5th century  |  Crete  |  Prophet / sign-act

He called himself “Moses” and promised to lead Jews through the sea back to the land of Israel — a literal second Exodus. Many followers leapt into the sea and died. The choice of the name Moses and the sea-crossing sign-act places him squarely in the prophetic-deliverer tradition: one who repeats the foundational miracle to inaugurate a new redemption.

Source: Socrates Scholasticus, Church History 7.38; Jewish Encyclopedia

10. Abu ’Isa al-Isfahani

Late 7th century  |  Isfahan, Persia  |  Forerunner / messiah

He claimed to be the last forerunner of the Messiah; some sources say he or his followers went further and treated him as the Messiah himself. He led a military revolt against the Umayyad caliphate and reportedly amassed a significant following among Persian Jews.

Source: Jewish Encyclopedia, s.v. “Abu ’Isa”

11. Yudghan / al-Ra’i

Early 8th century  |  Persia  |  Prophet / messiah

He declared himself a prophet, and his disciples regarded him as a messiah. He was a disciple of Abu ’Isa and continued the messianic-military tradition in the Persian-Jewish community.

Source: Jewish Encyclopedia

12. Serene / Serenus

c. 720–723  |  Syria  |  Pseudo-messiah

The Jewish Encyclopedia describes him flatly as a pseudo-messiah who appeared in Syria and promised expulsion of Muslim rule and restoration to the Holy Land. He attracted followers from both the Syrian and Spanish Jewish communities before being captured by the Umayyad authorities.

Source: Jewish Encyclopedia, s.v. “Serene”

III. Fragmentary Medieval Cases

Several unnamed or poorly documented claimants are preserved in the sources. These include an unnamed claimant in France around 1087, another in Córdoba around 1117, another in Fez around 1127, and an unnamed claimant in Yemen in 1172 discussed by Maimonides in the Iggeret Teman (Epistle to Yemen). Maimonides’s response to the Yemenite case is itself a major primary source for understanding rabbinic attitudes toward messianic claims: he counseled patience and warned against false messiahs while affirming the legitimate messianic hope. (Jewish Encyclopedia)

IV. High and Late Medieval Period (12th – 16th Century)

13. David Alroy

c. 1160  |  Kurdistan / Mesopotamia  |  Self-declared messiah

A classic medieval claimant: he declared himself the Messiah, said he had been sent by God to free the Jews from Muslim rule, and to lead them back to Jerusalem. Benjamin of Tudela mentions him in his Travels. He reportedly possessed considerable learning and charisma, and his movement destabilized Jewish-Muslim relations in the region until his assassination.

Source: Benjamin of Tudela, Sefer ha-Masa’ot; Jewish Encyclopedia

14. Abraham Abulafia

1279/1284  |  Sicily / Italy  |  Prophet, then self-declared messiah

He first presented himself as a prophet and then, in Sicily, declared himself the Messiah and announced 1290 as the start of the messianic era. Abulafia is also one of the most important figures in the history of Kabbalah: his “prophetic Kabbalah” involved letter-permutation techniques (tzeruf) designed to induce ecstatic states. His messianic claim grew directly out of his mystical practice.

Source: Jewish Encyclopedia

15. Moses Botarel of Cisneros

c. 1413  |  Spain  |  False messiah

The Jewish Encyclopedia identifies him with a false messiah active in that period, during the upheavals following the mass conversions and pogroms of 1391 in the Iberian Peninsula.

Source: Jewish Encyclopedia

16. Asher Lemmlein

1502  |  Istria / Northern Italy  |  Forerunner

He proclaimed himself a forerunner of the Messiah, urged repentance and almsgiving, and predicted the Messiah’s arrival within half a year, preceded by a pillar of cloud and smoke. The imagery directly invokes the Exodus pillar of cloud and fire (Exodus 13:21–22). His movement caused widespread fasting and excitement among Italian and German Jews before quietly dissipating when the prediction failed.

Source: Jewish Encyclopedia

17. Solomon Molko

Early 1530s  |  Portugal / Italy  |  Messiah or precursor

The Jewish Encyclopedia says he came to regard himself as “the Messiah, or his precursor.” Born a converso (forced convert to Christianity), he underwent circumcision as an adult, returned publicly to Judaism, and began preaching messianic visions. He was eventually burned at the stake by the Inquisition in Mantua. His case exemplifies the volatile intersection of Iberian converso identity, Kabbalistic mysticism, and messianic hope in the 16th century.

Source: Jewish Encyclopedia, s.v. “Molko”

18. Isaac Luria

16th century  |  Safed, Galilee  |  Ephraimitic messiah

The Jewish Encyclopedia says Luria and then Hayyim Vital belonged to the line of pseudo-messiahs in the sense that they claimed the role of the Ephraimitic messiah — the forerunner of the Davidic one. Luria’s Kabbalistic system, with its doctrine of tikkun (cosmic repair), provided the theological infrastructure that would later fuel the Sabbatean explosion. In Lurianic thought, every soul participates in the messianic process; the messiah is less a single figure than the culmination of collective spiritual labor.

Source: Jewish Encyclopedia

V. The Early Modern Explosion (17th – 18th Century)

19. Sabbatai Tzevi / Shabbetai Zevi

1660s  |  Smyrna / Ottoman Empire  |  Self-proclaimed messiah

The biggest early modern case by far. Sabbatai declared himself the Messiah, gained the endorsement of the influential prophet Nathan of Gaza, and his movement spread across the entire Jewish world — from Amsterdam to Aleppo, from Hamburg to Salonika. Entire communities sold their property in expectation of imminent redemption. The crisis came when Sabbatai was brought before the Ottoman sultan and given the choice of death or conversion to Islam. He converted. The shock to the Jewish world was immeasurable, but a significant minority of followers continued to believe — interpreting his apostasy as a mystical descent into the realm of evil for the purpose of redeeming the holy sparks trapped there. Sabbateanism survived underground for generations.

Source: Encyclopedia Britannica; Jewish Encyclopedia

20. Mordecai Mokiah

c. 1678–1683  |  Italy / Ottoman Empire  |  Self-declared Davidic Messiah

A Sabbatean who first upheld Sabbatai’s claim and then declared that he himself was the Davidic Messiah. His career illustrates a recurring pattern: in the wake of a failed messianic movement, secondary figures claim the mantle for themselves, sometimes reinterpreting the failure of the original claimant as part of a larger divine plan.

Source: Jewish Encyclopedia

21. Jacob Frank

From the 1750s  |  Podolia / Poland  |  Reincarnated messianic soul

The Jewish Encyclopedia says he presented himself as the reincarnation of the Sabbatean messianic soul, called himself the “holy lord,” and was treated by followers as messiah. Frankism represents the most radical extension of Sabbatean antinomianism: Frank taught that the path to redemption ran through deliberate transgression of Torah law. He eventually converted to Catholicism (echoing Sabbatai’s conversion to Islam), taking his followers with him, while maintaining an esoteric inner doctrine among the initiated.

Source: Jewish Encyclopedia

22. Moses Hayyim Luzzatto

18th century  |  Padua, Italy  |  Destined redeemer

Included by the Jewish Encyclopedia among pseudo-messiahs because he came to believe he was destined to redeem Israel. Luzzatto (known by the acronym Ramhal) is better remembered today as the author of Mesillat Yesharim (Path of the Upright), one of the most influential works of Jewish ethical literature. His messianic claims, rooted in Kabbalistic revelation, drew the condemnation of the Italian rabbinate and forced him into exile.

Source: Jewish Encyclopedia

VI. The Modern Period

23. Menachem Mendel Schneerson

20th century  |  Brooklyn, New York  |  Claimed by followers (different category)

This case is not like Sabbatai or Frank. Schneerson (1902–1994), the seventh Rebbe of Chabad-Lubavitch, was and remains treated as the long-awaited Messiah by many of his followers. Britannica says many Chabad members have believed Schneerson to be the messiah. The question of whether he himself explicitly believed or said so remains debated among scholars and within the Chabad community. What is not debated is that a significant segment of his movement — the so-called meshichists — continue to proclaim him as King Messiah even after his death, placing this case in a unique category among all entries in this catalogue: a messianic claim maintained and intensified after the claimant’s death, without the collapse that followed Sabbatai or the conversion that marked Frank.

Source: Encyclopedia Britannica

Notable Exclusions

David Reubeni is often placed near these figures, but the Jewish Encyclopedia says he expressly denied that he was a messiah or prophet. He presented himself as a military ambassador from a lost Israelite kingdom, not as a messianic claimant.

Judas the Galilean is sometimes folded into popular lists, but the evidence for an explicit messiah claim is weaker than for Simon of Peraea, Athronges, Menahem, or Bar Kokhba. Josephus presents him primarily as a revolutionary ideologue — the founder of the “Fourth Philosophy” — rather than as a messianic pretender. (Jewish Encyclopedia)

The Core Canon

If one wants the shortest list of the best-attested Jewish messiah claimants or pretenders, it would be: Simon of Peraea, Athronges, Theudas, the Egyptian Prophet, Menahem ben Judah, Bar Kokhba, Moses of Crete, Abu ’Isa, David Alroy, Abraham Abulafia, Sabbatai Tzevi, Jacob Frank — with Schneerson in a separate “claimed by followers” category.

Recommended Reading

  • Gershom Scholem, Sabbatai Sevi: The Mystical Messiah, 1626–1676
    Princeton University Press. The definitive scholarly account of the Sabbatean movement, over 900 pages of primary-source analysis.
  • Gershom Scholem, Major Trends in Jewish Mysticism
    Schocken. The foundational work on Kabbalah and Jewish mysticism, with extensive treatment of the messianic dimension in Lurianic and Sabbatean thought.
  • Moshe Idel, Messianic Mystics
    Yale University Press. A comprehensive study of the intersection between Jewish mysticism and messianic claims, from antiquity through modernity.
  • Harris Lenowitz, The Jewish Messiahs: From the Galilee to Crown Heights
    Oxford University Press. A broad survey of messianic claimants across Jewish history, including primary-source translations.
  • Richard A. Horsley and John S. Hanson, Bandits, Prophets, and Messiahs: Popular Movements in the Time of Jesus
    Trinity Press International. Focuses on the social context of messianic and prophetic movements in the late Second Temple period.
  • Peter Schäfer, The Jewish Jesus: How Judaism and Christianity Shaped Each Other
    Princeton University Press. Explores how rabbinic Judaism and early Christianity defined messiahship in dialogue and opposition.
  • Pawel Maciejko, The Mixed Multitude: Jacob Frank and the Frankist Movement, 1755–1816
    University of Pennsylvania Press. The most thorough modern study of Frankism, drawing on Polish, Hebrew, and Yiddish primary sources.
  • Kenneth R. Stow, Jewish Dogs: An Image and Its Interpreters
    Stanford University Press. Examines the broader cultural context of Jewish-Christian messianic tensions in medieval and early modern Europe.
  • John J. Collins, The Scepter and the Star: Messianism in Light of the Dead Sea Scrolls
    Eerdmans. Essential for understanding the variety of messianic expectations in Second Temple Judaism, drawing on Qumran evidence.
  • Israel Jacob Yuval, Two Nations in Your Womb: Perceptions of Jews and Christians in Late Antiquity and the Middle Ages
    University of California Press. Explores how Jewish messianic expectation and Christian redemption theology developed in mutual reaction.
