Branch I — χριστός Migration

christos_migration/

Philological investigation of the word χριστός across the TLG corpus: from its earliest attestation as a pharmaceutical adjective in Classical Greek tragedy (Aeschylus, Euripides, c.460–428 BCE) through its use as a Jewish ritual title in the LXX Septuagint, its eschatological intensification in Second Temple literature, and its crystallization into both a messianic title and a proper name in the New Testament. Includes a complete diachronic semantic map and the full LSJ lexicon entry.

Branch II — Pharmakon Research

pharmakon_research/

Investigation of the pharmacological tradition embedded in Greek mystery religion (Eleusinian, Bacchic, Orphic) as a neglected cultural substrate for understanding the ministry and passion of Jesus. The hypothesis argues that the physical act of anointing (χρίω) with pharmacologically active substances connects the Greek pharmakological lineage from Medea and Hecate through Galen's theriac to the anointing oils, death-drugs, and sacred meals of early Christianity. Includes a 63-entry master sources database.

Branch III — Jason / Jesus Sidequest

jason-jesus/

Research sidequest documenting the etymological and structural relationship between Ἰάσων (Jason) and Ἰησοῦς (Jesus), historically documented in 2 Maccabees 4:7 where a Jewish high priest named Joshua/Yeshua adopts the Greek name Jason. Develops the hypothesis that the Argonautica (Apollonius Rhodius, c.250 BCE) is a literary proto-Gospel, with Medea as a literary ancestor of Mary Magdalene, through seven structural parallels including the dove at the threshold, the woman's pharmacological anointing, and the exile of the female resurrection-figure.
