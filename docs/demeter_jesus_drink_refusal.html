☉ Pagan Roots of Monotheism

Demeter and Jesus Refuse

Ritual Drink Refusal Branch Wine Refused  ·  Sacred Drink Accepted  ·  Threshold Crossed
Hypothesis: The refusal of wine followed by the acceptance of an alternative ritual drink at a moment of sacred transition is a coherent Mediterranean literary and ritual motif, attested independently in the Homeric Hymn to Demeter (7th–6th century BCE) and the crucifixion narratives of the New Testament Gospels. The recurrence of this pattern — identical in structure, complementary in theology — points toward a shared symbolic grammar in which ritual beverages mark initiation, transformation, and passage through death toward renewal.

I. The Pattern — Refusal, Substitution, Transformation

The pattern is simple enough to state in three steps, and remarkable enough to require explanation:

The Core Structure

1
A sacred figure is offered wine — the ordinary ritual drink of the Greek world, the drink of Dionysus, of sacrifice, of social bonds.
2
The figure refuses the wine — a deliberate rejection that marks the moment as outside ordinary ritual order.
3
The figure accepts a different drink — a substitute liquid with distinct symbolic or pharmacological associations — and by accepting it, crosses a liminal threshold into a new sacred order.

Both instances occur at the most charged moment in their respective narratives. In the Homeric Hymn to Demeter, Demeter is at the nadir of her grief and the threshold of the initiatory act that will create the Eleusinian Mysteries. In the Passion narratives, Jesus is at the moment of his death — the pivot of the Christian account of redemption. Wine is offered and refused. A different drink is offered and accepted. The world changes.

"The refusal of wine is not mere abstinence. It is a semiotic act: the sacred figure signals departure from the ordinary ritual order by refusing its central sacramental drink. The acceptance of the alternative is the corresponding entry into a new one."

II. Demeter at Eleusis — The Κυκεών Refusal

The evidence begins with the Homeric Hymn to Demeter, generally dated to the 7th–6th century BCE and among the most important documents for understanding Greek mystery religion. The hymn narrates Demeter's grief after the abduction of Persephone by Hades, her wandering in disguise, her arrival at the house of Celeus at Eleusis, and the founding of her cult.

The drink-refusal scene appears at lines 206–211. Metaneira, queen of Eleusis, offers the goddess (disguised as an old woman) a cup of sweet wine (μελιηδέα οἶνον). Demeter refuses. She instructs her hostess instead to mix her a κυκεών — a ritual drink of barley-meal, water, and soft pennyroyal.

Homeric Hymn to Demeter, lines 208–211 ἣ δ’ ἀπανήνατο ἔργον, ἀνήνατο δεῖπνον ἑλέσθαι·
αὐτὰρ ἐπεὶ κεδνὴ Μετάνειρα δέπας προτέτυκτο
οἴνου ἐρυθροῖο μελιηδέος, ἡ δ’ ἀπανήνατο—
οὐ γὰρ ἔφη θεμιτὸν εἶναι οἶνον ἐρυθρὸν πίνειν· She refused the task, refused to take food; but when noble Metaneira offered a cup of honey-sweet red wine, she refused it — for she said it was not lawful (themis) for her to drink red wine.
Homeric Hymn to Demeter, lines 208–211 (continued) — The Kykeon Requested ἀλλ’ ἄλφι καὶ ὕδατι δοῦναι μῖξαι γλήχωνι τέρεινῃ. But she bade them mix barley-meal and water with soft pennyroyal [to give her].

The key term is θεμιτόν: "lawful," "permitted by divine law or custom." This is not personal preference. Demeter is making a theological declaration: wine belongs to a different sacred order — Dionysus's order — and at this liminal moment, that order is precisely what she is stepping outside. The kykeon (κυκεών: from κυκάω, "to mix, to stir") is the alternative: a mixed drink, a threshold drink, associated not with the Olympian economy of wine and sacrifice but with the chthonic economy of grain, death, and renewal.

Offered Red Wine μελιηδέα οἶνον Dionysian, social, ordinary
→
Refused Refused οὐ θεμιτόν "Not lawful" — the threshold declaration
→
Accepted Kykeon κυκεών Barley + water + pennyroyal

This same kykeon became the sacred drink of the Eleusinian Mysteries — the beverage initiates drank at the climax of the nocturnal ceremony, after days of fasting, a rite promising initiation into the mystery of death and return. Scholars including R. Gordon Wasson, Albert Hofmann, and Carl Ruck (The Road to Eleusis, 1978) have proposed that ergotized barley — barley infected with Claviceps purpurea, the natural precursor to LSD — may have given the kykeon psychoactive properties, explaining the visionary experiences reported by initiates. Whether or not this pharmacological hypothesis is correct, the kykeon's ritual function is unambiguous: it is the drink of the threshold, the drink of the initiated, the drink that accompanies the symbolic experience of death and rebirth.

The critical point: Demeter does not merely refuse wine out of ascetic scruple. She is in the act of founding a new mystery cult. By refusing the Dionysian drink and demanding the kykeon, she is constituting a new ritual order — one governed by her own chthonic law, not the conventions of Olympian festivity. The kykeon she accepts at Eleusis is the same drink that will initiate thousands over the next thousand years into the mysteries of grain, death, and renewal.

III. Jesus at Golgotha — The Crucifixion Drink Sequence

The Gospel narratives present a two-act drink sequence at the crucifixion. The structure is rarely read as a unit; it repays close attention.

Act I: The Refusal. According to Mark 15:23, immediately before the crucifixion begins, Jesus is offered wine mixed with myrrh (ἐσμυρνισμένον οἶνον). He refuses it. Matthew 27:34 narrates the same scene, substituting gall (χολή) for myrrh — possibly a deliberate allusion to Psalm 69:21, which reads: "They gave me gall for food, and for my thirst they gave me vinegar to drink." Either way, the first drink is wine-based and it is refused.

Mark 15:23 καὶ ἐδίδουν αὐτῷ ἐσμυρνισμένον οἶνον· ὃς δὲ οὐκ ἔλαβεν. And they offered him wine mixed with myrrh; but he did not take it.
Matthew 27:34 ἔδωκαν αὐτῷ πιεῖν οἶνον μετὰ χολῆς μεμιγμένον· καὶ γευσάμενος οὐκ ἠθέλησεν πιεῖν. They gave him wine mixed with gall to drink; and when he had tasted it, he would not drink.

The wine-myrrh mixture is significant. According to the Babylonian Talmud (b. Sanhedrin 43a), it was the practice in Jerusalem to offer condemned prisoners a narcotizing drink before execution — specifically wine mixed with frankincense — as a mercy, to dull the senses and ease the passage into death. Myrrh served a similar analgesic function; it was used as a pain-reliever in ancient pharmacology. This was, in other words, an anesthetic wine — a drug designed to blunt consciousness at the moment of death. Jesus refuses it.

Act II: The Acceptance. Much later in the narrative, at the moment of death itself, Jesus accepts a very different drink. John 19:28–30 records that Jesus, knowing all things were now finished, says "I thirst" (Διψῶ). A sponge soaked in sour wine (ὄξος — posca, the sour wine of Roman soldiers, vinegar-wine diluted with water) is raised to his lips on a hyssop stalk. He receives it and says: Τετέλεσται. "It is finished."

John 19:28–30 μετὰ τοῦτο εἰδὼς ὁ Ἰησοῦς ὅτι ἤδη πάντα τετέλεσται, ἵνα τελειωθῇ ἡ γραφή, λέγει· Διψῶ.
σκεῦος ἔκειτο ὄξους μεστόν· σπόγγον οὖν μεστὸν τοῦ ὄξους ὑσσώπῳ περιθέντες
προσήνεγκαν αὐτοῦ τῷ στόματι. ὅτε οὖν ἔλαβεν τὸ ὄξος ὁ Ἰησοῦς εἶπεν· Τετέλεσται. After this, Jesus, knowing that all things were now finished, so that Scripture might be fulfilled, said, "I thirst." A jar full of sour wine was standing there; so they put a sponge full of the sour wine on a hyssop branch and brought it to his mouth. Therefore when Jesus had received the sour wine, he said, "It is finished."
Offered Wine + Myrrh ἐσμυρνισμένον οἶνον Analgesic, mercy drug, dulls consciousness
→
Refused Refused οὐκ ἔλαβεν "He did not take it" — full consciousness chosen
→
Accepted Posca (Sour Wine) ὄξος Soldier's drink, thirst quenched, "It is finished"

The posca Jesus accepts is not a sacred drink by nature — it was the common field ration of Roman soldiers, sour wine cut with water. What makes it sacred is the context: it is the drink Jesus requests precisely at the moment of fulfillment (Τετέλεσται), the declarative completion of his sacrificial death. The hyssop stalk on which it is presented is itself ritually charged: hyssop is the purification plant of Leviticus 14 and Psalm 51:9 (ὑσσώπῳ appears in both), used for sprinkling blood in the Passover (Exodus 12:22). John's use of hyssop transforms the posca offering into a typological act — the sour wine on hyssop recalls the Passover blood on hyssop. The accepted drink is the completion drink, the drink of the moment at which the threshold is crossed.

IV. Structural Comparison — Side by Side

The two narratives map onto each other across four independent structural axes.

1 The Liminal Context
Demeter / Homeric Hymn Demeter is at the lowest point of grief, wandering after the loss of Persephone. She arrives at Eleusis on the threshold of the foundational ritual act: the inauguration of the Mysteries. She sits on the Mirthless Stone at the Well of Beautiful Dances. This is the hinge-point of the myth.
Jesus / Passion Narratives Jesus is at the threshold of death, having been condemned, scourged, and led to Golgotha. The crucifixion is the hinge-point of the Christian narrative: the moment at which the sacrificial logic of the Passion reaches its culmination and (by Christian theology) the new covenant is inaugurated.
2 The Wine Refused
Demeter / HhDem 208–211 Metaneira offers honey-sweet red wine (μελιηδέα οἶνον). Demeter refuses with a theological declaration: οὐ θεμιτόν — "it is not lawful." Wine belongs to Dionysus's order; this moment belongs to a different order entirely.
Jesus / Mark 15:23 The soldiers offer wine mixed with myrrh (ἐσμυρνισμένον οἶνον). Jesus refuses: οὐκ ἔλαβεν — "he did not take it." The narration gives no reason; the refusal stands as a bare act. In context it reads as a refusal of the mercy-drug, a choice of full consciousness at the threshold of death.
3 The Alternative Drink Accepted
Demeter / HhDem 208–211 Demeter requests and accepts the kykeon (κυκεών): barley-meal, water, and soft pennyroyal. A mixed drink, not a fermented one. A drink associated with grain, the earth, and initiatory ritual. It becomes the sacred drink of the Mysteries.
Jesus / John 19:29–30 Jesus accepts sour wine (posca, ὄξος): the soldier's vinegar-wine, humble and unadorned, lifted on hyssop. Not a sacred drink by nature, but made sacred by the act: the drink that accompanies Τετέλεσται. "It is finished."
4 The Transformation or New Order
Demeter / Eleusinian Tradition The refusal and acceptance at Eleusis precede Demeter's institution of the Mysteries — the most important initiatory cult of the ancient world, promising participants direct experience of death and return, and the hope of a blessed afterlife. The kykeon is its central sacrament.
Jesus / Christian Tradition The refusal and acceptance at Golgotha frame the moment of Jesus's death, which Christian theology interprets as the atoning sacrifice that inaugurates the new covenant. The Eucharist — the wine of the Last Supper ("my blood of the new covenant") — is its central sacrament.

V. The Drinks as World-Makers — Kykeon, Posca, and the Eucharist

The deepest structural parallel is not merely that wine is refused and something else is accepted. It is that in both cases the pattern of refusal and acceptance constitutes a new sacred order. The drink that is accepted becomes the founding sacramental drink of a new ritual world.

The Kykeon as Mystery Sacrament

Demeter's Drink The kykeon Demeter requests at Eleusis does not merely quench her thirst. It constitutes the sacred drink of the Eleusinian Mysteries. For nearly a thousand years (roughly 700 BCE to 400 CE), initiates at Eleusis drank the kykeon after the ritual fast — a drink linked to Demeter's own experience of grief, threshold, and return. The drink Demeter chose at her most liminal moment became the drink initiates chose at their own liminal moment: the enactment of symbolic death and rebirth.
The Last Supper Cup The sour wine at the cross is not Jesus's last drink in the narrative arc of the Gospels. His last ritual drink is the cup of the Last Supper (τοῦτο γάρ ἐστιν τὸ αἷμά μου τῆς διαθήκης, "for this is my blood of the covenant," Matt 26:28), which he explicitly refuses to drink again until the Kingdom of God arrives (Mark 14:25). That cup becomes the Eucharist — the sacramental drink of Christian initiation, drunk in memory of his death and in anticipation of resurrection. Just as the kykeon commemorates Demeter's threshold moment, the Eucharist commemorates Jesus's.

The Refusal as Constitutive Act

Demeter's Refusal Demeter refuses wine not because wine is bad but because wine belongs to Dionysus's world. At the moment of founding a mystery cult rooted in her suffering, her law, and her grain, she refuses the drink that belongs to another deity's domain. The refusal is a declaration of differentiation: what follows is governed by a different logic, a different sacred economy. The Eleusinian order is constituted by and against the wine-refusal.
Jesus's Refusal Jesus's refusal of the wine-myrrh is a refusal of anesthesia at the most painful threshold of his existence. In the Gospel of Mark's stark narration, the refusal carries enormous weight: this death will be undergone consciously, without dulling. Read theologically: the sacrifice requires full presence and full suffering; the mercy-drug would short-circuit the atonement. What follows — the death, the cry of dereliction, the completion — is governed by a different logic than the mercy the soldiers were prepared to offer.
"In both cases, the sacred figure's refusal of wine is a world-making act. Demeter's refusal founds the Eleusinian order. Jesus's refusal founds — or rather confirms and enacts — the new covenant logic of his death. The drink refused tells us which world we are leaving; the drink accepted tells us which world we are entering."

VI. The Pharmacological Thread

This motif connects directly to the broader Pharmakon research branch of this archive. The Greek world did not draw clean lines between medicine, poison, intoxicant, and sacred substance — all were φάρμακα (pharmaka), substances with power over the boundary between life and death. The drink-refusal motif is pharmacologically as much as theologically charged.

The Pharmacology of the Drinks

Kykeon The three components of the kykeon — barley-meal, water, and pennyroyal (γλήχων) — each carry pharmacological associations. Pennyroyal (Mentha pulegium) was used in antiquity as an abortifacient, an emmenagogue, and a ritual herb. Wasson, Hofmann, and Ruck proposed that ergotized barley introduced ergine (d-lysergic acid amide) — a natural analogue of LSD — into the mixture, producing the visionary experiences reported by Eleusinian initiates including Plato, Pindar, and (reputedly) Cicero. Whether or not the ergot hypothesis holds, the kykeon was understood as a threshold-crossing substance.
Wine-Myrrh Myrrh (σμύρνα) was known in antiquity as an analgesic and a sedative. Modern pharmacology has confirmed that myrrh contains sesquiterpene compounds with opioid-receptor activity. The wine-myrrh mixture Jesus refuses is, in pharmacological terms, a pain-dulling drink — designed to reduce the suffering of crucifixion by chemically suppressing consciousness. What Jesus refuses is not merely a drink but an analgesic pharmacon — a substance that would have altered his consciousness at the moment of sacrifice. The refusal, read pharmacologically, is a choice of unmediated suffering.

The contrast is pharmacologically precise as well as theologically significant:

The Pharmacological Contrast

Demeter refuses ordinary wine and accepts the kykeon — a substance associated with altered consciousness, visionary experience, and the chthonic threshold. She accepts a pharmacologically active drink in order to cross into a new ritual space.

Jesus refuses pharmacologically active wine (wine + analgesic myrrh) and accepts the posca — a pharmacologically neutral drink (merely sour wine and water). He refuses the pharmacon that would alter his consciousness and accepts the drink that leaves it intact.

The polarity is inverted, but the logic is identical: each figure refuses the drink that would keep them in the ordinary world (ordinary wine for Demeter; drugged wine for Jesus) and accepts the drink appropriate to the sacred threshold being crossed.

VII. Transmission Mechanisms — How the Pattern Persists

Direct literary borrowing from the Homeric Hymn to Demeter by the Gospel authors is neither demonstrable nor the most plausible explanation. The Gospel writers worked within Jewish scriptural traditions, and the Passion narrative as a whole draws most heavily on the Hebrew Psalms (22, 69) and the Suffering Servant passages of Isaiah. Three alternative transmission mechanisms are more compelling.

Mechanisms of Persistence

1
Shared Mediterranean Symbolic Grammar. Throughout the ancient Mediterranean world, ritual drinks mark sacred transitions. Initiatory rites, mystery cults, and rites of passage consistently feature a special drink that separates sacred from profane time. This is not a Greek invention: it is a deep structural feature of human ritual across cultures. The wine-refusal motif participates in a more general grammar in which refusing the ordinary drink is a powerful ritual sign of departure from ordinary time.
2
The Hellenistic Cultural Environment. Early Christianity was born and grew to maturity within the Greek-speaking Mediterranean world. Paul writes in Greek; the Gospels were composed in Greek; the earliest Christian communities lived in cities like Corinth, Ephesus, and Antioch where mystery religion was a living cultural force. It is not necessary to propose that Gospel authors read the Homeric Hymn to Demeter: they breathed the symbolic air of a world in which the Eleusinian narrative — grief, wandering, sacred drink, threshold, return — was part of shared cultural consciousness.
3
The Narrative Grammar of the Liminal Hero. Myth-criticism has identified a recurring pattern across cultures: the hero at the threshold refuses ordinary nourishment (food or drink) before undergoing the ordeal that transforms them. Moses fasts forty days on Sinai. Elijah fasts in the desert. Jesus fasts forty days before his ministry. The refusal of food or drink signals entry into sacred time. The wine-refusal at the cross is the culminating instance of this grammar within the Gospel narrative: at the ultimate threshold, the ordinary is refused, and the sacred alternative accepted.

VIII. Limitations and Scholarly Cautions

A rigorous presentation of this hypothesis requires acknowledgment of the counterarguments and the limits of the comparison.

Scholarly Cautions

The Jewish scriptural background dominates the Passion narrative. Mark 15:23 and Matthew 27:34 both read most naturally as fulfillments of Psalm 69:21 ("they gave me gall for food; for my thirst they gave me vinegar to drink"). The wine-refusal in the Gospels is best explained first by its Hebrew scriptural context, and only secondarily by any parallel with Greek religion.

The ergot hypothesis for the kykeon remains unproven. While the Wasson-Hofmann-Ruck argument is philologically stimulating, the archaeological and pharmacological evidence is inconclusive. The ritual significance of the kykeon does not depend on its psychoactivity, but the pharmacological comparison with the myrrh-wine is weakened if neither drink's active compounds are confirmed.

Structural parallels may arise independently. The ritual logic of refusing the ordinary and accepting the sacred is available to any storyteller at any time. The parallel between Demeter and Jesus may represent independent deployments of a universal narrative grammar rather than evidence of cultural transmission.

The substances are not equivalent. Kykeon is a non-alcoholic mixed drink; posca is diluted sour wine. Neither is the same substance as the wine-myrrh or wine-gall that is refused. The comparison is structural and functional — it argues for parallel narrative patterns, not identical ritual content.

These cautions do not falsify the comparison; they calibrate it. The hypothesis is strongest as a claim about shared narrative structure and participation in a common Mediterranean symbolic grammar, not as a claim of direct literary dependence or identical ritual content.

IX. The Cumulative Argument

Taken together, the evidence supports a coherent hypothesis.

The strongest version of the argument:

Both Demeter and Jesus stand at the most charged liminal moment of their respective narratives — Demeter at the founding of the Mysteries, Jesus at the pivot of the Passion. In both cases, wine is offered. In both cases, wine is refused. In both cases, a different drink is accepted. In both cases, the accepted drink marks or inaugurates a new sacred order.

The coincidence is too structurally precise to be random and too culturally embedded to be surprising. The ancient Mediterranean world thought in categories of sacred threshold, ritual drink, and the meaning of refusal. The Homeric Hymn to Demeter and the Passion narratives of the Gospels — composed centuries apart, within traditions that claim no direct relationship — both deploy this grammar at their most critical moment.

The pattern belongs to no single tradition. It belongs to the symbolic inheritance of the ancient world: the drink refused names the world you are leaving; the drink accepted names the world you are entering.

The Eleusinian Mysteries promised initiates that those who drank the kykeon and survived the night-rite had glimpsed what lay beyond death and would face it without fear. The Christian Eucharist promises those who drink the cup of the new covenant a share in the death and resurrection of Jesus. Both promises are sealed by a drink. Both drinks were preceded, in the foundational narrative, by the refusal of wine.
