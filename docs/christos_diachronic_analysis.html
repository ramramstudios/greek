I. Research Audit of Project Files

Source Notation Key

[PF: filename] = Project file · [PT: author, work, citation] = Primary ancient text · [Lex: source s.v.] = Lexicon · [Infer: source] = Scholarly inference · [Disputed: note] = Textually or historically contested

A. What the Files Get Right

1. The Tripartite Semantic Structure

The three project files correctly identify three distinct semantic phases: (1) pharmaceutical/literal adjective in Classical Greek, (2) institutional title in LXX Greek, (3) proper name in NT/early Christian Greek. This tripartite structure maps directly onto the LSJ entry (I, II, III).

2. The Two Classical Witnesses

Correctly identified as the only two Classical authors in LSJ s.v.: Aeschylus PV 480 (χριστά neut. pl., opposed to πιστά) and Euripides Hipp. 516 (χριστόν neut. sg., opposed to ποτόν). Both correctly described as pharmaceutical vocabulary with zero religious resonance.

3. LXX Evidence

The LXX analysis is the strongest section: priestly title in Leviticus correctly documented; royal formula χριστὸς κυρίου in Samuel and Psalms well attested; plural χριστούς at Hab 3:13 correctly flagged; Isaiah 45:1 (Cyrus as χριστός) correctly identified; Daniel 9:25–26 Theodotion/LXX variant (χριστός vs. χρῖσμα) correctly noted.

4. Josephus Handling

Files correctly distinguish: Ant. 18.63 (Testimonium Flavianum) marked as disputed; Ant. 20.200 marked as more secure, τοῦ λεγομένου Χριστοῦ correctly analyzed as a distancing formula.

5. John 1:41 as Translational Bridge

Correctly identified as the most explicit bilingual gloss in the NT. The grammatical point — Μεσσίαν = transliterated Aramaic מְשִׁיחָא; ὁ Χριστός = Greek title — is correctly drawn.

6. Paul's Article/No-Article Distinction

The shift from title (ὁ Χριστός) to proper name (Χριστός without article) in the Pauline letters is correctly identified with representative passages (1 Cor 1:23–24; 15:3–4; Gal 3:28).

7. ψευδόχριστοι as Confirmatory Evidence

The compound ψευδόχριστοι (Matt 24:24 / Mark 13:22) is correctly noted as confirming that Χριστός had become sufficiently technical by the time of the Synoptic Apocalypse to generate the category of "false claimants."

8. Inflected Forms Coverage

The table of inflected forms is comprehensive for the attested forms, with appropriate caveat that χριστῶν and χριστοῖς were not separately confirmed in the open sources reviewed.

B. What Needs Correction or Qualification

1. Hippocrates Entry — Morphological Error (Occurrence 3) ⚠ CORRECTION REQUIRED

The passage cited is: "τοῖσι δὲ ἐν θέρει μὲν καὶ χειμῶνι, ἐλαίῳ χρηστέον ἐπὶ τὰ ἕλκεα" [PT: Hippocrates, De ulceribus 18 (Perseus:text:1999.01.0248)]

The form χρηστέον is a verbal adjective (gerundive) from χράομαι ("to use, employ, treat"), NOT from χρίω ("to anoint/smear"). These are distinct verbs with distinct roots. The Hippocratic corpus does use genuine χριστ- forms (from χρίω) in surgical contexts, but the specific passage quoted is morphologically distinct.

2. Aeschylus Prometheus Vinctus — Authorship Caveat Insufficient ⚠ QUALIFICATION REQUIRED

The file notes "date debated; possibly post-Aeschylean" but does not adequately flag that the authorship of the Prometheus Vinctus is a substantive scholarly dispute. Mark Griffith (Cambridge, 1983) and M.L. West (JHS 99, 1979) have argued the play is not by Aeschylus but by a later imitator. The form is still Classical (5th–4th century BCE) but the authorial authority is reduced. The LSJ citation of "Aesch. Pr. 480" reflects the traditional attribution and remains valid as a corpus citation.

3. Leviticus 4:3 — Form Distinction Not Consistently Maintained ⚠ MINOR CORRECTION

Lev 4:3 uses the PERFECT PASSIVE PARTICIPLE κεχρισμένος ("having been anointed"), while Lev 4:5, 4:16, 6:22 use the ADJECTIVE χριστός. Both render Hebrew הַמָּשִׁיחַ; the LXX translators' alternation reflects stylistic variation rather than semantic distinction, but the morphological forms are grammatically distinct.

4. "First Messianic Use" at 1 Sam 2:10 — Overconfident Wording ⚠ QUALIFICATION REQUIRED

"Messianic" is anachronistic if it means "referring to a future deliverer figure." At 1 Sam 2:10, Hannah's prayer closes with a reference to the future Israelite king within royal ideology — it is not yet eschatological in a Second Temple sense. The qualification "arguably" in the original text appropriately hedges.

5. Psalms of Solomon Entirely Absent from Files ⚠ SIGNIFICANT GAP

The Psalms of Solomon (c. 50 BCE) contain the most important extra-LXX, pre-NT use of χριστός as a title for a FUTURE messianic king. The files do not mention this text at all. Its absence is a substantial lacuna — see Stage 3 in the Diachronic Map below for full documentation.

6. Early Church Sources Entirely Absent from Files ⚠ GAP

The files acknowledge that "post-NT Christian literature (Ignatius, Justin, Origen, etc.)" contains thousands of occurrences but document none. The research goal specifically requires Stage 5: "Early church Greek: stabilization, expansion, and theological narrowing." This section is entirely missing — see Stage 5 below.

7. John 4:25 Missing from Documentation

John 4:25 provides a second bilingual gloss in the voice of the Samaritan woman: "Μεσσίας ἔρχεται, ὁ λεγόμενος Χριστός". Here Μεσσίας is glossed by ὁ λεγόμενος Χριστός — using the same distancing formula as Josephus 20.200, but in a Christian text as a narratorial explanation for Greek-speaking readers.

8. 1 Cor 15:3–4 Pre-Pauline Claim Lacks Source Notation

The dating "c. 35–40 CE" for the underlying tradition is widely accepted in NT scholarship (cf. J.D.G. Dunn, The Theology of Paul the Apostle, 1998, pp. 235–238) but must be labeled as inference from the transmission formula (παρέδωκα…ὃ καὶ παρέλαβον), not a textual fact.

9. Josephus Ant. 18.63 — "Neutral Original" is Speculation

Reconstructions of a neutral Josephan original (proposed by Meier, Crossan, Whealey) are modern editorial hypotheses — there is no surviving neutral Greek text. The only extant Greek manuscript tradition has ὁ χριστὸς οὗτος ἦν. Any reference to a "reconstructed neutral original" should be clearly labeled as modern scholarly speculation.

LSJ s.v. cites Hab 3:13 (acc. pl. χριστούς) and implies nom. pl. exists; full TLG corpus would confirm or deny nom. pl. attestations.

II. Diachronic Stage Map

A corrected and supplemented timeline from Classical Greek through early church Greek. Each stage is attested; the mechanism of change between stages is analyzed in Section IV.

Stage 1 — Classical Greek: Pure Pharmaceutical Adjective

c. 460–400 BCE (and continuing in medical prose through 1st century CE)

χριστός, -ή, -όν — verbal adjective of χρίω
Meaning: "that which is/has been/is to be smeared on, applied (as an ointment/unguent), topical"
Paradigmatic opposition: χριστά ≠ πιστά / ποτά / ποτόν ("drinkable")

φάρμακα … χριστά τε καὶ πιστά — Aeschylus, PV 480 [PT: TLG 0085.003]

πότερα δὲ χριστὸν ἢ ποτὸν τὸ φάρμακον; — Euripides, Hipp. 516 [PT: TLG 0006.005]

χριστά φάρμακα, χριστέον (from χρίω) — Hippocratic corpus, passim [Lex: LSJ s.v. χριστός I]

No ritual, religious, or royal connotation. The word describes PHYSICAL APPLICATION of a substance. The semantic space is identical to English "applied/topical."

Stage 2a — LXX Hellenistic: Priestly Anointing Title

Translation c. 250–200 BCE (Pentateuch)

χριστός now renders Hebrew הַמָּשִׁיחַ (ha-māšîaḥ) in institutional/ritual contexts.
Morphological status: Attributive adjective in a frozen title: ὁ ἱερεὺς ὁ χριστός (article + noun + article + adjective = definitized/nominalized title).

Key change from Stage 1: The adjective now modifies persons, not things. It marks a specific institutional status conferred by a ritual act. The article (ὁ) is essential: it signals "the anointed priest" as a designated role, not a quality.

ὁ ἱερεὺς ὁ χριστὸς ὁ τετελειωμένος τὰς χεῖρας — LXX Lev 4:5 [PT: Rahlfs-Hanhart]

ὁ ἱερεὺς ὁ χριστός — LXX Lev 4:16; 6:22

τὸ ἔλαιον τὸ χριστόν — LXX Lev 21:10 (bridge: ritual oil — neut. sg.)

Translation mechanism: STRUCTURAL CALQUE (parallel morphology: māšîaḥ [qal passive ptcp. of מָשַׁח] ≈ χριστός [verbal adj. of χρίω])

Stage 2b — LXX Hellenistic: Royal Davidic Title

Translation c. 200–150 BCE (Historical books, Psalms)

χριστός now renders מְשִׁיחַ יְהוָה (mešîaḥ YHWH) as a royal title for the reigning Davidic king.
Formula: ὁ χριστὸς κυρίου = "the LORD's anointed" — nearly formulaic (stereotyped).

Key change from Stage 2a: Shifts from priestly to royal domain. Still refers to LIVING individuals, not future figures.

τῷ χριστῷ κυρίου … χριστὸς κυρίου ἐστὶν οὗτος — LXX 1 Sam 24:7 (David refuses to harm Saul)

χριστὸς κυρίου συνελήμφθη — LXX Lam 4:20 (King Zedekiah captured, 586 BCE)

τῷ χριστῷ μου Κύρῳ — LXX Isa 45:1 (Cyrus — unique extension to a non-Israelite)

τοὺς χριστούς σου — LXX Hab 3:13 (acc. pl. — the only plural in the whole LXX corpus per LSJ)

Stage 2c — LXX Hellenistic / Transition: Proto-Messianic Use

Translation c. 150–100 BCE (Psalms, Daniel)

χριστός begins to carry eschatological / future referent. Ps 2:2 is in a "royal" psalm but was read in Jewish and early Christian tradition as referring to a future anointed king (cited in NT at Acts 4:25–26).

ἕως χριστοῦ ἡγουμένου ἑβδομάδες ἑπτά — Dan 9:25 (Theodotion) — ANARTHROUS; future referent in "70 weeks" schema

Dan 9:26: LXX reads χρῖσμα ("anointing"), Theodotion reads χριστός ("anointed one") — the variant itself shows translators navigating the title/abstract-noun boundary.

Stage 3 — Second Temple Jewish: Explicitly Eschatological Messianic Title NOT IN ORIGINAL PROJECT FILES — new evidence

Psalms of Solomon c. 50 BCE

This stage is THE CRITICAL MISSING LINK between LXX royal usage (Stage 2b) and NT messianic title (Stage 4). It is absent from all three original project files.

The Psalms of Solomon are Jewish pseudepigraphical poems written originally in Hebrew c. 50 BCE (following the Pompey invasion of Jerusalem, 63 BCE), preserved in Greek. They are NOT part of the LXX but circulated in the same Greek Jewish literary milieu.

βασιλεὺς αὐτῶν χριστὸς κυρίου — PsSol 17:32 [PT: ed. Rahlfs]

"their king, the Lord's anointed" — the king is FUTURE; no Davidic king currently reigns

ὑπὸ ῥάβδον παιδείας χριστοῦ κυρίου — PsSol 18:7 [PT: ed. Rahlfs]

Why this stage is critical: (1) The "king" in PsSol 17 is a FUTURE figure — genuine eschatological messianism. (2) The formula χριστὸς κυρίου WITHOUT article has begun to shed the article = moving toward name-status. (3) PsSol 17 is the closest parallel to NT messianic language and almost certainly represents the type of Jewish messianic expectation into which Jesus was inserted by his followers.

[Infer: J.J. Collins, The Scepter and the Star, 2nd ed. (Eerdmans, 2010), pp. 49–73]

Stage 4a — NT Paul: Title → Proper Name

c. 49–55 CE (earliest layer)

Χριστός is predominantly ANARTHROUS (without article) = proper name. ὁ Χριστός is retained in explicitly theological/titular passages. Formula Ἰησοῦς Χριστός is fully established.

ἐν θεῷ πατρὶ καὶ κυρίῳ Ἰησοῦ Χριστῷ — 1 Thess 1:1 (c. 49–51 CE) — earliest datable Christian document

Χριστὸς ἀπέθανεν ὑπὲρ τῶν ἁμαρτιῶν ἡμῶν — 1 Cor 15:3 (c. 53–55 CE) — pre-Pauline creedal tradition; anarthrous subject = name

εἷς ἐστε ἐν Χριστῷ Ἰησοῦ — Gal 3:28 — ἐν Χριστῷ formula (~164 times in Pauline corpus); locative with person's name = name

Stage 4b — NT Synoptic Gospels

c. 70–90 CE

ὁ Χριστός retained as a live messianic TITLE in narrative contexts. The Gospels narrate the historical process of identifying Jesus as χριστός, so the article is more common than in Paul.

Βίβλος γενέσεως Ἰησοῦ Χριστοῦ, υἱοῦ Δαυίδ — Matt 1:1 (incipit — no article = proper name)

Σὺ εἶ ὁ Χριστὸς ὁ υἱὸς τοῦ θεοῦ τοῦ ζῶντος — Matt 16:16 (Peter's confession — WITH article = title)

ψευδόχριστοι καὶ ψευδοπροφῆται — Matt 24:24 — COMPOUND proves title formation complete

Stage 4c — NT Johannine Literature

c. 90–100 CE

Εὑρήκαμεν τὸν Μεσσίαν, ὅ ἐστιν μεθερμηνευόμενον ὁ Χριστός — John 1:41 — THE KEY PASSAGE: only NT explicit bilingual gloss

Μεσσίας ἔρχεται, ὁ λεγόμενος Χριστός — John 4:25 — second bilingual gloss (Samaritan woman's voice)

ἵνα πιστεύ[σ]ητε ὅτι Ἰησοῦς ἐστιν ὁ Χριστὸς ὁ υἱὸς τοῦ θεοῦ — John 20:31 — the Gospel's stated organizing purpose

Stage 4d — External Attestation / Acts

c. 90–94 CE

καὶ Κύριον αὐτὸν καὶ Χριστὸν ἐποίησεν ὁ θεός — Acts 2:36 — the anointing is by RESURRECTION, not oil (adoptionist-sounding formula)

χρηματίσαι τε πρῶτον ἐν Ἀντιοχείᾳ τοὺς μαθητὰς Χριστιανούς — Acts 11:26 — derived appellative Χριστιανοί confirms full onomastic status

τὸν ἀδελφὸν Ἰησοῦ τοῦ λεγομένου Χριστοῦ — Josephus, Ant. 20.200 — ✓ AUTHENTIC; distancing formula confirms name-status by 93 CE

Stage 4e — NT Hebrews: Etymological Revitalization

c. 70 CE composition (debated)

A unique theological move: Χριστός is re-connected to its priestly LXX background (Stage 2a). The author of Hebrews explicitly identifies Jesus as a HIGH PRIEST, simultaneously using the name Χριστός and exploiting its earlier meaning as the anointed priest — a deliberate ETYMOLOGICAL REVITALIZATION.

Χριστὸς δὲ παραγενόμενος ἀρχιερεὺς τῶν γενομένων ἀγαθῶν — Heb 9:11

Stage 5a — 1 Clement: Fixed Proper Name NOT IN ORIGINAL PROJECT FILES — new evidence

c. 96 CE

Formulaic use of Ἰησοῦς Χριστός throughout: fully a proper name. The messianic titular force has become formulaic/liturgical — no messianic explanation offered or needed.

…κλητοῖς ἡγιασμένοις ἐν θελήματι θεοῦ διὰ τοῦ κυρίου ἡμῶν Ἰησοῦ Χριστοῦ — 1 Clem 1:1 [PT: TLG 1271.001]

Stage 5b — Ignatius of Antioch: Neologism as Proof NOT IN ORIGINAL PROJECT FILES

c. 107–115 CE

Systematic use of Ἰησοῦς Χριστός throughout all seven letters. The Pauline formulaic patterns are fully reproduced.

Χριστιανισμός (contrast with Ἰουδαϊσμός) — Ign. Phld. 6:1; Magn. 10:1–3 — NEOLOGISM THAT SEALS STAGE 5

The -ισμός abstract suffix can ONLY be attached to proper names, never to titles. Χριστιανισμός is impossible unless Χριστός is a stable proper name. Compare: Ἡρωδιανοί (followers of Herod = proper name), Καισαριανοί (followers of Caesar = proper name).

Ignatius also uses θεὸς ἡμῶν Ἰησοῦς Χριστός (Ign. Eph. 18:2) — applying ὁ Χριστός as an appositive to θεός, a theological step Paul never makes explicitly.

Stage 5c — Justin Martyr: Opacity as Evidence NOT IN ORIGINAL PROJECT FILES

c. 155–165 CE

Justin's works provide the clearest evidence that Χριστός has become a proper name whose etymological meaning is no longer self-evident to Greek-speaking audiences — which is precisely why he must EXPLAIN it.

1 Apol. 4: Justin defends Christians against persecution based on their NAME (Χριστιανοί). He plays on phonological similarity: Χριστιανοί (from Χριστός) vs. χρηστοί / Χρηστιανοί (from χρηστός = "good"). Tacitus (Ann. 15.44) calls the founder "Chrestus" — reflecting the same phonological ambiguity.

Dial. Tryph. 86: Justin explicitly explains why Jesus is called χριστός — he is "anointed" (κεχρισμένος) as priest, king, and prophet. This ETYMOLOGICAL RECOVERY proves the word is an opaque proper name for his audience.

KEY INFERENCE: Justin's need to explain the etymology is the strongest evidence that Χριστός is no longer a transparent Greek adjective, recognized Jewish messianic title, or translation term — it is a proper name requiring philological unpacking. [Infer: M.J. Edwards, JECS 3 (1995)]

IV. Analytical Conclusion

Q1: "Borrowing into Hebrew and back into Greek"?

INACCURATE. This formulation rests on a false model. The correct model has three distinct components:

Component A — Independent Native Development

Greek χριστός existed as a native Greek verbal adjective (from χρίω) with purely pharmacological meaning centuries before any Jewish Greek translation. Hebrew מָשִׁיחַ existed as a native Hebrew nominal derivation (from מָשַׁח) within Israelite tradition. These two words developed independently. Hebrew NEVER borrowed the Greek word χριστός.

Component B — Semantic Calque in the LXX

LXX translators chose χριστός based on STRUCTURAL EQUIVALENCE: both words derive from verbs meaning "to anoint/smear" and both function as verbal adjectives meaning "anointed one." This is a SEMANTIC CALQUE (loan-translation), not a borrowing: a calque takes the meaning/structure and expresses it in native vocabulary. Through repeated use as the translation equivalent of מָשִׁיחַ, χριστός ACCUMULATED the semantic field of מָשִׁיחַ. Direction of transfer: Hebrew → Greek.

Component C — Transliteration as a Separate Channel

Aramaic מְשִׁיחָא was BORROWED into Greek as Μεσσίας — this IS a genuine loanword. Μεσσίας and ὁ Χριστός are two separate channels for the same underlying Hebrew concept. John 1:41 explicitly presents ὁ Χριστός as the TRANSLATION of Μεσσίας, confirming the two-channel system was still recognized c. 90 CE.

Correct model: (a) χριστός = Greek verbal adjective used as SEMANTIC CALQUE for Hebrew מָשִׁיחַ. (b) Semantic content of מָשִׁיחַ migrated INTO χριστός through LXX translation — direction is Hebrew → Greek. (c) Μεσσίας = genuine Greek loanword from Aramaic — a separate channel. (d) "Borrowing into Hebrew" is a false description: Hebrew never borrowed χριστός.

Q2: Semantic Continuity vs. Narrowing / Reinterpretation

Continuity

The physical semantic base (anointing with oil) is maintained as a referential anchor throughout all stages. Even in the NT, the connection is theologically activated: Acts 10:38 ("ἔχρισεν αὐτὸν ὁ θεὸς πνεύματι ἁγίῳ" — "God anointed him with the Holy Spirit"), citing Isa 61:1. The anointing is spiritualized, but χρίω remains the interpretive key.

Narrowing

  1. Stage 1: any substance smeared onto any surface or person
  2. Stage 2a: any anointed priest of Israel
  3. Stage 2b: any reigning Davidic king
  4. Stage 3: THE expected future Davidic deliverer (Psalms of Solomon)
  5. Stage 4: Jesus of Nazareth, specifically
  6. Stage 5: Jesus as a fixed proper name

The trajectory is one of PROGRESSIVE SINGULARIZATION: plural → collective → specific individual → proper name.

Reinterpretation

The one significant theological reinterpretation occurs between Stage 3 (PsSol — the future Davidic king who will defeat Rome militarily) and Stage 4 (NT — a χριστός who is crucified). Paul explicitly acknowledges this tension: "Χριστὸν ἐσταυρωμένον, Ἰουδαίοις μὲν σκάνδαλον" (1 Cor 1:23) — "Christ crucified — a stumbling block to Jews." [Infer: N.T. Wright, The Climax of the Covenant (T&T Clark, 1991), ch. 2]

Q3: Lexical Translation / Title Formation / Resemanticization

Lexical Translation (Stage 2a–b)

LXX Leviticus: χριστός as direct translation of מָשִׁיחַ in fixed priestly and royal contexts. Meaning is transparent.

Title Formation (Stage 2b–c and PsSol)

When ὁ χριστὸς κυρίου becomes a recognized formula, it solidifies from a descriptive adjective into a quasi-title. Formal criteria: (a) stereotyped collocation, (b) definite article as title marker, (c) referential stability. PsSol 17:32 shows the title beginning to detach from its article — becoming more name-like.

Theological Resemanticization (Stage 4)

Acts 2:36 ("God has MADE him both Lord and Christ") and 1 Cor 1:23 ("Christ crucified — scandal to Jews") are the two textual moments where the semantic content is most explicitly renegotiated: (a) the anointing is by RESURRECTION, not oil; (b) the criteria for identifying the "anointed" are inverted (crucifixion ≠ victory). This is genuine RESEMANTICIZATION.

Q4: Strongest Evidence for Adjective → Title → Name Transition

Adjective → Title

Best evidence: LXX Lev 4:5 — the triple-article construction ὁ ἱερεὺς ὁ χριστὸς ὁ τετελειωμένος shows χριστός in a FROZEN TITLE SLOT. Compare: ordinary adjective (ἱερεὺς χριστός = "an anointed priest") vs. title (ὁ ἱερεὺς ὁ χριστός = "the anointed priest" — a specific role). The article's repetition signals χριστός has entered the nominal sphere.

Title → Quasi-Proper Name (proto-stage)

Best evidence: Dan 9:25 (Theodotion): ἕως χριστοῦ ἡγουμένου — ANARTHROUS. Absence of article in a temporal formula shows χριστός beginning to behave like a name: names take no article. Also: PsSol 17:32 (χριστὸς κυρίου without article).

Quasi-Name → Proper Name (three simultaneous criteria)

  • (a) Paul's anarthrous formula "Χριστὸς ἀπέθανεν" (1 Cor 15:3): anarthrous subject of a main verb = name.
  • (b) Derived appellative "Χριστιανοί" (Acts 11:26): follower-names derive only from proper names.
  • (c) Justin's need to EXPLAIN the etymology (1 Apol. 4; Dial. 86): when speakers must recover a word's meaning philologically, it is a name.

All three criteria point to full name-status no later than c. 50–55 CE in Paul, confirmed externally by c. 96 CE (1 Clement, Ignatius), and lexicographically confirmed by c. 155 CE (Justin's explanations).

Q5: Early Church Evidence that Χριστός is No Longer a Translation

Criterion: If Χριστός were still felt as a translation of מָשִׁיחַ, writers would not need to explain its meaning to Greek-speaking audiences.

Evidence 1 — Ignatius, Phld. 6:1 (c. 107–115 CE)

Χριστιανισμός vs. Ἰουδαϊσμός — "Christianity" as a proper-noun abstract system named from the name Χριστός. This naming practice treats Χριστός as a FOUNDER'S NAME, not as a translation term.

Evidence 2 — Justin, 1 Apol. 4 (c. 155 CE)

Justin distinguishes Χριστιανοί (from Χριστός) vs. χρηστοί (from χρηστός, "good/useful"). If Χριστός were still transparent as "the anointed one," Justin would explain its messianic Jewish content. Instead he must explain the WORD FORM — its similarity to another Greek word that sounds similar. This phonological confusion is only possible if χριστός has become OPAQUE as a name.

Evidence 3 — Tacitus, Annals 15.44 (c. 116 CE, Latin)

Tacitus writes "Christus" as a proper name for his Latin audience without explanation or translation — confirms the name required no decoding in educated circles by c. 116 CE.

Conclusion: By c. 107–115 CE (Ignatius), Χριστός is a FIXED CHRISTIAN IDENTIFIER — it names a religion, defines identity, and generates new vocabulary without invoking its Hebrew/Aramaic background. By c. 155 CE (Justin), even its adjectival etymology ("anointed") has become non-transparent. The transition from adjective to title to opaque proper name is COMPLETE by the mid-2nd century CE.
