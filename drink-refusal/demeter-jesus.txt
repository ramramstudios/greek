**Refusal of Wine and Acceptance of an Alternative Ritual Drink: A Comparative Literary Motif from Greek Religion to Early Christianity**

### Abstract

This report examines a proposed literary and cultural motif in Mediterranean antiquity: the **refusal of wine followed by the acceptance of an alternative ritual drink at a moment of sacred transition**. Evidence is drawn primarily from the *Homeric Hymn to Demeter* and the crucifixion narratives of the New Testament Gospels. The analysis suggests that this pattern may represent a broader narrative trope tied to ritual liminality, initiation, and transformation. While direct textual dependence is unlikely, the recurrence of this pattern indicates participation in shared symbolic structures within the ancient Mediterranean religious imagination.

---

### 1. Introduction

Wine held deep symbolic significance throughout the ancient Mediterranean world. Associated with social life, sacrifice, and the cult of Dionysus, wine represented both divine blessing and ritual intoxication. However, several ancient narratives describe a **deliberate refusal of wine in favor of another beverage** during moments of profound religious significance.

This study investigates whether such scenes represent a recurring **literary or ritual trope**, transmitted culturally across religious traditions.

---

### 2. The Kykeon in the *Homeric Hymn to Demeter*

The earliest example appears in the **Homeric Hymn to Demeter** (lines 206–211), generally dated to the 7th–6th century BCE. After wandering in grief over the abduction of Persephone, Demeter arrives at Eleusis disguised as an old woman. When offered wine, she refuses and instead instructs her hosts to prepare a **kykeon**, a mixture of barley, water, and pennyroyal.

This drink later became central to the **Eleusinian Mysteries**, a major initiatory cult promising participants insight into death and rebirth.

Scholars have proposed that the kykeon may have contained psychoactive components derived from ergotized barley, a hypothesis most prominently advanced by **R. Gordon Wasson**, **Albert Hofmann**, and **Carl A. P. Ruck** in *The Road to Eleusis* (1978). While controversial, the proposal emphasizes the drink’s ritual and transformative function.

In literary terms, Demeter’s refusal of wine—associated with Dionysian culture—signals a transition into a **distinct ritual order tied to agricultural fertility and initiatory revelation**.

---

### 3. Parallel Motifs in the Crucifixion Narratives

A comparable narrative structure appears in the Passion accounts of the New Testament. According to the **Gospel of Mark** (15:23), Jesus is offered wine mixed with myrrh but refuses it. A similar description appears in the **Gospel of Matthew** (27:34), where the drink is wine mixed with gall.

Later in the narrative, however, Jesus accepts sour wine shortly before his death, as described in the **Gospel of John** (19:29–30). The drink offered is likely *posca*, a diluted sour wine commonly consumed by Roman soldiers.

The literary sequence parallels the Demeter episode:

1. **Refusal of a particular wine mixture**
2. **Acceptance of a different drink**
3. **Occurrence during a liminal, transformative moment**

In the Gospel narrative, this moment marks the culmination of Jesus’s sacrificial death, interpreted by Christian theology as inaugurating redemption and resurrection.

---

### 4. Structural Comparison

The two narratives share several structural elements.

**Liminal context**

* Demeter: grief and the threshold of the Eleusinian initiation tradition.
* Jesus: the crucifixion, a threshold between death and resurrection.

**Ritual beverage substitution**

* Wine is rejected.
* An alternative drink with symbolic or ritual associations is accepted.

**Transformation or revelation**

* Eleusinian Mysteries promise insight into life, death, and rebirth.
* Christian theology interprets the crucifixion as the pivotal moment of salvation.

Although the substances differ—kykeon versus sour wine—the narrative function is similar: the drink becomes a **marker of sacred transition rather than ordinary consumption**.

---

### 5. Possible Transmission Mechanisms

Direct literary borrowing is unlikely because the Gospel writers worked within Jewish scriptural traditions. However, several mechanisms could explain the persistence of the motif:

1. **Shared Mediterranean symbolic structures**
   Ritual beverages often mark initiation or divine encounter.

2. **Hellenistic cultural environment**
   Early Christianity developed within a Greek-speaking world familiar with mystery cult symbolism.

3. **Narrative archetypes**
   Mythic storytelling frequently emphasizes the hero’s refusal of ordinary sustenance before entering a sacred ordeal.

---

### 6. Limitations of the Hypothesis

Several cautions must be noted:

* The Gospel narratives may primarily reflect **Jewish scriptural traditions**, particularly the fulfillment of prophecy such as Psalm 69:21.
* The **ergot hypothesis** for kykeon remains debated among scholars.
* Similar motifs may arise independently rather than through transmission.

Thus, the comparison should be understood as **structural and thematic**, not evidence of direct dependence.

---

### 7. Conclusion

The episodes of Demeter and Jesus both employ a striking literary pattern: the rejection of ordinary wine and the acceptance of a different drink at a moment of sacred transition. While emerging from distinct religious traditions, these narratives participate in a broader Mediterranean symbolic language in which ritual beverages mark **initiation, transformation, and passage through death toward renewal**.

The recurrence of this motif suggests that early Christian storytelling may have resonated with, or unconsciously echoed, narrative structures already familiar within the wider Hellenistic religious world.
