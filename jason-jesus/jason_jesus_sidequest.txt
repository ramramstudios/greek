SIDEQUEST: JASON / JESUS — A LITERARY RESURRECTION
The Argonautic Myth as Proto-Gospel; Medea as Proto-Magdalene

Project: christ-tlg | Branch: jason-jesus
Date: 2026-03-07
Status: Research Direction Document — Future Expansion
================================================================================

This document records a research sidequest for future development. The core
observation is simple and philologically grounded, then opens into a wider
mythological typology:

    Jason (Ἰάσων) is another name for Jesus (Ἰησοῦς).

Not metaphorically. Etymologically, functionally, and historically documented.

And if Jason and Jesus share a name — and a name in antiquity is a program,
a destiny, a role — then the oldest surviving extended narrative about a hero
named Jason (Apollonius Rhodius's Argonautica) may be something more than
background context for our research. It may be a literary ancestor — a dry
run — of the same mythological figure who appears four centuries later in the
Gospels.

And if that is true, then Medea is something more than a parallel figure to
Mary Magdalene. She may be an earlier draft of the same character: the foreign
woman with pharmacological knowledge, devoted to the hero, marginalized by
the tradition that follows him, and ultimately left holding the instruments
of death and resurrection while the male story moves on without her.

SECTION I: THE NAME — Ἰάσων AND Ἰησοῦς
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The name Jesus (Ἰησοῦς) is the Greek rendering of the Aramaic/Hebrew name
Yeshua (יֵשׁוּעַ), which derives from the root yasha' (יָשַׁע) = "to save,
to deliver." It means "God is salvation" or "the one who saves/delivers."

The name Jason (Ἰάσων) is Greek, from the root ἰάομαι (iaomai) = "to heal."
It means "the healer."

Two different etymological roots — Hebrew yasha' (save) and Greek iaomai (heal)
— but the same semantic field: the one who restores those who are lost,
broken, or dying.

The crucial historical link is in 2 Maccabees:

  2 Maccabees 4:7 (LXX, TLG 0527.001):
  "When Seleucus died and Antiochus called Epiphanes succeeded to the
  kingdom, Jason the brother of Onias (Ἰάσων δὲ ὁ ἀδελφὸς Ὀνίου)
  obtained the high priesthood by corruption."

  2 Maccabees 4:10-13:
  "When the king assented and Jason came to office, he at once shifted
  his compatriots over to the Greek way of life... He set aside the
  existing royal concessions to the Jews... and introduced new customs
  contrary to the law."

Jason of 2 Maccabees is a Jewish high priest (~175 BCE) who Hellenized
Jerusalem and adopted a Greek name. His Hebrew name was Joshua (Yeshua —
the same name as Jesus). He took the Greek name Jason as the functional
equivalent of Yeshua. The Hellenizing Jewish world chose Ἰάσων as the Greek
form of Ἰησοῦς / Yeshua.

This is not speculation. 2 Maccabees 4:7 is the historical document of the
equivalence. Jason = Jesus in the Greek-Jewish naming world.

Paul's associate in Thessalonica (Acts 17:5-9) is also named Jason
(Ἰάσων) — a Diaspora Jew with a Greek name that is the Hellenized form of
Yeshua/Joshua. Romans 16:21: "Jason, my kinsman" — Paul's greeting to a
Jewish companion named Jason.

The name floats freely between Hebrew Yeshua and Greek Jason in the 2nd
century BCE through 1st century CE Jewish world. They are the same name
in different linguistic registers.

SECTION II: THE FIGURE — JASON AS PROTO-JESUS
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Given the name identity, the structural parallels between the Argonautic
hero Jason and the Gospel figure Jesus deserve systematic comparison.

PRIMARY SOURCE: Apollonius Rhodius, Argonautica (~3rd c. BCE; TLG 0001.001)
This is the fullest surviving version of the Jason myth. It would have been
widely read in the Hellenized Jewish world of the 1st century BCE-CE, as
part of the standard Greek literary education (paideia).

Parallel 1: THE GATHERING OF DISCIPLES
  · Jason assembles the Argonauts — a band of exceptional men who follow him
    on a divine mission to recover a sacred object (the Golden Fleece).
  · Jesus assembles twelve disciples — a band of ordinary men who follow him
    on a divine mission (the Kingdom of God).
  · Both groups are specifically called and specifically chosen (not random).
  · Both narratives describe the individual callings (Arg. 1.23-227 lists
    each Argonaut; the Gospels record each disciple's calling).

Parallel 2: THE DIVINE MISSION AND SACRED OBJECT
  · The Golden Fleece (χρυσόμαλλον δέρας): a divine object in a distant land,
    guarded by supernatural forces, whose recovery will vindicate Jason's claim
    to his heritage (his father's kingdom).
  · The Kingdom of God (βασιλεία τοῦ θεοῦ): a divine reality to be recovered/
    inaugurated, guarded by religious and political opposition, whose arrival
    will vindicate the children of God's claim to their inheritance.
  · Both are described as something lost that must be recovered through a
    dangerous journey.

Parallel 3: THE PASSAGE THROUGH DANGEROUS WATERS
  · The Argonauts pass through the Symplegades (Clashing Rocks) — the
    threshold at the Bosphorus between the known world and the unknown.
    Arg. 2.536-610: the rocks crash together and nearly destroy the Argo;
    only a divine dove guides them through.
  · Jesus passes through the Jordan River at baptism — the threshold between
    the old life and the new mission (Mark 1:9-11). The Jordan was the
    historical entry-point to the Promised Land (Joshua 3-4 LXX).
  · Both threshold passages involve divine guidance (dove: Arg. 2.536;
    dove at Jesus's baptism: Mark 1:10) and a transformation of the hero.

Note: the dove at the Symplegades and the dove at the Jordan baptism.
In both narratives, a divine bird guides the hero through a death-threshold.
Argonautica 2.536: the dove flies ahead; the rocks crash but only clip
the tail-feathers. Mark 1:10: the Spirit descends "like a dove" (ὡς περιστεράν)
immediately after Jesus rises from the death-water.

Parallel 4: THE HERO'S PASSIVITY AND THE WOMAN'S AGENCY
  · The standard critique of Jason as an Argonautic hero: he succeeds largely
    because Medea does the actual work. She provides the pharmaka, navigates
    the ritual, breaks the dragon's sleep, betrays her father.
  · Jesus in the Gospels is often passive in the anointing narratives:
    he is anointed by women (Mary of Bethany; the sinful woman in Luke 7;
    the spice-bearers at the tomb). The women provide the pharmacological
    agency; the male hero receives it.
  · In both narratives, the woman's knowledge and action — specifically
    involving anointing substances — is what makes the hero's mission possible.

Parallel 5: THE BETRAYAL AND ABANDONMENT
  · Jason abandons Medea for Glauce (Euripides Medea): he trades the woman
    who gave him everything for a politically advantageous marriage. Medea's
    revenge destroys everything.
  · Jesus's male disciples abandon him at the crucifixion (Mark 14:50:
    "they all left him and fled" — καὶ ἀφέντες αὐτὸν ἔφυγον πάντες).
    The women remain (Mark 15:40-41). Mary Magdalene is there at the cross
    and at the tomb.
  · In both narratives, the male hero is surrounded by male followers who
    ultimately fail or betray; the female figure (Medea / Mary Magdalene)
    remains and is the true witness.

Parallel 6: THE RESURRECTION FIGURE
  · Medea performs resurrection in the myth: she rejuvenates the aged Aeson
    (Jason's father) by draining his blood and replacing it with a magical
    herb-potion; she deceives Pelias's daughters into killing their father
    with a false promise of rejuvenation (Ovid Met. 7.159-293; Diodorus 4.52).
    She is the resurrection-figure of the myth: she knows how to bring back
    the dead and how to send to death.
  · Mary Magdalene is the resurrection-witness of the Gospels: she is the
    first to see the risen Jesus (John 20:14-18). She comes to the tomb with
    spices (ἀρώματα — the same word used for pharmacological aromatic
    substances) for anointing the body (Mark 16:1).
  · Both women carry pharmacological knowledge toward a dead or dying male
    figure and are the agents of the death/resurrection transition.

SECTION III: MEDEA AS PROTO-MAGDALENE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The structural parallel between Medea and Mary Magdalene is not incidental.
It maps across multiple independent axes:

AXIS 1: FOREIGN WOMAN / MARGINAL GEOGRAPHY
  · Medea is from Colchis (eastern Black Sea coast) — a barbarian from the
    margins of the Greek world. She is "other" in the Hellenistic imagination.
  · Mary Magdalene is from Magdala (Migdal) on the Sea of Galilee — a woman
    from the Galilean margins. "Galilee of the Gentiles" (Matt 4:15).
    Her name (Magdalene) marks her geographic identity, just as "Medea" marks
    the Colchian/Median connection.

AXIS 2: PHARMACOLOGICAL KNOWLEDGE
  · Medea: her defining characteristic is her mastery of pharmaka, taught by
    Hecate. She anoints Jason (Arg. 3.844-869). She heals and kills with
    substances.
  · Mary Magdalene: she comes to the tomb with anointing spices (Mark 16:1:
    ἀγοράσωσιν ἀρώματα ἵνα ἐλθοῦσαι ἀλείψωσιν αὐτόν — "to buy spices
    so that they might come and anoint him"). She is an anointing-woman.
  · In the tradition of Luke 7:37-50, a woman (sometimes identified as
    Mary Magdalene) anoints Jesus with ointment from an alabaster flask
    and wipes his feet with her hair. The ointment (μύρον) is pharmacologically
    active (myrrh-based or spikenard). Her act is intimate, physical, and
    pharmacological.

AXIS 3: DEVOTION TO THE HERO / REJECTION BY THE TRADITION
  · Medea sacrifices everything for Jason — her homeland, her family, her
    father's trust. Jason later abandons her. The tradition (through Euripides)
    transforms her from a devoted helper into a terrifying vengeful figure —
    making her the villain so that Jason's failure is obscured.
  · Mary Magdalene is the most prominent female follower of Jesus in the
    Gospels. In John's Gospel she is the first witness to the resurrection
    — the most privileged position. But in the subsequent tradition she is
    reduced, conflated with the sinful woman of Luke 7, and marginalized.
    Pope Gregory I (591 CE) officially declared her a reformed prostitute —
    a move with no Gospel basis, which effectively demoted her from
    resurrection-witness to penitent sinner.
  · In both cases: the woman who holds the pharmacological and spiritual
    key to the hero's mission is subsequently demoted and demonized by the
    male tradition that succeeds the hero.

AXIS 4: THE RESURRECTION KNOWLEDGE
  · Medea knows how to resurrect the dead (Aeson's rejuvenation; the trick
    with Pelias). She is the keeper of resurrection pharmacology.
  · Mary Magdalene is the first to see and testify to the resurrection.
    In John 20:11-18, she mistakes Jesus for "the gardener" (κηπουρόν) —
    possible: a figure tending plants, roots, herbs; possibly an echo of
    the rhizotomos / root-cutter / pharmacological practitioner role.
  · Both women: what looks like death is reversed in their presence.
    The resurrection knowledge belongs to the woman.

AXIS 5: THE HECATE / MAGDALENE PARALLEL
  · Medea is Hecate's priestess. Hecate is the goddess of crossroads, the
    underworld, pharmaka, and liminal spaces between life and death.
  · Mary Magdalene was reportedly delivered of "seven demons" (Luke 8:2) —
    she lived at the boundary between the possessed and the freed, between
    death-in-life and life. She is a liminal figure, like Hecate's devotees.
  · The tower (migdal) of Magdala could evoke a watchtower at a liminal
    point — a crossing-place. Compare Hecate at the crossroads.

SECTION IV: THE LITERARY RESURRECTION HYPOTHESIS
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The proposal is not that the Gospels plagiarized the Argonautica. The proposal
is more interesting: that both texts draw on a common deep mythological grammar
of the Mediterranean world, and that the Greek literary tradition offered
a ready-made template — a hero named "the Healer/Savior" guided by a
pharmacological woman through death and back — which was available to the
authors of the Gospels and to the cultural imagination of their audience.

This is what we can call a LITERARY RESURRECTION: not that Jason literally
becomes Jesus, but that the mythological figure of the hero-named-healer,
guided by the woman-with-pharmaka through the death-threshold, was resurrected
in the Gospel tradition — reborn in a new theological register, carrying the
same structural skeleton.

The tradition would have worked as follows:

  1. The Jason myth was widely known in the educated Hellenized Jewish world.
     The Argonautica was part of the standard literary curriculum (paideia).
     Educated Jews in Alexandria, Antioch, and Hellenized Palestine read it.

  2. The name Jason = Yeshua / Jesus was a recognized equivalence (2 Macc 4:7).
     A Hellenized Jew reading the Argonautica and hearing about a hero named
     Ἰάσων would experience the same name-recognition as a Christian hearing
     Ἰησοῦς — the healer/savior.

  3. The Argonautic structure — gathering of followers, divine mission,
     dangerous journey, woman with pharmaka, death-threshold, recovery of
     sacred object — was a pre-existing narrative template.

  4. The Gospel authors (writing in Greek for Greek-speaking audiences, 40-100 CE)
     worked within this shared cultural imagination. Whether deliberately or
     by absorption, the Jason/Medea template shapes the Jesus/Magdalene narrative.

  5. The result is a LITERARY RESURRECTION: the ancient mythological hero is
     reborn in the Gospel figure, carrying the structural inheritance of the
     Argonautic tradition into the new theological context.

The most elegant version of this argument:
  Jason is Jesus's literary ancestor.
  Medea is Mary Magdalene's literary ancestor.
  The Argonautica is the proto-Gospel that the Greek world had already
  written, four centuries before the historical Jesus.
  When Jesus lived and died and was proclaimed risen, his story was told
  in the only narrative grammar available: the grammar of the hero-named-healer,
  guided by the woman-with-pharmaka, through the waters of death and back.

SECTION V: SPECIFIC TEXTUAL PARALLELS FOR FUTURE RESEARCH
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The following specific textual parallels deserve detailed future analysis:

1. THE DOVE AT THE THRESHOLD
   · Arg. 2.536: dove guides the Argo through the Symplegades (death-rocks)
   · Mark 1:10: dove descends at Jesus's baptism (death-water threshold)
   · Both: a divine bird guides the hero through a death-threshold at the
     start of the mission.
   · Research direction: examine περιστερά (dove) in LXX and Hellenistic
     religious contexts; what is the dove's role in mystery cult imagery?

2. THE GOLDEN FLEECE AND THE KINGDOM OF GOD
   · The Fleece is a divine object that belongs to the hero's heritage but
     is held in a foreign land by hostile forces.
   · The Kingdom is a divine reality that belongs to the children of God
     but is occluded by hostile political-religious forces.
   · Both are recovered/inaugurated at the end of the hero's journey.
   · The Fleece is golden (χρυσόν) — gold as divine light; compare the
     transfiguration (Matt 17:2: "his face shone like the sun").

3. THE WOMAN'S ANOINTING AND THE HERO'S INVULNERABILITY
   · Medea anoints Jason with the Promethean drug (Arg. 3.844-869): makes him
     invulnerable to fire and iron for one day.
   · Mary of Bethany anoints Jesus with spikenard (Mark 14:3-9): Jesus
     interprets this as his burial preparation — a pharmakological preparation
     for death that paradoxically enables his resurrection.
   · Both: the woman's anointing (χρίω) makes the hero capable of passing
     through death.

4. THE GARDEN AND THE DRAGON
   · The Golden Fleece is guarded by a never-sleeping dragon in a sacred grove
     (Arg. 4.127-166). Medea drugs the dragon to sleep.
   · Jesus's ordeal begins in the Garden of Gethsemane (κῆπος — garden),
     and immediately after Gethsemane comes the "serpent's" move: Judas's
     betrayal (the garden as threshold of the death-ordeal).
   · Note: the Garden of Eden in Genesis has a serpent guarding the sacred tree.
     The Fleece-grove has a dragon-serpent guarding the sacred object.
     Gethsemane is a garden that precedes death. The garden-serpent-sacred object
     pattern appears at all three sites.

5. THE BETRAYAL WITHIN THE INNER CIRCLE
   · Jason's mission is nearly undone not by external enemies but by internal
     dynamics — Medea's betrayal of her own father and brother; the Argonauts'
     disputes among themselves.
   · Jesus's mission is undone by Judas — one of the twelve, from inside.
   · Both narratives hinge on betrayal from within as the mechanism of the
     hero's greatest test.

6. THE TRIAL BEFORE A KING
   · Jason must perform impossible tasks set by King Aeetes (Arg. 3.400-421)
     before receiving the Fleece: yoke fire-breathing bulls, sow dragon's teeth,
     defeat the armed men who spring up.
   · Jesus undergoes trial before Pilate (king/governor) who sets him an
     impossible political test; the crowd demands Barabbas; Pilate's verdict
     is the mechanism of the death.
   · Both: the hero faces a king whose judgment sends him toward death.

7. MEDEA'S EXILE / MARY MAGDALENE'S MARGINALIZATION
   · After Jason abandons her and she kills her children (Euripides' version),
     Medea flies away in her dragon-drawn chariot — exiled, unreachable,
     terrifying. She escapes to Athens (or Colchis in alternate versions).
     She disappears from the narrative.
   · After the resurrection appearance in John 20:11-18, Mary Magdalene
     effectively disappears from the canonical narrative. She delivers the
     message ("I have seen the Lord") and is not heard from again in the
     canonical Gospels. In later tradition she is exiled — sent to Provence
     (the medieval legend), sent to Ephesus, sent to the margins.
   · Both women: after their central role in the death/resurrection moment,
     they are exiled from the main narrative. The tradition cannot hold them.
