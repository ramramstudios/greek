ΦΑΡΜΑΚΟΝ AND THE CHEMICAL LINEAGE: FROM MEDEA TO JESUS
A Philological-Pharmacological Research Report

Project: christ-tlg | Branch: pharmakon_research
Date: 2026-03-06
Method: TLG corpus analysis, LSJ lexicography, secondary scholarship synthesis
Hypothesis: That the pharmakological tradition embedded in Greek mystery religion
(Eleusinian, Bacchic, Orphic) — specifically the ritual use of death-inducing
substances and antidotes — forms a neglected cultural substrate for understanding
the ministry and passion of Jesus of Nazareth, who as a Galilean Jew living
under Hellenistic cultural saturation may have encountered this tradition
directly and incorporated its symbolic grammar into his self-presentation as
χριστός (the anointed/smeared one).

SECTION I: THE POLYSEMY OF ΦΑΡΜΑΚΟΝ
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The Greek word φάρμακον is irreducibly ambivalent. It designates:

  (a) a drug or medicine (healing sense)
  (b) a poison (lethal sense)
  (c) a charm or magical substance (ritual sense)
  (d) a dye or pigment (physical-material sense — directly cognate with χρίω)

LSJ s.v. φάρμακον (φάρμακον, τό):
  I. a drug, whether healing or noxious
  II. a charm, spell, enchantment
  III. a dye, paint (Theocritus, Plato)

This semantic instability — the same word for cure and poison, medicine and
magic — is not incidental. It encodes a Greek metaphysical assumption: that
all powerful substances occupy a threshold between life and death, and that the
distinction between healer and poisoner, physician and witch, depends entirely
on dosage, intent, and ritual context.

Jacques Derrida's analysis ("La pharmacie de Platon," 1968) has made this
polysemy philosophically canonical. But for our purposes, the philological
evidence within TLG is more relevant.

Key passages establishing the dual semantic field:

1. HOMER, Odyssey 4.219-230 (TLG 0012.002)
   Date: ~8th–7th c. BCE (Ionic oral tradition; written form ~700 BCE)
   Context: Helen of Troy, now in Egypt, drops a drug into the wine-bowl.

   Greek (4.220-221):
   αὐτίκ' ἄρ' εἰς οἶνον βάλε φάρμακον, ἔνθεν ἔπινον,
   νηπενθές τ' ἄχολόν τε, κακῶν ἐπίληθον ἁπάντων.

   "Straightway she cast into the wine of which they drank a drug (pharmakon),
   nepenthes and free from gall, a remedy for grief and anger, that makes men
   forget all ills."

   The nepenthes (νηπενθές) has been identified in ancient commentary as an
   Egyptian opium preparation. Helen received it from Polydamna, an Egyptian
   woman — the first documented drug transfer from Egypt to Greece in Western
   literature. Note: Egypt is the pharmacological font in Greek imagination,
   just as it is in the Exodus narrative.

2. HOMER, Odyssey 10.234-236, 302-306 (TLG 0012.002)
   Context: Circe turns Odysseus's men to swine with pharmaka.

   10.234-236:
   τοῖσι δὲ Κίρκη | δῶκε κυκεῶ τε καὶ τυρόν τε καὶ ἄλφιτα λευκά, |
   μίξασ' ἐν Πραμνείῳ οἴνῳ, ἀνέμισγε δὲ σίτῳ | φάρμακα λύγρ'.

   "Circe gave them a kykeon [mixed drink] of cheese and barley-meal, mixed
   in Pramnian wine, and stirred in baneful pharmaka."

   10.302-306: Hermes gives Odysseus the herb moly (μῶλυ) as antidote
   (ἀντίφαρμακον) against Circe's poison.

   This is structurally a DEATH-INDUCER / ANTIDOTE pair — the primal
   pharmacological dyad that will recur across centuries of Greek ritual.
   Note that the antidote is called ἀντίφαρμακον — "counter-pharmakon."

3. PLATO, Charmides 155e (TLG 0059.005)
   Date: ~380 BCE
   Context: Socrates describes a charm (ἐπῳδή) that must accompany a leaf
   given as pharmakon.

   "The leaf has a certain power, but without the charm (ἐπῳδή) it is of no
   use. It is not possible to benefit from the pharmakon without the charm."

   This confirms that Greek pharmaka regularly required ritual accompaniment —
   drug and incantation were a unified pharmacological-magical act.

4. PLATO, Phaedo 117a-118a (TLG 0059.008)
   Date: ~360 BCE
   Context: The death of Socrates by hemlock (κώνειον).

   Socrates drinks the pharmakon (the hemlock preparation). The jailer describes
   the action: numbness begins in the feet and ascends toward the heart. When
   it reaches the heart, Socrates will die. This is a clinically accurate
   description of hemlock (Conium maculatum) alkaloid poisoning — ascending
   paralysis of the peripheral nervous system.

   Crucially, Socrates approaches his death as a philosopher's initiation:
   "Practice for dying" (μελέτη θανάτου, Phaedo 81a). The structural parallel
   to mystery cult initiation — a deliberate death-like experience — is made
   explicit by Plato. The pharmakon is the initiatory instrument.


SECTION II: THE MYTHOLOGICAL FOUNDATION — MEDEA, HECATE, AND PHARMACOLOGICAL POWER
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The mythological lineage of Greek pharmacology begins with Hecate (Ἑκάτη),
goddess of the crossroads, the underworld, and pharmaka. Her priestess-initiate
is Medea (Μήδεια), whose name may derive from μέδομαι ("to devise, to know"),
cognate with the root that gives us Latin "medicus" (physician).

Medea is the ur-pharmacist of Greek mythology: she holds the power of death and
rejuvenation through chemical knowledge. Her myth is the ideological charter of
Greek pharmaceutical practice.

1. PINDAR, Pythian 4 (TLG 0033.002)
   Date: 462 BCE (composed for Arcesilas of Cyrene)
   Context: Extended narrative of the Argonautic expedition; Medea's role.

   Lines 221-223:
   πολυφάρμακοι Μήδειαν... θελξίμβροτοι ἐπᾳοιδαί
   "Medea, skilled in many drugs (polypharmakos)... with man-enchanting chants"

   Pindar establishes Medea as a polypharmakos — a master of many pharmaka.
   The word recurs in Homer's description of Circe (Od. 10.276): πολυφάρμακος.
   These are the two great female pharmacologists of Greek mythology, both
   operating at the margins of the civilized world (Colchis, Aeaea).

2. PINDAR, Pythian 3.1-60 (TLG 0033.002)
   Date: ~474 BCE
   Context: The myth of Asclepius, who raised the dead with pharmaka and was
   struck down by Zeus for transgressing the boundary of life and death.

   Lines 54-58:
   ὁ δὲ καὶ τὸν ἐκεῖθεν ἀμαχανίᾳ δεδεμένον
   ἤδη τιν' ἄνδρ' ἐκ θανάτου κόμισεν —
   χρυσὸν ἐν χερσὶν λαβόντα μισθόν—
   Ζεὺς δ' ἐκ ματέρων | τεκέων τε κοινὸν ἄρεν θάνατον.

   "He [Asclepius] brought back from death a man already bound in hopeless
   bonds — taking gold in his hands as fee — and Zeus, offended, destroyed
   him from both parents and children."

   The pharmacist-healer who raises the dead is divinely punished for
   transgressing the boundary. This myth encodes the terrifying ambiguity of
   the resurrection-drug: the one who brings back the dead violates cosmic law
   and pays with his life. The structural parallel to the Jesus narrative
   (healer who raises Lazarus and is then killed) is remarkable.

3. EURIPIDES, Medea (TLG 0006.003)
   Date: 431 BCE
   Context: Medea's pharmacological revenge on Jason's new bride and Creon.

   Lines 384-385: Medea speaks of her knowledge:
   ἐκ τῆς γὰρ Ἑκάτης Φεραίας, ἣν ἐγὼ
   πάντων μάλιστα δαιμόνων σέβω ξύνεργον.
   "From Hecate of Pherae, whom I revere above all divine powers as my
   collaborator."

   Lines 1200-1221: The poisoned robe and crown — the garments, once put on,
   adhere and burn like fire. This is consistent with contact poisons known in
   antiquity (possibly involving phosphorus compounds, or aconite-laced oil).
   The image of a garment that anoints with death is a pharmakological
   inversion of χρίω — where anointing normally consecrates or heals, here it
   kills.

   Lines 716-718: Medea's offer to Aegeus in exchange for asylum:
   τεκεῖν σέ φημι παῖδας, ὧν ἔχω φάρμακα.
   "I will make you father children; I have the pharmaka for it."
   Fertility drugs — the other pole of the life/death pharmaceutical spectrum.

4. APOLLONIUS RHODIUS, Argonautica 3.844-869 (TLG 0001.001)
   Date: ~3rd c. BCE (Hellenistic period, Alexandria)
   Context: Medea prepares the Promethean drug for Jason.

   Lines 844-857:
   νυκτὶ δ' ἐν ὀρφναίῃ φάρμακον ἔντυεν,
   ὃ καλέουσι Προμήθειον...
   τὸ δ' ἀπὸ ζωῆς ἀνδρῶν ἔτι θαλπόμενον
   ἄνθος ἐρυθρόν, ἴκελον κρόκου.

   "In the dark of night she prepared the drug which they call the Promethean...
   its flower, still warmed by the blood of living men, is blood-red,
   like saffron."

   The drug is derived from the blood dripped from Prometheus's liver as the
   eagle ate it. It is harvested at midnight (the hour of Hecate), processed
   with ritual care, and applied as a protective ointment (χρίω again) to make
   Jason's body invulnerable. The substance is applied to the BODY SURFACE —
   it is a χρίσμα, an anointing substance. Its protective power is its
   pharmakological essence.

   Lines 528-533: Hecate taught Medea pharmaka:
   ἡ δ' εἴ τι σθένος εἴχεν, ἐπ' Αἰήτεω θυγατρί,
   Ἑκάτης μεγάλης δεδαημένῃ φάρμακα κείνης.
   "If she [Medea] had any power, it came from Hecate the great, from whom
   she had learned her pharmaka."

5. SOPHOCLES, Women of Trachis (Trachiniae) (TLG 0011.007)
   Date: ~450-430 BCE
   Context: Deianira sends Heracles the robe soaked in Nessus's blood,
   believing it is a love-charm. It is a death-inducer.

   Lines 555-581: Nessus, dying from Heracles' arrow dipped in Hydra venom,
   tells Deianira his blood is a love potion (φίλτρον). It is not. When Heracles
   puts on the robe, the Hydra-poisoned blood adheres to his flesh and cannot
   be removed. He burns alive.

   The structural logic: a substance presented as a love charm / resurrection
   tool (φίλτρον, anointing potion) is actually a death-inducer. This is the
   pharmakological dyad again — but the antidote role is absent, making it a
   tragedy. The hero dies from his own anointing.


SECTION III: HERODOTUS — HISTORICAL ETHNOGRAPHY OF RITUAL SUBSTANCES
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Herodotus (~484-425 BCE) is our primary source for ethnographic documentation
of ritual psychoactive substance use in the ancient world. His Histories (TLG
0016.001) contain several passages of direct relevance.

1. Histories 4.73-75 (TLG 0016.001)
   Date of composition: ~440 BCE
   Context: Scythian funerary rites. After a royal burial, the Scythians
   purify themselves in a vapor bath.

   4.74-75 (English rendering of Herodotus's Greek):
   "The Scythians take the seed of this hemp and, creeping under the felt
   coverings, they throw it upon the red-hot stones; and, being thrown, it
   smolders and gives out such a vapor as no Greek vapor-bath could exceed;
   the Scythians, delighted, shout for joy (ὀλολύζουσι)."

   Greek text includes: κάνναβις (kannabis) — the first unambiguous textual
   reference to cannabis psychoactive use in Western literature.
   The ritual context is FUNERARY — the substance is used in rites surrounding
   death. The shout of joy (ὀλολύζειν, normally a ritual cry in religious
   contexts) suggests a structured, communal ecstatic experience.

   This is not a curiosity: Herodotus documents a death ritual that uses
   a mind-altering substance to induce altered consciousness — structurally
   identical to what the entheogen hypothesis proposes for the Eleusinian
   kykeon. The substance differs; the ritual logic is the same.

2. Histories 4.67 (TLG 0016.001)
   Context: Scythian divination ritual using hemp.

   "The Enarees [Scythian androgynous diviners] say that the art of divination
   was given them by Aphrodite... they divine by means of willow rods and
   linden bark."
   Combined with 4.73-75, this establishes the Scythian hemp cult as a
   full religious system with divinatory, purificatory, and funerary dimensions.

3. Histories 1.211-212 (TLG 0016.001)
   Context: Cyrus of Persia defeated by Tomyris, queen of the Massagetae.

   Tomyris, in an earlier episode, warned Cyrus not to use wine to defeat
   her son in battle. Cyrus did exactly that: he prepared a feast with wine
   for the Massagetae, who did not drink wine and were drunk and slaughtered.

   Herodotus 1.211:
   "Cyrus set out quantities of all the good things that the army had,
   including unwatered wine (ἄκρητον οἶνον). The Massagetae fell upon the
   Persian camp, killed those who resisted, and then, seeing the food,
   lay down and feasted, drinking the wine until they were overcome."

   Wine as a military pharmakon — a substance weaponized to incapacitate.
   This is the pharmakological logic applied to geopolitics.

4. Histories 7.114 (TLG 0016.001)
   Context: Xerxes at the Strymon River. He buries alive nine boys and nine
   girls as a gift to the river god. Herodotus notes the Persians sacrifice
   white horses by throwing them into rivers, and use other ritual destructions.

   While not pharmacological, this documents the death-sacrifice-renewal rite
   in a Persian religious context relevant to the period (early 5th c. BCE).


SECTION IV: THE ELEUSINIAN MYSTERIES — KYKEON AND THE ENTHEOGENIC HYPOTHESIS
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The Eleusinian Mysteries were the most prestigious religious institution in the
ancient Greek world, celebrated at Eleusis (near Athens) for perhaps 2000 years
(~1500 BCE–392 CE). Initiation (μύησις) promised initiates a blessed afterlife
and a transformed understanding of death and rebirth.

The initiatory drink was the κυκεών (kykeon).

1. HOMERIC HYMN TO DEMETER, Lines 206-211 (TLG 0012.003)
   Date: ~7th–6th c. BCE
   Context: Demeter, disguised, arrives at Eleusis and is offered wine by
   Metaneira. She refuses wine and asks instead for kykeon.

   Lines 206-211 (Greek):
   τὴν δ' οὔτε πίνειν γλυκερὸν οἶνον ἤθελεν
   — εἶπεν γὰρ αὐτῇ —
   ἀλλά τε κυκεῶ τεῦξαι ἐκέλευσε
   ἀλφίτῳ ἐν ὕδατι, βληχῶνί τε μαλακῷ.

   "She [Demeter] was not willing to drink the gleaming wine.
   She commanded them to mix a kykeon:
   barley-groat (αλφίτῳ) in water, with soft pennyroyal (βληχών)."

   The kykeon's three ingredients: barley (ἄλφιτον), water, and βληχών
   (blechon = pennyroyal, Mentha pulegium). The Homeric Hymn preserves the
   founding mythological scene in which the initiatory drink is distinguished
   from wine — a categorical separation.

   The entheogenic hypothesis (R. Gordon Wasson, Albert Hofmann, Carl A.P. Ruck,
   "The Road to Eleusis," 1978): The barley in the kykeon was ergotized —
   infected with Claviceps purpurea (ergot fungus), which produces ergotamine
   and related alkaloids, precursors of LSD. A controlled dose would produce
   a powerful visionary experience; this is what initiates described as seeing
   (ἐπόπτης, "the one who has seen").

   Supporting evidence:
   - Ergot grows preferably on barley and rye in moist conditions
   - The Rarian plain near Eleusis was the mythic site of first grain cultivation
   - The kykeon's mixing (κυκεῶν = "that which has been stirred") suggests
     a processed or extracted preparation, not simple barley water
   - Cicero (De Legibus 2.14.36): "We have learned from [the Mysteries] the
     beginnings of life, and gained the power not only to live happily,
     but also to die with a better hope." — The Mysteries transformed death.

2. CLEMENT OF ALEXANDRIA, Protrepticus 2.12-22 (TLG 0555.001)
   Date: ~195 CE (but transmits earlier Mystery tradition as polemic)
   Context: Clement, as a Christian apologist, reveals the Eleusinian synthema
   (password).

   "I fasted; I drank the kykeon; I took from the chest; having done my task,
   I placed in the basket, and from the basket into the chest."
   (ἐνήστευσα, ἔπιον τὸν κυκεῶνα, ἔλαβον ἐκ κίστης·
   ἐργασάμενος ἀπεθέμην εἰς κάλαθον καὶ ἐκ καλάθου εἰς κίστην.)

   The initiate drank the kykeon as a central ritual act. Whatever its chemical
   composition, this was a physically consumed substance that mediated
   the death/rebirth experience of initiation.

3. PAUSANIAS, Description of Greece 1.38.7 (TLG 0525.001)
   Date: ~2nd c. CE
   Context: Eleusis sanctuary; Pausanias notes what he may and may not reveal.

   "A dream told me not to describe the things within the wall of the sanctuary."

   Pausanias, himself an initiate, observes the prohibition on revelation —
   confirming that the Mysteries had operative secrets, including ritual
   practices involving the kykeon.


SECTION V: DIONYSIAC RITES — WINE, SPARAGMOS, AND DEATH/RESURRECTION
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Dionysus (Διόνυσος) is the god of wine, of ecstatic dissolution of self, and
of death-and-rebirth. His mythological biography includes multiple deaths and
resurrections. His cult offered initiates a version of his experience:
to die (go mad, be torn apart symbolically or literally) and return.

1. EURIPIDES, Bacchae (TLG 0006.016)
   Date: 405 BCE (posthumous)
   Context: The Bacchae dramatizes the violent consequences of refusing
   Dionysiac religion.

   Lines 135-169 (the Chorus of Bacchants):
   The initiates are described as running in the mountains, wearing fawn-skins,
   eating raw meat (ὠμοφάγια, omophagia), drinking wine, being possessed by
   the god. The wine is explicitly a pharmakon:

   Lines 772-774:
   ὁ δ' αὖ θεὸς Βρόμιος οἶνον ἀνθρώποις φέρει·
   τούτου θανόντος οὐκέτ' ἔστιν οὐδ' ἐρᾶν
   οὐδ' ἄλλο τερπνὸν οὐδὲν ἀνθρώποισι ἐν.
   "The god Bromios brings wine to men.
   If this [wine] dies [ceases], there is no longer love,
   nor any other pleasure for men."

   Lines 1125-1136: Agave tears off her son Pentheus's head believing him
   a lion — the sparagmos (σπαραγμός, "tearing apart"). The violence is
   read ritually as a parallel to the tearing of Dionysus by the Titans.

2. ORPHIC TRADITION
   The Orphic theogony narrates the birth, death, and rebirth of Dionysus
   (Zagreus): the Titans tear him apart, Zeus reassembles him (or swallows
   his heart and begets a new Dionysus). This death-resurrection is the
   cosmological template for Orphic initiation.

   Orphic Gold Tablets (5th–4th c. BCE, found at Thurii, Pelinna, Hipponion,
   Petelia):
   The tablets are buried with the dead and contain instructions for
   navigation of the underworld. Key lines (Pelinna tablet, ~325 BCE):

   "Now you have died and now you have been born, O thrice happy one, on
   this same day. Say to Persephone that the Bacchic one himself [Dionysus]
   released you."
   (νῦν ἔθανες καὶ νῦν ἐγένου, τρισόλβιε, ἄματι τῷδε.
   εἰπεῖν Φερσεφόναι σ' ὅτι Βάκχιος αὐτὸς ἔλυσε.)

   Death and rebirth on the same day; Dionysus as liberator of the dead.
   The pharmacological parallel: death-inducing substance → antidote →
   return to life on the same day.

3. PLUTARCH, De Iside et Osiride (TLG 0007.062)
   Date: ~100 CE
   Context: Plutarch interprets the Egyptian Osiris myth through Greek
   mystery religion.

   He draws explicit parallels between Osiris (torn apart by Set, reassembled
   by Isis, resurrected) and Dionysus-Zagreus. The death/resurrection pattern
   is understood as a universal mystery religion template.

   On pharmaka: Plutarch notes (ch. 16) that Isis used pharmaka in restoring
   Osiris, implying a pharmacological dimension to the resurrection rite.


SECTION VI: THEOPHRASTUS AND THE RHIZOTOMOI
─────────────────────────────────────────────

Theophrastus (~371-287 BCE), Aristotle's successor at the Lyceum, wrote the
first systematic botanical works (Historia Plantarum, De Causis Plantarum).
His account of the rhizotomoi (ῥιζοτόμοι, "root-cutters") in HP Book 9
is the most direct ancient source for the ritual pharmacological tradition.

1. THEOPHRASTUS, Historia Plantarum 9.8.1-8 (TLG 0085.001)
   Date: ~300 BCE

   HP 9.8.1: The rhizotomoi are specialized drug-collectors who accompany
   their harvesting with rituals. They hedge their practices with precautions
   and rituals that make the process partly religious.

   HP 9.8.5-6 (on mandrake, μανδραγόρας):
   "They say one should draw three circles around the mandrake with a sword,
   and cut it looking toward the west; at the second cutting, one should
   dance around the plant and speak as many erotic things as possible."
   (τρεῖς κύκλους ξίφει περιγράψαντα δεῖν ἐκτεμεῖν πρὸς ἑσπέραν ὁρῶντα·
   τῇ δὲ δευτέρᾳ τομῇ κυκλεῖν καὶ λέγειν τὰ ἐρωτικά.)

   This is pharmacology embedded in ritual: the act of harvesting requires
   circumambulation, directional orientation (west = the land of the dead),
   and verbal incantation. The drug and the ritual are inseparable.

   HP 9.8.7: On aconite (ἀκόνιτον):
   "They say aconite comes from Akonai, or according to some, it sprang from
   the mouth of Cerberus when Heracles dragged him up from Hades."
   The drug's origin myth locates it in the realm of death itself — it is
   a substance of the underworld.

2. THEOPHRASTUS, Historia Plantarum 9.9.6 (TLG 0085.001)
   On mandrake as anesthetic:
   "The root is also used to produce anesthesia: the appropriate dose is
   cut and given in wine for those who are about to have a limb cut off or
   cauterized, so that they feel no pain."
   (δίδοσθαι δὲ καὶ ἐπὶ τοῦ ἀνωδύνου τοῖς τεμνομένοις ἢ καιομένοις.)

   This is the first documented description of surgical anesthesia in
   Western medical literature. The instrument: wine of mandrake.

3. THEOPHRASTUS, Historia Plantarum 9.15.1-7 (TLG 0085.001)
   On white hellebore (ἐλλέβορος λευκός):
   "White hellebore is more effective as a drug and more dangerous. It purges
   from above [emetic]; black hellebore purges from below [cathartic]."
   Large doses of white hellebore cause convulsions and death; small doses
   were used to treat madness (the Melampus tradition).

   HP 9.17.1-2: On aconite again:
   "Aconite acts within the same day; those who use it add sesame and wine
   to slow the effect so that the person dies more slowly." This is explicit
   dose-timing pharmacology for the purpose of death management.


SECTION VII: DIOSCORIDES — THE PHARMACOPOEIA OF THE FIRST CENTURY CE
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Pedanius Dioscorides (~40-90 CE) wrote De Materia Medica (Περὶ ὕλης ἰατρικῆς)
during the reigns of Nero and Vespasian — overlapping with the generation
immediately after Jesus. His work is the most comprehensive ancient
pharmacopoeia and documents in exhaustive detail the very substances that
appear in Greek mystery religion and in the passion narrative.

TLG reference: TLG 0058.001

1. De Materia Medica 4.64 — OPIUM (μηκώνιον / ὄπιον)

   The latex of the poppy (Papaver somniferum) is described:
   "It has a soporific effect (ὑπνωτικόν). Taken in excess it is lethal
   (θανάσιμον). The juice, called opos or opion, if taken in too large a
   quantity sends men to sleep forever (εἰς ὕπνον ἀΐδιον καταφέρει)."

   Opion (ὄπιον) → opium. It is the primary ingredient in the theriac antidote
   developed by Mithridates and systematized by Galen. In small doses: sleep
   and pain relief. In large doses: death. The boundary between the two doses
   is the pharmacological threshold of the mystery rite.

2. De Materia Medica 4.68 — HENBANE (ὑοσκύαμος, hyoscyamos)
   Species: Hyoscyamus niger

   "Its leaf, seed, and juice have a narcotic and benumbing power
   (ναρκωτικὴν καὶ καρωτικὴν δύναμιν). Taken in excess, it produces
   madness (μανίαν) and in greater excess death. Some have used it as
   a poison (φαρμακεύουσι)."

   Hyoscyamus contains scopolamine, hyoscine, and atropine — anticholinergic
   alkaloids that produce vivid hallucinations, confusion, and at higher doses,
   respiratory failure. These same alkaloids appear in mandrake and belladonna
   (Atropa belladonna, named for Atropos, the Fate who cuts the thread of life).

3. De Materia Medica 4.75 — MANDRAKE (μανδραγόρας, mandragoras)
   Species: Mandragora officinarum

   "The bark of the root, crushed with wine, is given in a dram measure to
   those who cannot sleep, or who are in pain, or who are about to be cut
   or cauterized — they do not feel the pain because they have been put to
   sleep by it (καθυπνώσαντες γὰρ οὐκ αἰσθάνονται)."

   "Its leaves, smeared (ἐπαλειφόμενα) on the eyes [i.e., applied as a
   salve], induce sleep (κατακοιμίζει)."

   Note ἐπαλειφόμενα — from ἐπαλείφω, "to smear upon" — a verb in the same
   semantic field as χρίω (to anoint). Pharmacological anointing.

   "The wine of mandrake (οἶνος μανδραγορίτης): soak the root in new wine for
   three months, then give a cup-measure to those who need to be put to sleep
   before a cutting or cauterizing."

   This is surgical anesthesia via wine infusion — exactly the kind of
   preparation that could be prepared in secret and administered via a sponge.

4. De Materia Medica 4.77 — ACONITE (ἀκόνιτον, aconitum)
   Species: Aconitum napellus (monkshood/wolfsbane)

   "Also called wolf-strangler (λυκοκτόνον) or wolf-bane. It is deadly
   (θανάσιμον). Some say it springs from the spittle of Cerberus... others
   that it grew from Hecate's drool."

   Again: the mythological frame places aconite at the intersection of Hecate
   and the underworld. It is a death-substance with a ritual genealogy.

5. De Materia Medica 4.78 — HEMLOCK (κώνειον, cōneion)
   Species: Conium maculatum

   "A cold drug... its juice taken in too large a dose kills, numbing the
   limbs from below upward (κατ' ὀλίγον ψυχομένου). Used by the Athenians
   and some other Greeks as the official means of execution."

   The reference to Athenian execution confirms the Platonic context of
   Phaedo. Hemlock is the institutionally sanctioned death-pharmakon.

6. De Materia Medica 4.148-162 — HELLEBORE (ἐλλέβορος)
   Black hellebore (Helleborus niger / H. orientalis): purgative, used to
   treat madness (μανία). The legend of Melampus — he used hellebore to cure
   the mad daughters of Proetus — is the founding myth of Greek psychiatry.

   Large doses: severe convulsions and death.
   The verb "to hellebore someone" (ἐλλεβορίζειν) means to purge/cure them
   of madness — suggesting the therapeutic dose was believed to induce a
   temporary death-like state followed by sanity.


SECTION VIII: MITHRIDATES VI AND THE ANTIDOTE TRADITION
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Mithridates VI Eupator (~134-63 BCE), King of Pontus, is the pivotal figure
in the chemical lineage because he synthesizes the death/antidote dyad into
a royal mythological-pharmacological system.

Pontus (the Black Sea coast of modern Turkey) was a region of extraordinary
pharmacological knowledge, near Colchis — the mythological homeland of Medea.
The geographical continuity is not coincidental: the pharmacological tradition
of the Medea myths was geographically grounded in the Pontic region, which
maintained it through a living botanical and pharmaceutical tradition.

Sources:
- PLINY THE ELDER, Natural History 25.3-7; 29.8 (written ~77 CE)
- GALEN, De Antidotis (TLG 0057.077)
- APPIAN, Mithridatic Wars

Mithridates' practice (Pliny NH 25.3):
"Mithridates, king of Pontus, the greatest king in the memory of our time...
is said to have consumed poisons daily with antidotes (antidota), so that by
custom and habit he made them harmless."

The mithridate recipe (Galen, De Antidotis 1.1-2):
Mithridates' antidote contained ~54 ingredients including opium prominently,
along with various plant alkaloids. It was taken daily as prophylaxis against
poisoning. When Pompey defeated him and he wished to commit suicide, he could
not poison himself — he had to order a soldier to run him through.

This is the pharmakological death/resurrection as individual practice:
Mithridates enacted the mystery cult structure on his own body. He was a man
who had chemically conquered death — and then could not die when he wished to.

The theriac (θηριακή — originally an antidote against snake bites, theria)
was developed from the Mithridatic tradition and became the canonical
universal antidote of Greco-Roman medicine. Galen prepared it personally
for emperors. It contained opium as a key ingredient.

Symbolic resonance: The antidote that makes one deathproof, derived from
Pontic (Medea-adjacent) pharmacological tradition, which includes as its
central component the same substance (opium) that causes death in large doses.
The antidote and the poison are the same substance in different doses.


SECTION IX: GALEN — SYSTEMATIZING THE ANTIDOTE
─────────────────────────────────────────────────

Claudius Galenus (~129-216 CE), the most influential physician of antiquity,
systematized the pharmacological tradition in a vast corpus (TLG 0057).

Relevant works:

1. De Antidotis (TLG 0057.077) — "On Antidotes"
   A comprehensive treatise on the theriac and other antidotes. Galen traces
   the lineage explicitly: Mithridates → Andromachus (physician to Nero) →
   Galen's own preparations for Marcus Aurelius.

   The theriac as described by Galen contains:
   - Opium (ὄπιον) — the active core
   - Viper flesh (ἔχιδνα) — on the homeopathic principle that snake flesh
     counters snake venom
   - ~60 additional ingredients including castor, long pepper, cinnamon,
     saffron, various roots

   The opium in the theriac is present at a dose carefully calibrated to
   provide sedation and pain relief without causing death — the threshold
   dose again.

2. De Theriaca ad Pisonem (TLG 0057.081) — "On Theriac, to Piso"
   Galen argues that theriac, properly prepared, provides near-universal
   protection against all poisons. He explicitly uses the language of
   resurrection: the theriac prevents the body from being "killed" (ἀναιρεῖσθαι)
   by poisons, making the body in a sense deathproof.

3. De Simplicium Medicamentorum Temperamentis (TLG 0057.070)
   On individual drug properties. Mandrake: "cools and moistens beyond
   the fourth degree, and in this way causes sleep, and if given to excess,
   death." The temperature metaphysics (hot/cold/wet/dry) is superimposed
   on what are in fact alkaloid pharmacological effects.


SECTION X: THE CRUCIFIXION PHARMACOLOGY — A CLOSE READING
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The passion narratives contain a sequence of pharmacological events that has
received insufficient philological attention. Read against the background of
the Greek pharmacological tradition documented above, they reveal a structured
death/antidote pattern.

1. Mark 15:23 (TLG 0031.001):
   Greek: καὶ ἐδίδουν αὐτῷ ἐσμυρνισμένον οἶνον· ὃς δὲ οὐκ ἔλαβεν.
   "And they gave him myrrh-mixed wine; but he did not take it."

   ἐσμυρνισμένον = myrrh-infused. Myrrh (σμύρνα, Commiphora myrrha) was:
   - A standard component of anointing oils (χρίσμα) — cf. Exodus 30:23
   - A known narcotic analgesic in ancient medical use
   - One of the gifts at Jesus's birth (Matt 2:11) — a strange gift for a
     newborn unless read pharmacologically
   - Used in embalming (John 19:39-40 — Nicodemus brings 100 litrai of myrrh
     and aloe for Jesus's burial preparation)

   The refusal of myrrh-wine is notable: Jesus refuses what could be read as
   a death-inducer or analgesic preparation before crucifixion.

2. Matthew 27:34 (TLG 0031.002):
   Greek: ἔδωκαν αὐτῷ πιεῖν οἶνον μετὰ χολῆς μεμιγμένον·
   καὶ γευσάμενος οὐκ ἤθελεν πίειν.
   "They gave him wine mixed with gall (χολή) to drink;
   and having tasted it, he would not drink."

   χολή (chole) = bile, but also used for bitter substances including
   potentially herbal bitters. In LXX usage, "water of gall" (ὕδωρ χολῆς)
   is associated with death and punishment (Jer 8:14, 9:15). Matthew's
   description suggests the substance was tasted (γευσάμενος) and then
   refused — he recognized what it was.

3. John 19:29-30 (TLG 0031.004):
   Greek: σκεῦος ἔκειτο ὄξους μεστόν· σπόγγον οὖν μεστὸν τοῦ ὄξους
   ὑσσώπῳ περιθέντες προσήνεγκαν αὐτοῦ τῷ στόματι.
   ὅτε οὖν ἔλαβεν τὸ ὄξος ὁ Ἰησοῦς εἶπεν· τετέλεσται,
   καὶ κλίνας τὴν κεφαλὴν παρέδωκεν τὸ πνεῦμα.

   "A vessel full of sour wine (ὄξος) was standing there. So they placed
   a sponge full of the sour wine on a hyssop branch and brought it to his
   mouth. When Jesus had received the sour wine, he said 'It is finished,'
   and bowing his head, he gave up his spirit."

   ὄξος = sour wine, posca (Roman soldiers' drink). But note:
   - The sequence: first the death-inducing/analgesic preparations are offered
     and refused; then ὄξος is offered and accepted; death immediately follows.
   - The hyssop branch (ὕσσωπος): in the LXX, hyssop is the purification
     plant par excellence (Ps 51:7 "purge me with hyssop"; Exod 12:22 —
     hyssop used to apply the Passover blood). Its use here is liturgically
     freighted.
   - If the sponge contained a pharmacological preparation masked by vinegar
     (as mandrake wine or opium tincture could be), the sequence would be:
     [refuse death-inducer A] → [refuse death-inducer B] → [accept substance C
     → die immediately].

   This reading does not require a pharmacological conspiracy to be meaningful.
   Even at a symbolic level, Jesus participates in the death/pharmakon pattern
   at the moment of his death.

4. The burial spices (John 19:39-40):
   Nicodemus brings "a mixture of myrrh and aloe, about a hundred litrai."
   The quantity (100 litrai = ~33 kg) is enormous — far more than needed for
   simple embalming. Aloe (ἀλόη) has in antiquity referred both to Aloe vera
   (anesthetic properties) and to Aquilaria agallocha (agarwood, used in
   incense). The pharmacological density of the burial narrative deserves
   continued study.


SECTION XI: JOSEPHUS, THE ESSENES, AND JEWISH SECTARIAN PHARMACOLOGY
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Flavius Josephus (~37-100 CE) provides crucial evidence for the milieu of
Jesus's Judean contemporaries.

1. Jewish War 2.8.6 (Josephus, TLG 0526.003) — The Essenes
   "They are forbidden to taste anything of what has not been prepared
   by their curators. They do not eat or drink anything from outside the
   community. They do not allow any swearing except by God, since any other
   oath is considered perjury."

   The Essene control of food and drink — nothing consumed from outside —
   speaks to a community acutely aware of pharmacological vulnerability.
   Their strict purity rules and initiatory structure (three-year probationary
   period, gradual access to community meals) parallel mystery cult initiation.

2. Jewish War 7.8.7 — Eleazar's speech at Masada
   Josephus attributes to Eleazar ben Yair an argument about sleep as a
   paradigm of death:
   "Every night the soul, bearing no chains, departs from the body and...
   having a taste of death, returns to us." (ὥσπερ ἐκ μέθης... ἐπανέρχεται)
   The comparison to drunkenness (μέθη) and sleep as death-simulations
   echoes the pharmakological-initiatory logic precisely.

SECTION XIII: THE χριστός / χρίω CONNECTION — ANOINTING AS PHARMACOLOGICAL ACT
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The word χριστός derives from χρίω (to anoint, to smear) via the verbal
adjective: "the one who has been smeared/anointed." In the LXX it renders
Hebrew מָשִׁיחַ (mashiach, "anointed one") where anointing designates royal or
priestly consecration with olive oil mixed with aromatic substances.

But χρίω and its cognates appear throughout the pharmakological literature
in physical, non-religious senses:
- Dioscorides uses ἐπιχρίω and ἐπαλείφω interchangeably for applying drug
  preparations to skin surfaces
- Apollonius uses χρίω for the application of Medea's Promethean drug to Jason
- The anointment of the dying and the anointment of the dead are both
  χριστικαί acts that involve pharmaka

If Jesus understood himself as χριστός in the full Greek semantic field —
including the physical sense of "one who has been smeared with a substance" —
then his role intersects with the pharmakological tradition at the lexical root.

The anointed one is, in Greek, also the smeared one. And in Greek religious
practice, to be smeared with a powerful substance (Medea's Promethean ointment;
the warrior-hero's protective chrism; the initiate's ritual anointing) is to
enter the pharmakological-sacred zone where death and life are held in tension.
